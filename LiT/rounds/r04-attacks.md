# Round 04 — Option Stress-Test & Trade-off — Step 2: Attacks on Each Option's Weakest Assumption

**Round:** 04 Option Stress-Test & Trade-off
**Unit (sequential step 2 of 4):** One pass over Options 0–7. DISSENT runs the attack on every option (Playbook R04 step 2, "DISSENT runs the attack"); the relevant domain seat co-attacks each from its own discipline. No scores are set here. Each attack isolates the single load-bearing assumption on which the option's case rests, presses it at full adverse strength, and names the criterion cell (per `rounds/r04-criteria-weighting.md`) the attack threatens to cap. Step 3 scores the matrix; the attacks below are its evidentiary record and the source of the change-triggers in step 4.
**Lead:** Moderator frames; **DISSENT** attacks all; domain seat named per option. **PILOT** (phase-active R04–R07) co-attacks the execution-heavy options (5, 6).
**Lens:** ADVISOR, prospective capital provider (locked in `rounds/r00-frame-lock.md`). The attack standard is the advisor's: *what single assumption, if it fails, strands the capital*, not what is rhetorically weakest.
**Inputs:** `rounds/r03-options.md` (Options 0–7), `rounds/r04-criteria-weighting.md` (instrument, scale, gating-override rule §7), `synthesis/SWOT.md` (risk register), Round 00 research.

> Method. For each option: (1) the **load-bearing assumption** — the one premise the whole direction rests on; (2) **DISSENT's attack**; (3) the **domain seat's attack** from its discipline; (4) the **criterion most threatened** and whether the gating-override rule (§7) is in play; (5) the **defeater** — the specific evidence event that would blunt the attack, carried forward as a change-trigger. Attacks are adversarial by design and do not by themselves set scores; a successful rebuttal in step 3's per-cell rationale can stand the option back up. Independence note: each attack targets its own option and does not coordinate with the others.

## Option 0 — Pass / do not proceed
**Domain co-attacker:** CAPITAL (the seat that bears the opportunity cost of passing). DISSENT here attacks its *own* base option at full strength, per red-team integrity: the null option must survive its own cross-examination to remain the floor.

**Load-bearing assumption.** That the value destroyed by passing (a forgone option on a possibly-real recovery technology in a structurally pulled market) is smaller than the capital-and-reputation loss avoided. Equivalently: that the three gates (R-IP-1, R-TECH-1, R-ADOPT-2) are *unlikely enough to clear cheaply* that waiting dominates engaging.

**CAPITAL's attack.** Passing is not free under the advisor lens; it forfeits the cheapest entry point. The binding constraints (validation, IP, a first reference) are exactly the things a small, gated, repriced tranche could *buy down* for a fraction of the growth round, and the entry is at or below median seed (R-REV-10: $13M pre-money ≈ 80% of the ~$16M median, `r00-seed-valuation-norms.md`). If Option 1 can convert the bet into a small abandonable validation loss, then "pass" is leaving a positively-priced option on the table to avoid a downside Option 1 already caps. The demand backdrop is Cited and durable (O2, declining head grades, ~16 Bt/yr tailings stream, Market Research 50–54); passing because the *instrument* is mispriced, when re-papering the instrument is on the menu, over-weights deal friction over thesis.

**DISSENT's self-attack (steelman against itself).** The pass case is weakest where it treats the company as static. If the company will return with cleared IP and an independent number anyway, the advisor gains nothing by passing now except lost first-mover terms; the correct move is the *conditional* (Option 1), not the *unconditional* pass. Pass only dominates if (i) the gates are likely to fail outright, or (ii) the company will not restructure on the advisor's terms — both of which are about the company's conduct, which is unretrieved (C1/C3/C4).

**Criterion most threatened.** C6 (risk-adjusted return) on the *upside* limb and C3 (capital efficiency, the opportunity-cost convention §2). The attack does not invoke the gating-override (Option 0 carries no gate); it contests whether 0 should beat 1 on C6 once 1's downside is shown to be small.

**Defeater / change-trigger.** Option 0 holds as the recommendation only if a proceed option (realistically Option 1) cannot beat capital-preserved-and-redeployed on C6 *under the down-world*, per DISSENT condition §6.1. Trigger to abandon Pass in favour of Restructure-first: a credible path to a small (<~$0.5–1M) validation-and-IP tranche whose total downside is bounded and whose gates are individually retirable. Trigger to *keep* Pass: the company refuses repricing/gating, or C1/C3/C4 retrieval surfaces a disqualifying IP or validation fact.

## Option 1 — Restructure-first: conditional, gated, repriced re-entry
**Domain co-attacker:** ROYALTY (will the counterparty accept the restructured instrument and terms).

**Load-bearing assumption.** That the company will *accept* a small validation-only tranche, repriced entry, narrowed royalty base, and growth capital withheld behind three gates — and that the three gates are *retirable in series within a tolerable time and cost*. The option's whole appeal is "small abandonable bet"; that depends on the gates being cheap to test and the founder agreeing to be gated.

**ROYALTY's attack.** A founder raising at a seed mark on a 1% whole-mine NSR thesis has revealed a high reservation price for control and valuation. Restructure-first asks that founder to (a) accept a lower effective entry for stage-of-risk, (b) abandon the headline royalty basis, and (c) hand the investor abandonment optionality at each gate. Counterparties who can raise elsewhere walk from exactly this structure; if the company *cannot* raise elsewhere, that is itself an adverse signal about the underlying. Either way the term is harder to close than the matrix's "high capital-efficiency" framing implies. Acceptance is an assumption, not a control the advisor holds.

**DISSENT's attack.** The gates are assumed *independent and serial*; they may be *entangled and slow*. The validation number (R-TECH-1) cannot be cleanly read until scaled and reproduced (PILOT: bench ≠ scaled, `r00-flotation-scaleup.md`), the IP clearance (R-IP-1) depends on the unretrieved VTIP option-to-license terms (C3) and an FTO against crowded sub-500C art (`r00-ip-patent-landscape.md`, `r00-lowtemp-spodumene-priorart.md`), and a "first paid pilot" gate is itself paced by the qualification cycle (R-ADOPT-2, multi-year, `r00-operator-qualification-cycles.md`). If the three gates are sequential and each is 6–18 months, the "small abandonable bet" quietly becomes a multi-year tie-up of capital and attention at a low mark — a worse risk-adjusted profile than the staged framing concedes, and the time cost lands on C4.

**Criterion most threatened.** C4 (time-to-revenue) — the gates may be slow and entangled — and the *acceptance* premise sits behind C3 (capital efficiency) and C5 (adoption). Gating-override is *favourable* here: Option 1 internalizes the gates as release conditions, so per §7 it is scored on the post-gate state with gates logged, which is the structural reason it can outscore Option 2.

**Defeater / change-trigger.** Attack blunted by: a signed term sheet evidencing the company accepts gating and repricing (retires ROYALTY's attack); and a sequencing analysis showing the IP and validation gates can run *in parallel* and read within ~12 months for a bounded spend (retires DISSENT's entanglement attack). Change-trigger to downgrade Option 1: company refuses gating, or gate-clearing cost/time estimates exceed the abandonable-bet threshold.

## Option 2 — Licensing-first base case as written (founder anchor / strawman)
**Domain co-attacker:** CLAIM (IP) with ROYALTY (instrument). This is the strawman; the attack establishes the size of the gap the alternatives must close.

**Load-bearing assumption.** That LiT holds, today, a controlled exclusive right to license a validated process, monetizable at a 1% whole-mine NSR accepted by global majors on calendar-keyed terms. Every clause of that sentence is a separate unmet precondition.

**CLAIM's attack.** There is no cleared, controlled, exclusive right to license. The live HHS surface-chemistry IP is VTIP-owned and MRC-licensed under undisclosed terms; the only LiT-side filing on the public record is a possibly-lapsed 2024-03 provisional; the VT agreement is an *option-to-license* (Art. 13.4–13.6), not an executed license (`r00-ip-patent-landscape.md`, `r00-company-queue-disposition.md`). A license-to-others business with no exclusive right to license is unsellable. This is the R-IP-1 gate, *unmitigated* in Option 2 because the option leaves it unaddressed.

**ROYALTY's attack.** The 1% whole-mine NSR is off-market and value-destroying as an instrument: process-tech licensing does not price on whole-mine NSR (`r00-licensing-norms.md`), majors resist the basis (R-ADOPT-4), and it caps capture on REE's small tonnage while overstating it on the rest (R-REV-1, R-REV-4). Calendar-keyed tranches assume revenue arrives on the deck's dates, not the operator's qualification cycle (R-ADOPT-2). The instrument fails on basis, on acceptance, and on timing simultaneously.

**Criterion most threatened.** C2 (IP defensibility) and C5 (adoption) — both subject to the §7 gating-override at the *unmitigated* state (R-IP-1 High and R-ADOPT-1/4). Per §7, Option 2 scores 1 on C2 and is *ineligible for recommendation* regardless of weighted total. C4 also caps low (calendar vs cycle).

**Defeater / change-trigger.** None available *within* Option 2's definition — by construction it does not retire its gates. The only "defeater" is to convert it into Option 1 or 4 (i.e., stop being Option 2). Its role is fixed: the baseline whose gap to the alternatives quantifies the value of restructuring.

## Option 3 — Anchor co-development / strategic-investor first
**Domain co-attacker:** MILL (the operator/buyer voice), with CHOKEPOINT on concentration.

**Load-bearing assumption.** That a single strategic anchor can be signed *early* (before independent validation and before a cleared exclusive), and that the reference it produces *transfers* to the broader market as a repeatable license offer.

**MILL's attack.** Two failures sit on this premise. First, an operator does not co-fund and qualify an unvalidated, IP-unclear process cheaply; the operator runs its *own* multi-year qualification on its *own* feedstock against its *own* criteria (R-ADOPT-2, `r00-operator-qualification-cycles.md`), and prices its participation against an all-in adoption cost the deck omits (R-ADOPT-3). The anchor *is* the qualification cycle, not a way around it. Second, a reference does not transfer: a second operator does not inherit the first's proof and re-qualifies from near-zero (R-ADOPT-5), so "license the reference to the market" overstates the spillover. The anchor de-risks the *first* deal, not the franchise.

**DISSENT's attack.** The anchor is the same fact as the concentration risk (R-ADOPT-1). A single strategic that funds validation, supplies the site, and takes a field-of-use/equity position acquires the leverage to extract the value, build it in-house, or fund a competitor — precisely the behaviours the majors already exhibit (Freeport runs internal leach R&D *and* backs Jetti; Rio funds ElectraLith; Glencore Technology is an in-channel licensor competitor; `r00-major-miner-techprograms.md`). The anchor relationship that collapses adoption risk *concentrates* counterparty and self-substitution risk into one party who has seen the flowsheet. CHOKEPOINT adds: a single-anchor dependency is a supply-chain single point of failure that an allied-positioning thesis (Option 7) would itself flag.

**Criterion most threatened.** C5 (adoption probability) — improved on *first deal*, but exposed on transfer and on counterparty leverage (R-ADOPT-1). C4 (time-to-revenue) is the anchor's strength (it is the fastest credible path to a first reference) and is where Option 3 earns its score; the attack contests whether that first reference is worth the concentration it buys.

**Defeater / change-trigger.** Attack blunted by: an anchor term that ring-fences field-of-use and bars in-house replication/competitor funding for a defined period (retires DISSENT's self-substitution attack); and evidence of at least a *second* operator's evaluation interest contingent on the reference (tests MILL's transfer attack). Change-trigger to downgrade: anchor demands exclusivity or equity that forecloses the broader license market, converting Option 3 into a single-customer dependency.

## Option 4 — Repriced narrow-base licensing (model kept, instrument fixed)
**Domain co-attacker:** ROYALTY (instrument design), with SPOT (does the narrow base capture enough value).

**Load-bearing assumption.** That a verifiable narrow base (metered licensed-process throughput or incremental recovered metal) (a) is *measurable and auditable* in the operator's circuit without dispute, and (b) captures *enough* value at recalibrated terms to justify the same build as the richer instruments — while *also* clearing the same IP and validation gates as everyone else.

**ROYALTY's attack.** "Incremental recovered metal" is the hardest quantity to meter in a brownfield circuit: it requires an agreed counterfactual baseline (what the operator would have recovered without the process), continuous reconciliation, and a metering point the operator controls. The verifiability that is Option 4's selling point (S3) is exactly where post-deal disputes live; operators will contest the baseline that sets the royalty. The instrument fixes the *basis* objection (R-REV-1, R-ADOPT-4) but inherits a *measurement* objection the whole-mine NSR avoided by being crude. The fix is real but not free.

**SPOT's attack.** A narrow base captures less per deployment than a hybrid/capacity model (Option 5) for the *same* recovery narrative and the *same* build, validation, and IP cost. If the narrow royalty is set low enough for majors to accept (R-ADOPT-4) it may not clear the company's cost of capital across a qualification-cycle-paced ramp; if set high enough to matter it re-triggers the acceptance resistance it was meant to solve. Option 4 risks being the instrument that is *acceptable but thin* — value-capture parity with Option 5 is the open question, and it is contested.

**Criterion most threatened.** C6 (risk-adjusted return) via value-capture sufficiency, and a *secondary* C5 friction (metering disputes slow conversion). C4/C5 still carry the shared qualification-cycle risk (R-ADOPT-2) that Option 4 does not escape. Gating-override is favourable (gates internalized, §7).

**Defeater / change-trigger.** Attack blunted by: a metering/baseline protocol with precedent in process-tech licensing (`r00-licensing-norms.md`) that survives procurement and audit (retires ROYALTY's measurement attack); and a value-capture model showing the narrow base clears cost of capital under the base price world (retires SPOT's thinness attack). Change-trigger to prefer Option 5 over 4: modelling shows the narrow base captures materially less than a capacity model for equal build and adoption cost.

## Option 5 — Hybrid technology-as-a-service / capacity model (higher value capture)
**Domain co-attacker:** PILOT (execution and scale-up), with WATERSHED on partial liability. Per the §5 PILOT scoring instruction, the C1/C3 cells here must price the team-build and capex ramp explicitly.

**Load-bearing assumption.** That LiT can *build and operate* per-tonne processing capacity (equipment, field-service organization, integration headcount) at acceptable capital intensity *and* capture shared-savings on incremental recovered metal — while remaining short of title to the waste so liability stays with the miner.

**PILOT's attack.** This is where execution stops being free. A capacity/TaaS model converts a two-principals-plus-Dhanbad research outfit into an equipment-deploying, field-service-running operating company. That is a different company: capex for processing units, a service org to install and run them in someone else's circuit, integration and uptime accountability, and a headcount ramp (the two-to-seven-person build understates it materially for a BOO-adjacent service model). The scale-up itself is unproven (bench ≠ piloted ≠ per-site qualified; `r00-flotation-scaleup.md`, `r00-operator-qualification-cycles.md`), so LiT would be standing up a service organization around a process whose *scaled* number is not yet established. C1 must be scored on *scaled, reproduced* recovery and C3 must absorb the capex/field-service ramp; scored honestly, Option 5's higher capture is bought with materially lower capital efficiency and higher execution risk than the licensing options.

**WATERSHED's attack.** "Stops short of title" is thinner protection than it reads. Operating capacity in the miner's circuit, handling and routing tailings streams, and taking shared-savings on recovered metal moves LiT toward operator-like custody and arranger-adjacent exposure; partial R-ESG-2 is not zero, and the CERCLA/closure boundary (`r00-tailings-liability.md`) must be drawn explicitly in the contract, not assumed by model label. DISSENT-OPT-3 binds here: the extra capture is *bought with* extra liability and price exposure, and step 3 must price that into the C6 down-world, not note and drop it.

**DISSENT's attack.** Option 5 takes on the *capital intensity and partial liability* of producing without the *adoption-independence* of fully producing (Option 6 removes the buyer; Option 5 still needs the operator to host capacity in its circuit). It risks the worst of both axes: more build and liability than licensing, less control than producing. The DLE TaaS/BOO precedent (36% share, `r00-businessmodel-economics.md`) is Cited but is a *different feedstock and maturity*; transferring its share economics to brownfield mineral tailings is an Assumption, not a Citation.

**Criterion most threatened.** C3 (capital efficiency) — heaviest hit, per PILOT — and C6 (risk-adjusted return) once capex, execution, and partial liability enter the down-world. C5/C4 still carry the host-operator qualification risk. Value capture (the upside limb of C6) is the offsetting strength.

**Defeater / change-trigger.** Attack blunted by: a capex-light deployment design (e.g., operator-owned equipment, LiT licenses + commissions rather than owns) that keeps C3 near the licensing options while retaining capacity-based capture; an explicit contractual liability boundary opined to keep CERCLA/closure with the miner (retires WATERSHED's attack); and a strategic equipment round that funds the build without diluting to failure. Change-trigger to prefer 4 over 5: capex/execution modelling shows capital efficiency falls far enough that risk-adjusted return drops below the narrow-base licensing option.

## Option 6 — Build-own-operate / producer (own the metal)
**Domain co-attacker:** WATERSHED (environmental liability) and PILOT (build), with SPOT on price exposure.

**Load-bearing assumption.** That capturing full margin justifies taking *title/custody* of tailings (hence strict joint-and-several retroactive CERCLA liability and closure obligations) and *full commodity-price exposure*, financed by a large strategic/project round, executed by a team that has never built a plant.

**WATERSHED's attack.** This is the option where remediation-as-asset inverts to remediation-as-liability. The moment LiT holds the waste, strict, joint-and-several, retroactive CERCLA current-owner/operator liability attaches when cleanup costs are incurred (`r00-tailings-liability.md`), and the Good Samaritan shield (PL 118-155, Dec 2024) is too narrow to cover a for-profit producer (federal land only, no profit beyond cost recovery, NPL-excluded). R-ESG-2 inverts from contained (under licensing) to *High*. Plus closure bonds. The full-margin thesis is gross of an environmental liability tail that the licensing options keep entirely off LiT's balance sheet.

**SPOT's attack.** Option 6 swaps a recovery-economics story that pays *regardless of cycle* (SPOT-OPT-1, the durable thesis) for a producer that is fully long the commodity price. The lithium deck spans ~2x and REE swings ~2.6x intra-year (`r00-li-ree-price-forecasts.md`); a producer's returns are dominated by the price world, R-REV-3 moves from a royalty-base modifier to a *direct* P&L driver, and the down-world is a leveraged loss. The one structural advantage of the technology — that it earns on *recovery economics* independent of price level — is discarded by becoming a price-taker.

**PILOT's attack.** A first-of-a-kind processing plant built by a team with no plant-building track record, on a process whose scaled number is unproven, is the highest-execution-risk path on the board. Capital efficiency is the lowest of any option (largest committed round, least abandonable), and the build cannot be staged into small bets — once committed, it is committed. This is the opposite of the executable, gated posture the instrument rewards.

**Criterion most threatened.** C3 (capital efficiency — lowest, least abandonable) and C6 (risk-adjusted return — the CERCLA tail and price exposure dominate the down-world; DISSENT-OPT-3's liability limb is at maximum here). Offset: C5 (adoption) is highest because Option 6 *removes the buyer* (R-ADOPT-1 dissolved). The trade is adoption-independence bought with liability and capital intensity.

**Defeater / change-trigger.** Attack blunted by: government-backed capital and offtake that floors the price exposure (the Option 7 overlay) *and* a liability ring-fence (project SPV, bonded closure, indemnity from the impoundment owner) that caps the CERCLA tail. Even blunted, Option 6 remains the high-capture/high-liability extreme. Change-trigger that would *raise* Option 6: a secured impoundment with clean title and a strategic/government partner absorbing both the capital and the liability — i.e., Option 6 only survives as Option 6-plus-7, never standalone.

## Option 7 — Government-anchored allied-supply-chain play (policy-first capital and demand)
**Domain co-attacker:** CHOKEPOINT (geopolitics/policy, phase-active R04), with SPOT on whether the economics stand without the subsidy.

**Load-bearing assumption.** That non-dilutive policy capital, government offtake, and a price floor will (a) *arrive in time and at scale* to fund validation and subsidize the operator's qualification cost, and (b) *persist* long enough to underwrite the downside — i.e., that the thesis can lean on policy without policy becoming the thesis.

**CHOKEPOINT's attack.** Policy capital is Cited but explicitly a *bounded accelerant, not a structural base* (R02 Cluster 5 ruling, R-GEO-1). Grant timelines, appropriations risk, and administration turnover make the price-floor and offtake a time-bounded instrument; a business whose downside protection lapses with the policy cycle has not retired its downside, it has rented cover. Worse, the allied-positioning asset is undercut by its own supply chain: 75% of R&D concentrated at IIT(ISM) Dhanbad is an ITAR/EAR and IP-custody exposure (R-GEO-2, T12) that a government counterparty's diligence will find, so the policy capital that is the option's premise is also gated on closing the offshore exposure — a cost and delay the option's appeal understates.

**SPOT's attack.** Policy durability inverts on C7 by design (high score = *less* policy dependence), so Option 7 *as a standalone base* must lose on C7: it is the most policy-dependent direction on the board. The defensible use of policy is as an *overlay* that floors the downside of an economically-standing option (1, 3, 4, 5), not as the base case. If the recovery economics do not clear without the subsidy (O2 must carry the thesis, SPOT-OPT-1), then Option 7 standalone is a bet on appropriations, not on minerals.

**DISSENT's attack.** Option 7 is the option most able to *launder a weak base case* behind a policy narrative: a price floor can make a thin royalty or an unvalidated process look financeable, masking the gates rather than retiring them. DISSENT requires that Option 7 be scored as an *overlay* on a named base option, with the base's gates (R-IP-1, R-TECH-1, R-ADOPT-2) still scored on their own merits; the subsidy must not buy points on C1/C2/C5.

**Criterion most threatened.** C7 (policy durability) — Option 7 standalone scores *low* by construction (most policy-dependent). Its strengths are C3 (non-dilutive capital) and the *down-world floor* limb of C6 (price-floor/offtake underwrites R-REV-3 downside) — but only as an overlay. Standalone, the gating risks are untouched.

**Defeater / change-trigger.** Attack blunted *only* by treating Option 7 as an overlay: scored on top of Option 1/3/4/5 with the base's gates intact and the policy instrument priced as a downside floor, not an upside subsidy. Onshoring the Dhanbad capability (CHOKEPOINT-OPT-2) retires the R-GEO-2 limb of CHOKEPOINT's attack. Change-trigger that would *raise* the overlay's value: a concrete, awarded (not prospective) grant/offtake/price-floor instrument with a term longer than the qualification cycle. Change-trigger that would *cut* it: reliance on the floor to make the base case clear cost of capital — that is the laundering DISSENT bars.

## Cross-option synthesis of the attack pass (carried to steps 3 and 4)

1. **Three attacks recur across every proceed option and are not option-specific — they are the gates.** R-IP-1 (no cleared exclusive), R-TECH-1 (no scaled/reproduced number), R-ADOPT-2 (qualification-cycle timing). Every option except 0 must *internalize* these as release conditions; Option 2 alone leaves them open and is therefore §7-ineligible. The matrix must not let any option's value-capture story (3, 4, 5, 6, 7) score around a gate it has not actually retired (Probe's §4 consistency check).

2. **The attacks sort the options along a single revealed trade-off axis: value-capture vs. capital-efficiency/liability** (DISSENT-OPT-3, now evidenced). Licensing (4) is low-capture/low-liability/high-abandonability; hybrid (5) is mid-capture bought with capex and partial liability; producer (6) is max-capture bought with max liability and zero abandonability. Step 3 must score C3 and C6 so this trade-off is visible, not averaged away.

3. **The anchor (3) and policy (7) directions are *configurations*, not free wins.** 3 buys time-to-first-reference with counterparty concentration; 7 buys a downside floor with policy dependence. Both survive the attack *as overlays on a gated base*, and neither survives it *as a standalone base*. Step 3 scores them as primary directions and step 3's sensitivities test them as overlays on Options 1/3/4/5.

4. **The pass/restructure contest (0 vs 1) turns on one unretrieved fact: whether the company will accept gating and repricing** (behind C1/C3/C4 retrieval, the VTIP option terms C3). DISSENT's §6.1 condition binds: 1 beats 0 only if its down-world C6 beats capital-preserved-and-redeployed. This is the decision the matrix exists to make; the attack pass leaves it correctly unresolved and hands it to step 3 with the down-world specified.

5. **Execution is not free for 5 and 6** (PILOT). The C1 and C3 cells for the capital-intensive options must price the team-build and capex ramp per the §5 instruction; absent that, the higher-capture options would be scored as if a two-person research outfit could become a plant operator at no risk.
