## Headline finding for the milestone realism question

Published analogs do not support a clean ~10-to-16-month bench-to-pilot path that ends in a commercially demonstrated process. For both the general fine-flotation literature and the two closest named technologies (Eriez HydroFloat coarse-particle flotation and the Virginia Tech / MRC HHS process), the lab → proof-of-concept → pilot → first-commercial arc is measured in **years, with material recovery degradation appearing specifically at the bench-to-larger-scale transition**, and it is driven by physics (froth transport, bubble carrying capacity, mixing) that worsen for fine and ultrafine particles. Month 10 and Month 16 are best read as **gate-entry dates for a pilot campaign, not dates by which a scaled, validated recovery number is in hand.** This corroborates and sharpens the W9 finding on operator qualification cycles. (Assumption, High confidence — multiple independent analogs converge.)

## 1. Fine and ultrafine particles lose recovery at scale for structural reasons (Cited)

The fine-flotation literature is explicit that recovery does not translate cleanly from bench to industrial scale, and that the gap is worst for the finest fractions:

- Fine and ultrafine particles are hard to float mainly because of **low bubble-particle collision probability and collision efficiency**, plus **entrainment of ultrafine hydrophilic gangue** that lowers concentrate grade. The same surface area that makes them hard to dewater makes bubble **carrying capacity the binding constraint**. (Cited — ScienceDirect S0892687521004490, "Hydrodynamics of froth flotation and its effects on fine and ultrafine mineral particle flotation: A literature review"; corroborated by ResearchGate 314095255 on gangue particle size and entrainment.)
- In scale-up, the dominant lost-performance factor is **froth transport and froth removal**, which degrades as cell size and capacity rise. One quantification attributes **48–58% of the scale-up factor to froth transport**, with the **mixing effect more sensitive for finer particles at ~16.2%**. (Cited — search abstract of ScienceDirect S0892687523005551, "Challenges in flotation scale-up: The impact of flotation kinetics and froth transport"; full PDF returned HTTP 403, so the percentages are quoted from the indexed abstract, Moderate confidence on exact figures.)
- **Direct lab-versus-industrial statement:** fine particles (−45 μm) show **slower flotation kinetics and poorer recovery on the industrial scale than at lab scale.** New launder and cell-geometry designs (radial / concentric, shorter froth-transport distance) are the engineering response. (Cited — same source family; SDSU CSRCR2010-01 "Scale-up of Flotation Processes" and the 911 Metallurgist up-scaling thesis corroborate that froth-recovery loss is the central scale-up risk.)

**Implication for LiT.** If LiT's recovery uplift depends on capturing a fine/ultrafine fraction that conventional flotation leaves behind, the headline bench recovery figure (the 16–19% claim, gated under C1) is exactly the kind of number most exposed to degradation on scale-up. The advisor should expect the piloted number to come in below the bench number, not at it, and should treat any pilot plan that assumes parity as optimistic. (Assumption, High confidence.)

## 2. The HydroFloat analog: ~decades concept-to-base-metals, multi-year per new application (Cited)

Eriez HydroFloat is the best-documented modern coarse/fine recovery technology sold into miners on a comparable "recover what the existing circuit loses" value proposition.

- Eriez describes pioneering coarse particle flotation **"nearly 30 years ago,"** evolving concept → lab → pilot → industrial through a deliberate multi-stage program, backed by **more than 50,000 lab and pilot CPF tests** and thousands of hours of industrial data. (Cited — Eriez "History of Coarse Particle Flotation"; me.smenet.org; Mining Engineering Online.)
- It was **commercial for over a decade in coarse phosphate and potash before expanding into base metals** — i.e., proving a process in one feedstock did not transfer for free to the next. (Cited — same.)
- Concrete deployment timeline in base metals: Eriez and Rio Tinto ran lab and pilot campaigns on Kennecott Copperton tailings **in 2013**; Anglo American's El Soldado demonstration plant with the **5 m, then-largest HydroFloat cell was commissioned across Q1–Q3 2021.** Even for the category leader, the **base-metals pilot-to-first-large-demonstration arc spanned several years**, and El Soldado was still styled a demonstration, not a settled commercial reference. (Cited — im-mining.com 2020-06-04 Anglo/Eriez El Soldado; Eriez Copper-2022 El Soldado commissioning white paper.)

**Implication for LiT.** The category leader, with decades of data, treats each new feedstock as a fresh multi-year qualification. A first-mover at bench/POC stage compressing this to ~10–16 months for a validated, transferable result is running well ahead of the best published analog. (Assumption, High confidence.)

## 3. The HHS analog: closest match to LiT Stage One, multi-year DOE-funded pilot, still pre-commercial (Cited)

The Hydrophobic-Hydrophilic Separation (HHS) process is the **direct technical ancestor of LiT's Stage One surface chemistry** (Yoon / Luttrell / Virginia Tech / MRC, per W1). It is the single most relevant scale-up precedent in the evidence base.

- HHS uses **non-polar short-chain alkane solvents to displace water from particle surfaces and agglomerate ultrafine particles (<325 mesh / <45 μm)**, then breaks the agglomerates mechanically downstream — a surface-chemistry separation, not conventional froth flotation. (Cited — NETL coal beneficiation listing; ResearchGate 296691488; vtechworks Li 2019 thesis.)
- Development path described as **"following extensive laboratory-scale test work, tested successfully in continuous operations at proof-of-concept (POC)- and pilot-scale"** — an explicit lab → POC → pilot ladder, not a single jump. (Cited — NETL listing.)
- The DOE-funded **pilot-scale** testing project (MRC + Virginia Tech) was **$1,445,486 with $361,371 cost share and an end date of 31 December 2022.** A single pilot campaign was a roughly seven-figure, multi-quarter, externally co-funded effort, and as of that program HHS was still being demonstrated for technical/economic/environmental benefit rather than sold as commercial. (Cited — NETL coal beneficiation project records; NETL 21ACP_Reyher and 22RS-27_Yoon program slides, titles only — the 22RS-27 PDF was retrieved but is binary/unreadable, so figures inside it are NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.)

**Implication for LiT.** The very lineage LiT draws on took **multiple years and grant funding to move from lab through POC to a pilot that was still pre-commercial.** This is strong, specific evidence against a self-funded ~10–16-month path to a commercially validated Stage One result, and it reinforces the W1 conclusion that the defensible asset and the proven process both sit upstream of LiT in VTIP/MRC hands. (Assumption, High confidence.)

## 4. Named scale-up failure modes to carry into Round 05 risk and Round 06 pre-mortem

From the above, the recurrent, physics-driven failure modes for a fine-particle surface-chemistry process at scale (Cited where literature-backed, else Assumption):

1. **Froth-transport / froth-recovery collapse** as cell size grows — the single largest scale-up loss factor. (Cited.)
2. **Bubble carrying-capacity ceiling** on ultrafines — high specific surface area outruns available bubble surface. (Cited.)
3. **Slower kinetics at industrial scale** for the −45 μm fraction, so residence-time assumptions from bench under-deliver. (Cited.)
4. **Gangue entrainment** degrading grade even where recovery holds. (Cited.)
5. **Feedstock non-transferability** — a result proven on one tailings/ore does not carry to the next without re-piloting (HydroFloat phosphate→base-metals; HHS coal-specific). (Cited via analog.)
6. **For the HHS-style route specifically:** solvent recovery/loss, agglomerate-breakage efficiency, and solvent-handling/HSE at scale are additional cost and reliability risks not present at bench. (Assumption, Moderate — inferred from the described HHS mechanism, not from a scale-up failure report.)

---

## 5. Bottom line for Round 05 milestone realism (the CRUCIBLE 1 question)

- A Month 10 / Month 16 pilot is **plausible as a campaign start**, but the published analogs (HydroFloat, HHS) put **first validated, transferable, commercially credible scaled recovery numbers years out, not ~16 months out**, and predict that the **piloted recovery will sit below the bench figure** for a fine-particle process. (Assumption, High confidence.)
- Round 05 should phase the pilot with an explicit **gate on achieving a defined fraction of bench recovery at pilot scale on the actual target feedstock**, not on hitting the bench number, and should **budget for per-feedstock re-piloting** rather than one general pilot. (Recommendation to Round 05.)
- Round 04 time-to-revenue and Round 06 pre-mortem should both carry **"recovery degrades on scale-up below the financeable threshold"** as a named, physics-grounded failure mode, not a generic execution risk. (Recommendation.)

This item is **P2 supporting** and does not gate Round 01 entry. It strengthens, but does not depend on, the C1 (recovery dataset) and W1 (IP) gate items.
