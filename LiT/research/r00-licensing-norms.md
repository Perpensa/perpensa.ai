## Two different things are both being called "NSR," and conflating them is the core risk

There are two distinct royalty constructs in the evidence, and LiT's model sits awkwardly between them:

1. **Mining NSR royalty (a financing instrument).** A royalty holder advances capital and receives a percentage of net smelter return for the life of mine. Standard range is **2%-5% of NSR**. This is paid on the whole mine's saleable metal because the royalty was bought against the whole asset. Cited (Wikipedia "Net smelter return"; INN; Uranium Royalty Corp "Royalty and Streaming 101").

2. **Process-technology licensing royalty (payment for using IP).** The licensee pays for the incremental value the technology creates, normally on the **product of the licensed process**, frequently structured as upfront + milestone + running royalty. The IP-valuation rule of thumb (the "25% rule") sets the running royalty near **25% of the licensee's operating profit attributable to the technology**; e.g. a 20% operating margin implies ~5% of net sales, and published rates span 0.1% to >25% of net sales, with commodity-linked sectors at the low end. The 25% rule was rejected as a damages tool in *Uniloc v. Microsoft* (2011) but remains a standard negotiation starting point. Cited (RoyaltyRange; UpCounsel; Goldscheider/PTC; Razgaitis ipHandbook ch.9).

**The live analog confirms construct 2, not construct 1.** Jetti Resources, the closest comparable (a catalytic-leach process licensed into operating copper mines), "charges its customers a per-pound royalty that is linked to the copper price," i.e. a royalty on the copper recovered through Jetti's technology, not on the mine's total output. Its commercial entry combined an equity investment plus an evaluation agreement (Teck, Jan 2022) ahead of site deployment (El Abra/Freeport, Feb 2023). Cited (im-mining.com; jettiresources.com; North American Mining).

## Findings for SPOT and ROYALTY

1. **1% of whole-mine NSR is off-market in basis, even though 1% looks low in rate.** The 1% rate is below the 2-5% mining-NSR band, which is what makes "one cent on the dollar" sound modest. But mining-NSR royalties at 2-5% are bought with upfront capital against the whole asset; a technology licensor has not bought the asset. The correct comparator is construct 2, where the royalty attaches to the product of the licensed process. Charging 1% of **total** mine production means LiT collects on tonnes its technology never touched. That basis, not the headline rate, is the realism problem. Assumption (High) — direct from the two constructs above plus the Jetti precedent.

2. **The "one cent for 16-19 cents" framing mixes the bases.** The 16-19% recovery uplift is an increment on the treated stream; the 1% is levied on whole-mine production value. An operator will price the deal on what LiT actually adds (incremental recovered metal x price x royalty), not on total production. Re-based to the product of the licensed process, a defensible royalty is more likely a higher percentage of a much smaller number, which materially changes the revenue model and should be stress-tested in Round 04. Assumption (High). The derivation of LiT's $229M/$2.29B production bases is unretrievable and sits in [Company] C2.

3. **Upfront fees are plausible in form, unanchored in amount.** Upfront + milestone + running royalty is the standard process-licensing shape, so a signing fee is normal. Whether $2M (small) and $10M (major) are market is not establishable from public data, since UOP/Hatch/Metso terms are confidential. Treat the absolute upfront figures as Assumption (Low-Moderate); the structure as Cited-normal. `NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL: comparable executed upfront license fees for minerals process technology.`

4. **Time-to-revenue is long and gated by evaluation/equity, not a clean LOI-to-license hop.** The one observable analog took roughly a year-plus from a signed evaluation-and-investment agreement to a first site deployment, and that was after the technology already had momentum. LiT's plan books a first license at Month 25 with LOI retainers in Months 23-24. The Jetti precedent suggests the evaluation-to-deployment leg alone can consume a year and often rides on the licensor taking on pilot risk or the operator taking equity, neither of which is in LiT's plan. The Month-25 first-revenue assumption is therefore optimistic and should carry a slip band in Round 04/05. Assumption (Moderate) — single-analog inference; reinforced by the general "mining pilots are the graveyard of good technology" adoption literature (see W9). Cited (African Mining; WMFTS pilot-plant note).

5. **Operator qualification is a structural gate, not a marketing step.** Adoption literature is consistent that operators require demonstrated reliability at industrial scale and pilot testing on their own feedstock before commercial commitment, lengthening the sales cycle and front-loading cost onto the licensor. This compounds finding 4 and belongs in the Round 05 sequencing and the customer-concentration risk. Assumption (High). Cited (African Mining; ScienceDirect minerals-industry reviews).
