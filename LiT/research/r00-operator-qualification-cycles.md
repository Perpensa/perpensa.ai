## Bottom line for the deliberation

The public record on new processing technology at major miners shows a long, gated, feedstock-specific qualification path. Even successful, now-celebrated technologies took roughly a decade from invention to first commercial reference, and roughly 2 to 3 years from a *demonstrated, on-site pilot* to a full commercial circuit at a single operation. Each new operator re-qualifies the technology on its own ore, in its own circuit, before committing capital. This is the central reality MILL flagged: the sales cycle is structurally long, the buyer is risk-averse, and adoption is won site-by-site, not platform-wide.

This directly pressures LiT's Month 10 / Month 16 pilot-to-licensing timeline and any model that assumes multiple operator licenses converting in parallel within the first 18 to 24 months. **Cited** evidence below; the read-through to LiT's specific milestones is tagged **Assumption**.

---

## Case 1 — Eriez HydroFloat coarse particle flotation (CPF)

The most complete public timeline of a new mineral-processing technology moving from lab to multi-operator commercial adoption.

- The technology was developed through university collaboration on coarse-particle flotation fundamentals, then progressed through numerous laboratory-scale separators and **multiple pilot-scale test programs**, then a full-scale demonstration at Rio Tinto's Kennecott Utah operation, before commercial operators installed full production circuits. **Cited** (me.smenet.org; e-mj.com). This is a multi-stage gate sequence, not a single proof point.
- **Newcrest / Cadia (first commercial sulphide adopter):** a CPF demonstration plant (four CrossFlow Separators and two HydroFloats) was delivered, commissioned and ramped **in 2018**; Newcrest then became the first to commercialise HydroFloat in a sulphide tail-scavenging application, with additional HydroFloat units supplied for Stage 2 of the Cadia expansion. Roughly **2 to 3 years from on-site pilot demonstration (2018) to full commercial circuit**. **Cited** (im-mining.com 2021-01-29; e-mj.com).
- **OZ Minerals → BHP / Carrapateena:** Eriez was awarded the HydroFloat CPF supply in **May 2023**; BHP (which acquired OZ Minerals) announced successful commissioning of the plant in **August / September 2025**. Roughly **2.3 years from supply award to commissioned plant** at a single, already-convinced site, delivered "on an accelerated schedule." **Cited** (im-mining.com 2023-05-01; im-mining.com 2025-09-17; australianmining.com.au).
- **Capstone / Pinto Valley** was still only "considering" HydroFloat in October 2020, illustrating that even a marketed, commercially-referenced technology sits in an evaluation queue at each new account. **Cited** (im-mining.com 2020-10-28).

**Read-through:** HydroFloat had Kennecott, Cadia, El Soldado and others as paying references and *still* required a fresh multi-year evaluate-then-commission cycle at each new operator. A pre-pilot, single-claim, no-commercial-reference technology should expect a longer first cycle, not a shorter one. **Assumption (High confidence).**

## Case 2 — Jetti Resources catalytic copper leaching at Capstone / Pinto Valley

- Amenability **testing with Capstone began in late 2017**; after positive results, **construction of the commercial facility began mid-2019**, and the catalyst was **deployed in 2019**, with cathode production per irrigated area roughly doubling within about a year. **Cited** (miningweekly.com 2020-07-28; jettiresources.com; mining-technology.com).
- So **~2 years from first operator bench/amenability testing to a producing commercial deployment** at the launch site, on a leach-pad application that is unusually fast to install relative to a flotation or hydrometallurgical retrofit. Jetti the company traces to ~2011 university IP, so the invention-to-first-commercial arc is closer to **~8 years**. **Cited / Assumption (Moderate)** on the founding date.

## Cross-case industry framing

- Mining is repeatedly characterised as conservative and slow to adopt new processing technology at scale, with high capital intensity and legacy circuits as structural barriers, and **customer qualification treated as a core gate** because processing is complex, environmentally intensive, and failure is costly. **Cited** (sciencedirect.com S0301420721002026 "Barriers and enablers of technology adoption in the mining industry"; link.springer.com 10.1007/s42461-020-00262-1; miningweekly.com 2025-10-22).
- Industry commentary in 2025 frames *faster* adoption as an unmet need driven by critical-mineral supply gaps, i.e. the default state is still slow; demand pressure is a tailwind but has not yet reset the qualification cadence. **Cited** (miningweekly.com 2025-10-22).
- Stage-gate R&D-to-deployment models are documented as the formal mechanism majors use to admit new processing technology. **Cited** (researchgate.net 313506059).

## Synthesized qualification cycle (the gate sequence a new entrant faces)

Reconstructed from the two cases and the framing literature. The stage *structure* is **Cited**; the indicative durations are **Assumption (Moderate)** because public reports give endpoints, not internal gate dates.

1. **Lab / bench amenability** on the operator's own feedstock — the operator will not rely on the vendor's third-party ore data. (Jetti: this was the late-2017 step.)
2. **Pilot / demonstration on site or on the operator's ore** — the dominant cost and time sink; HydroFloat ran multiple pilot programs plus a full-scale Kennecott demo before commercial sales.
3. **Engineering / integration study** into the existing circuit, plus capital approval through the operator's internal stage-gate.
4. **Commission and ramp** a commercial circuit (HydroFloat Carrapateena: ~2.3 years award-to-commission for a single site).
5. **Re-qualification at every subsequent operator** — adoption does not generalize from one reference; each account restarts near step 1 or 2.

**Indicative elapsed time:** roughly **2 to 3 years from a credible on-site pilot to a single commercial install**, and roughly **8 to 10 years from invention to first commercial reference**, per both cases. **Assumption (High)** that LiT, being pre-pilot with no commercial reference, sits at the *start* of this curve.

## Direct implications for the LiT thesis (read-through, tagged)

- LiT's Month 10 / Month 16 pilot milestones, even if technically met, would only place LiT at the *entry* of the operator qualification cycle, not at signed-license revenue. First licensing revenue from a major on a fresh technology is more plausibly a **3-to-5-year** event from first operator engagement than an 18-month one. **Assumption (Moderate-High).** Feeds Round 05 milestone realism and Round 04 time-to-revenue sensitivity.
- Adoption is **per-operator and per-feedstock**; a model assuming several operator licenses converting in the same early window overstates near-term probability. The realistic early shape is one lighthouse reference operator, then slow fan-out. **Assumption (High).** Feeds Round 04 revenue-ramp and customer-concentration findings.
- The mandatory **pilot-on-own-feedstock** gate means LiT must fund or co-fund operator-specific piloting before any royalty accrues, lengthening the cash-out window and strengthening the case for the producer / build-own-operate or technology-as-a-service alternatives examined in W5. **Assumption (Moderate).** Cross-links `research/r00-businessmodel-economics.md` and DISSENT gate (c).
- The qualification cadence is a tailwind-adjusted constant: critical-mineral demand is pushing majors toward faster adoption, which is a genuine Opportunity, but no public evidence shows the gate cadence has actually shortened yet. Treat accelerated-adoption narratives as upside, not base case. **Assumption (Moderate).**
