Research Brief · US & Canada

The evidence base for a US & Canada risk-advisory practice.

This brief assembles the research behind the market for risk and security advisory across the United States and Canada, focused on two buyer segments: Series A–C software companies and small-to-mid banks. It gathers findings on market size, the two segments, the threat and regulatory landscape, AI governance, cyber insurance, frameworks, pricing, client acquisition, and adjacent technology. The research draws on regulator and government data, major industry studies, analyst reports, and vendor and practitioner sources, each graded for type and tiered for confidence.

Objective What this research is for

This brief gives WNW Advisors a sourced, graded evidence base for two deliverables: a strategy document and a go-to-market plan for a boutique risk and security advisory serving Series A–C software companies and small-to-mid banks across the United States and Canada. It establishes what the market looks like and why the timing is live, so those two documents rest on evidence rather than assumption.

What this brief sets out to do

Size the market and define the two buyer segments, with institution counts, buying triggers, and framework needs. Establish the demand drivers: the threat landscape, the US and Canadian regulatory stacks, AI governance, and cyber insurance. Map the supply side: provider archetypes, the framework and audit ecosystem, pricing, and how firms win clients. Then mark where the evidence conflicts or runs thin, so the strategy is built on tested ground.

What it feeds

A strategy document: ideal-customer profile and segmentation, positioning and differentiation, service design and packaging, and a pricing model. A go-to-market plan: channel and partner strategy, messaging, the sales motion, a launch sequence, and the metrics to run it. The closing section lists exactly what to gather and build to get there.

Part A

Market & the two buyers

Size, regional split, institution counts, the framework needs of each segment, and the documented regulatory link between them.

Task 1 Market size & demand signals

Demand signals point up. The size estimates do not agree.

Analyst firms size the risk-advisory market with figures that diverge by more than 3x for the same period, because each draws a different boundary. The narrower, country-specific figures are firmer.

Published estimates for "risk management consulting" run from roughly $43B to $156B, each traceable to one firm's report and repeated downstream without independent corroboration.FactS1S2S5 The narrower, US-specific line, IBISWorld's "Risk Management, Insurance Advisory & Consulting," sits at about $10.3B for 2026 across ~26,500 businesses, growing low single digits.FactS6

For the Canadian half of scope, the closest sized market is cybersecurity rather than risk advisory specifically. Mordor Intelligence puts the Canada cybersecurity market near $9.67B in 2026 on a ~13.6% CAGR, with BFSI the largest end-user at 29.73% and SMEs the fastest-growing band.Fact single-originS7 Canada represents roughly 7% of the global cybersecurity market.FactS8 These are adjacent figures: cybersecurity spending is broader than advisory fees, so they bound context, not the advisory opportunity itself.

Trust caveatAll global market-size figures here are single-origin analyst estimates with incompatible definitions, and several (Mordor, Cognitive) recur across reports under the same house methodology. They are not corroborated facts. Country-specific government and industry figures (IBISWorld, FDIC, NCUA, OSFI) carry higher confidence and are used as the anchors.

Three structural signals recur with more consistency than the sizing: financial services is the single largest end-market for risk and cyber advisory, on the order of a quarter to a third of demand;FactS4S7 North America holds roughly 37–40% of the global market;FactS2 and the small-and-medium segment is projected as the fastest-growing band, near double-digit CAGR, as third-party and disclosure obligations reach smaller organizations.ForecastS2S7 A persistent talent shortage is also reported across sources, with a projected global gap of over a million cybersecurity and risk professionals.OpinionS3

Task 3 The two buyer segments

Counts, framework needs, and the link between them.

The two ICP segments are sized by regulator data, described separately in the sources, and joined by a documented regulatory relationship.

How many institutions exist

United States

FDIC-insured institutions numbered 4,336 at year-end 2025, down from 4,421 mid-year as mergers continue at roughly 40 per quarter; about 3,980 are community banks.FactS9S10 Separately, the NCUA counted roughly 4,400 credit unions (down from 4,571 a year earlier), serving 144.7M members with $2.43T in assets; over half hold under $50M in assets.FactS11

Canada

OSFI regulates more than 400 federally regulated financial institutions, including six systemically important banks and many small and mid banks.FactS12S14 There are about 35 domestic (Schedule I) banks, 15 foreign-subsidiary (Schedule II) and 27 foreign-branch (Schedule III) banks.FactS13 Most credit unions are provincially, not federally, regulated.FactS16

Fresh developmentOSFI announced a Streamlined Framework launching 25 June 2026 to create a faster, clearer path for provincial credit unions to continue federally and for fintechs ("innovators") to become federally regulated deposit-taking institutions, with phased, risk-based approvals.FactS15 This sits directly across the two segments, lowering the entry barrier for the vendor side into the regulated side.

What each segment needs

Vendor side: Series A–C software

SOC 2 becomes pressing when the first six-figure enterprise or bank deal enters the pipeline and procurement issues a security questionnaire, typically around Series A at roughly $1–2M ARR, when there is budget but no in-house security leader.FactS17S18 A source states that startups whose roadmap includes selling to banks should begin roughly six months ahead, because a bank's review is heavier than a generic SOC 2.OpinionS19 SOC 2 is an attestation, not a certification, and is not legally required; ISO 27001 and, for AI, ISO 42001 are the adjacent frameworks.FactS21

Buyer side: small & mid banks

US community and mid banks are mid-migration: the FFIEC retired its Cybersecurity Assessment Tool on 31 August 2025, directing institutions to map controls to the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, and regulators issued joint third-party risk guidance for community banks.FactS26S28 In Canada, small and mid FRFIs are operationalizing OSFI's principles-based B-13, B-10, and E-21.FactS33S34

The connection is explicit in the Canadian guidance: OSFI's third-party and outsourcing expectations mean vendors serving a regulated institution must evidence equivalent controls; a source paraphrases this as "if you supply technology to a bank, B-13 is effectively your problem too."OpinionS36 The US joint guidance similarly holds the institution accountable for its third parties, and the OSFI B-10 contract expectations name specific clauses: audit rights, ~30-day termination notice, breach notification, and concentration-risk and subcontractor (fourth-party) oversight.FactS28S34

Part B

Why now: the demand drivers

The threat landscape, the US and Canadian regulatory stacks, AI governance, and cyber insurance. The freshness-critical layer, leaning on primary and government sources.

Task 4a Threat & incident landscape

The numbers behind the urgency.

The IBM/Ponemon Cost of a Data Breach study (600 organizations, 17 industries) is the most authoritative source in this set. Its 2025 findings establish the cost and frequency facts underpinning every regulatory and insurance driver below.

  • Global average breach cost fell 9% to $4.44M; the US average hit a record $10.22M. Financial services averaged $5.56M, second only to healthcare at $7.42M.FactS22S23
  • Third-party involvement in breaches doubled from 15% to 30% in a single year; supply-chain compromise carried a $4.91M average and the longest containment time at 267 days.FactS22
  • AI governance is a measured gap: 63% of breached organizations lacked formal AI governance policies and 97% of AI-related breaches lacked proper AI access controls; shadow AI added ~$670K per breach.FactS23
  • The 2025 Verizon DBIR, cited across the insurance sources, found stolen credentials the top initial vector at 22%, used in 88% of basic web-application attacks; ransomware was linked to ~75% of system-intrusion breaches.FactS25
Source handlingBreach figures are attributed to IBM/Ponemon and Verizon directly; several were surfaced via vendor blogs (BARR, Kiteworks, SpyCloud) that resell security services, so the underlying study, not the reseller, is treated as the source.S24

Task 4b Regulatory drivers: United States

Bank framework migration, third-party rules, and a cooling SEC.

The US bank-side drivers are live and documented by regulators. The one enforcement trend moving in a counter-intuitive direction is the SEC's.

  • Framework migration. The FFIEC CAT sunset forces institutions to re-baseline on NIST CSF 2.0, whose new Govern function elevates board-level accountability; one source notes underrating inherent risk is the most common mistake.Fact OpinionS26S32
  • Third-party risk. The joint FDIC/Fed/OCC community-bank guidance makes vendor oversight a full lifecycle obligation and holds the institution accountable.FactS28S29
  • State and card rules. NYDFS cybersecurity regulation, PCI DSS, and a growing patchwork of state privacy laws add overlapping obligations for institutions and their vendors.FactS27
  • SEC cyber disclosure. The rules are settled, with a Reg S-P compliance deadline on 3 June 2026.FactS31
Evidence flag: do not over-readThe SEC dropped its SolarWinds CISO litigation in November 2025, and a law-firm source reports the current Commission signalling a shift toward charging individuals and "genuine harm" over corporate penalties, even while standing up a Cyber and Emerging Technologies Unit.FactS30 Disclosure obligations remain; enforcement intensity is the variable in motion. The evidence supports neither an "escalating enforcement" nor a "dead letter" reading. For private-company ICPs, SEC pressure reaches them indirectly, through enterprise customers' vendor requirements.Fact

Task 4c Regulatory drivers: Canada

A principles-based stack, with real penalties and a vendor reach.

Canada's federal financial-sector cyber regime is principles-based and proportional, which means interpretation work rather than a checklist. The supervisory consequence for a vendor is sharp.

  • OSFI B-13 (Technology & Cyber Risk Management, effective 1 Jan 2024): three domains and 17 principles; aligns with NIST CSF and CIS Controls; pairs with the Technology and Cyber Security Incident Reporting advisory.FactS33S35
  • OSFI B-10 (Third-Party Risk Management) and E-21 (Operational Risk & Resilience): a mandatory lifecycle framework for all third-party arrangements, with concentration-risk and data-residency scrutiny.FactS34
  • Penalties. B-13 has no fixed schedule, but non-compliance can trigger Administrative Monetary Penalties; a source cites a $10K–$500K CAD range and, via the IMF, that Canadian financial-sector cyber incidents tripled across 2022–2023.Fact single-originS35
  • Adjacent layers. FINTRAC enforces AML under the PCMLTFA (criminal penalties to CAD 2M / 5 years); PIPEDA governs privacy; CIRO layers cybersecurity expectations on investment dealers.FactS37

Task 4d AI governance frameworks

Three frameworks, one stack, dates in flux.

AI governance is emerging as a distinct obligation rather than a talking point. Three instruments dominate the sources, and they answer different questions.

  • EU AI Act (the law). Risk-tiered; prohibited practices took effect Feb 2025, GPAI rules Aug 2025, high-risk obligations Aug 2026. Penalties reach €35M or 7% of global turnover. It applies extraterritorially to any AI placed on the EU market or whose output is used in the EU.FactS38
  • NIST AI RMF (the method). Voluntary, not certifiable, organized as Govern / Map / Measure / Manage; widely adopted as a practical risk process.FactS38
  • ISO/IEC 42001 (the proof). The first certifiable AI management-system standard (10 clauses, 39 Annex A controls), accredited in the US via ANAB; Schellman is among the firms accredited to certify.FactS40S41
Evidence flag: dates are movingA Digital Omnibus political agreement reached in early May 2026 proposes pushing EU high-risk obligations to Dec 2027 (standalone) and Aug 2028 (embedded). These are proposed, not adopted; if the Omnibus is not formally adopted before Aug 2026, the original dates stand. Prohibited practices, GPAI, and transparency obligations are unaffected.ForecastS39

For the vendor segment, the relevant fact is convergence: AICPA guidance pulls AI/ML systems handling customer data into SOC 2's security scope, financial-services AI (credit scoring, fraud detection, algo trading) is classified high-risk under the EU AI Act, and a SaaS company embedding a third-party model is typically both a "deployer" and a "provider."FactS38S46 Whether this translates into billable advisory volume rather than tooling demand is not yet measured.Forecast

Part C

Supply, economics, and demand capture

The framework and audit ecosystem, the provider archetypes, pricing benchmarks, how firms acquire clients, and the adjacent technology.

Task 8 Frameworks & the attestation ecosystem

What gets attested, and by whom.

The vendor segment's compliance demand routes through a specific ecosystem: trust-services frameworks, the CPA firms that issue attestations, and the readiness work that precedes the audit.

  • SOC 2. An AICPA attestation against five Trust Services Criteria (Security mandatory). Type 1 is point-in-time; Type 2 covers a 3–12 month observation window most enterprise buyers require. Only a licensed CPA firm can issue the report.FactS21
  • Adjacent frameworks. ISO 27001 (information security management), ISO 42001 (AI management), HIPAA, PCI DSS, and NIST CSF mappings; controls often satisfy several frameworks at once.FactS46
  • The audit firms. A-LIGN reports being the number-one issuer of SOC 2; Schellman and Coalfire are among the established assessors, and Schellman is accredited to certify ISO 42001.FactS45S41
  • The readiness gap. Auditors attest; they do not build the program. Readiness, gap remediation, and evidence collection precede the audit, and one source argues a tool can attest a control exists without attesting that it works.OpinionS46

Task 6 Client acquisition

How boutique advisory firms are reported to win clients.

The channel evidence is consistent in direction but weak in provenance, because most of it lives in agency and consultant blogs. It is recorded as directional, not established.

Referrals are reported to convert 20–30% higher than any other source and to supply roughly 60% of advisory revenue, while stalling once a founder's network is exhausted.OpinionS48S49 Thought leadership is described as the mechanism that creates new relationships at scale and as a live demonstration of a firm's thinking.OpinionS50 B2B buyers are reported to complete ~70% of the buying journey before contacting a provider, and consulting sales cycles run weeks to months.OpinionS49

The Edelman–LinkedIn B2B Thought Leadership study, the most authoritative source in this set, found a large majority of decision-makers treat strong thought leadership as a more trustworthy basis for judging a firm than its marketing, and report being more receptive to outreach from firms that publish it.FactS48 Cold-outreach reply rates are reported below 2%, with a majority of B2B buyers avoiding firms that send unsolicited messages, and specialization is described as the lever a small firm uses to beat a large one.OpinionS48S51

Confidence flagChannel statistics here are drawn largely from agency and consultant blogs and are graded lower-confidence directional input. The Edelman–LinkedIn figures are attributed to that study, not to the blogs that repeat them.

Task 7 Adjacent technology

GRC automation, vendor-risk feeds, and the convergence trend.

The adjacent technology is GRC automation, which sources describe as splitting into three segments and converging toward "agentic" governance.

  • Three market segments. Compliance automation (Vanta, Drata, Sprinto, Secureframe); mid-market GRC (Hyperproof, LogicGate, Scrut); enterprise GRC suites (ServiceNow, Archer, MetricStream, AuditBoard). One estimate sizes GRC software at ~$23B in 2026 on a low-double-digit CAGR.Fact single-originS49S50
  • Capability race. Vanta ships an AI agent for policy and questionnaires; Drata runs 1,200+ hourly tests and acquired a trust-center product; both added NIST AI RMF support in early 2026.FactS48
  • Vendor-risk feeds. BitSight, SecurityScorecard, Black Kite, and RapidRatings supply security ratings and vendor scoring that banks operate inside a TPRM program.FactS52
  • The recurring claim. Across multiple sources, including vendors: "audit automation does not equal operational maturity"; a tool attests a control exists without attesting it works or defining risk appetite. Productized vCISO tiers sit at the low end as software with light oversight.OpinionS46S49
Evidence flagThe "audit automation ≠ operational maturity" line appears in sources that sell governance services or platforms, so it carries a commercial interest. It is recorded as a recurring industry opinion, corroborated across multiple parties, not as an independent finding.Opinion

Part D

Open questions

Where the evidence conflicts, where it is silent, and what it does not settle.

Open Conflicts & gaps in the evidence

What this evidence does not settle.

Naming where the evidence conflicts and where it is silent is part of the record. None of the following is resolved by the sources gathered here.

  • Market sizing conflicts. Global estimates differ by more than 3x and cannot be reconciled; only the country-specific government and industry figures have clean origins.S1S6
  • Advisory-fee market is unmeasured for the niche. No source sizes the specific market for cross-border (FFIEC + OSFI), both-sides fractional advisory; the Canadian figures size cybersecurity spend, not advisory fees.S7
  • Provider-pool overlap is unquantified. Sources do not measure how many boutiques hold both FFIEC and OSFI fluency or serve both vendor and bank sides. The gap is unestablished in either direction.
  • AI-advisory demand is a forecast. AI governance as a billable line rests on early signals (AICPA scope, platform RMF support, ISO 42001), not on observed engagement volume; EU high-risk dates may move.S39S46
  • Productized-vCISO displacement is unquantified. Whether sub-$5K tooling displaces advisory demand or serves a different buyer is asserted but not measured.S30b
  • Channel data is low-provenance. Referral and thought-leadership figures sit in agency blogs; only the Edelman–LinkedIn study is authoritative.S48
  • SEC enforcement direction is in motion. Obligations are settled; intensity is recalibrating. The evidence supports neither a "rising enforcement" nor a "dead letter" reading.S30
  • Cyber-insurance stats are single-origin. The 82% MFA-denial and 41% first-submission-denial figures trace to Coalition and Marsh respectively, surfaced via blogs; corroboration beyond the originating party is thin.S42S43

Next steps From evidence to strategy & GTM

Closing the gaps.

This brief records what is on the public record and grades it. Carrying it into an effective strategy document and go-to-market plan takes two more moves: validate the opinions, and gather or build the primary artifacts the record cannot supply. This is the work list.

1. Turn the opinions into evidence

The 36 quotes in the addendum are signals, not findings. To rely on them:

  • Verify each quote against its original source and date, and capture fuller context where a one-line excerpt could mislead.
  • Convert opinion into primary input through structured interviews with the people these voices represent. On the buyer side, founders and security leads at Series A–C SaaS, and risk or compliance officers at small and mid banks and credit unions; on the supply side, vCISO operators, auditors, and brokers.
  • Seek the missing side. Vendor skeptics, buyers who churned off a GRC platform or fired an advisor, and bank examiners will balance the on-the-record optimism.
  • Add Canadian operator voices, which are thin in the set, given the OSFI and credit-union scope.
  • Triangulate the forecast-style opinions, cyber-insurance pricing, EU AI Act dates, and AI-attack predictions, against primary filings and regulator text rather than secondary commentary.
  • Pressure-test vendor and customer testimonials against independent reviews and, where you can get it, win/loss and renewal data.

2. Documents and datasets to gather or assemble

The strategy and GTM plan need inputs this brief does not contain. Build or collect:

Demand-side primary research

Buyer interviews and jobs-to-be-done, a buying-trigger map (when SOC 2, a bank review, or an audit forces action), win/loss interviews, and reference calls.

Output: a validated ICP and demand narrative

Bottom-up market model

A TAM/SAM/SOM built from the institution and segment counts already gathered, plus engagement-value and pricing benchmarks, reconciling the conflicting top-down estimates flagged in Part D.

Output: a sizing you can defend

Competitive teardown

Profiles of the provider archetypes (GRC platforms, audit and attestation firms, vCISO shops, boutiques), a positioning-and-pricing matrix, and a partner and ecosystem map.

Output: where WNW fits and where the white space is

Regulatory primary texts

Pull and cite the source documents themselves: OSFI B-10, B-13, and E-21; the FFIEC-to-NIST CSF mapping; SEC cyber-disclosure rules; and the EU AI Act with the Digital Omnibus.

Output: primary citations replacing secondary summaries

Strategy artifacts

ICP and segmentation, a positioning statement, a differentiation thesis built on the defensibility and trust angle, a service catalog and packaging, and a pricing model.

Output: the strategy document’s spine

GTM artifacts

A channel strategy built on the referral-conversion evidence, a partner and alliance plan (auditors, insurers, fractional networks), a messaging house, a sales playbook and qualification model, a content and demand-gen plan, a 30/60/90 launch sequence, and funnel KPIs.

Output: the go-to-market plan

Financial and capacity model

Unit economics, a delivery-capacity model that respects the vCISO bandwidth limit raised in the addendum, pricing-to-cost, and a hiring plan.

Output: proof the model is viable, not just attractive

3. Suggested sequence

Validate demand and sizing first, through interviews and the bottom-up model, since they de-risk everything downstream. Build the competitive and positioning view next. Then assemble the GTM artifacts and the financial model. Throughout, treat the addendum’s opinions as hypotheses to test in those interviews, not as conclusions to repeat.
