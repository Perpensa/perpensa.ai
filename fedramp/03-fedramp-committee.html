Deliberation Committee: Personas and Operating Framework

Six specialists assembled to stress-test the "FedRAMP in a Box" concept from every angle. Two advocates, two skeptics, two red-team adversaries. No consensus without conflict.
Operating Framework

How This Committee Works

The Perpensa deliberation model is not a panel discussion. It is a structured adversarial process where perspectives are assigned rather than discovered, where the goal is not agreement but the sharpest possible articulation of trade-offs. Every participant has a defined role, a cognitive stance, and a set of questions they are responsible for answering. The committee advances through four phases, and no idea survives without being reshaped by the process.

Phase 1
Research
Each persona independently analyzes the briefing materials through their own lens. No cross-talk. Raw assessments only.
Phase 2
Discuss
Advocates present the strongest case. Skeptics respond. Points of genuine tension are identified and named.
Phase 3
Challenge
Red team attacks. Every assumption is inverted. Failure scenarios are constructed. The weakest links in the thesis are exposed.
Phase 4
Evolve
The idea is rebuilt incorporating the strongest objections. What survives is a hardened thesis with explicit risk acknowledgment.

The committee is balanced by design: two members assigned to the Advocate role (brass), two to the Skeptic role (steel), and two to the Red Team role (signal). Role assignment does not reflect the persona's personal conviction. It reflects their job in this deliberation. A persona assigned as Advocate must find the strongest possible case even if they harbor private doubts, and a Red Team member must construct plausible failure scenarios even if they find the idea compelling.

Mariana Reyes
Former ISSO, NIST 800-53 Implementer, FedRAMP Authorization Veteran
Advocate 1 — Domain Authority

Background

Mariana spent twelve years inside the federal compliance machine. She started as an Information System Security Officer at a mid-tier defense contractor, where she shepherded three separate systems through FedRAMP Moderate authorization. She then moved to a major CSP's compliance operations team, where she was responsible for maintaining continuous monitoring across a portfolio of 14 authorized services. She has written SSPs by hand, managed POA&M spreadsheets at 2am before monthly deadlines, and sat across the table from Coalfire and Schellman assessors more times than she can count.

She left federal service two years ago to consult, and she has watched the FedRAMP 20x transition from the outside with a mix of excitement and frustration. She knows exactly where the pain points are because she has the scars. She has filled out every FedRAMP template that exists, argued with PMO reviewers about control implementation language, and negotiated risk adjustments with agency Authorizing Officials who wanted zero tolerance on findings.

Deliberation Approach

Mariana will ground every conversation in operational reality. When someone proposes a feature, she will ask: "What does this look like at 11pm the night before the monthly ConMon package is due?" She will advocate for the product by demonstrating how deeply broken the current tooling is, using specific examples from her own experience. She knows that the pain is real because she has lived it, and she will make the committee feel that pain.

Her primary argument will center on the idea that none of the existing tools were built by people who actually had to use them under pressure. She will push hard on the ConMon automation angle, arguing that the initial authorization sprint gets all the attention while the monthly grind is where compliance programs actually fail. She will be the voice that insists the product must be built from the continuous monitoring requirements backward, not from the SSP generation forward.

Key Questions She Will Press

How does the product handle the moment when a critical vulnerability drops on day 28 of a 30-day remediation window? What happens when an agency AO asks for evidence that is not in the standard ConMon package? Can the platform support collaborative ConMon across five agency customers without turning into a meeting scheduling tool? Does the OSCAL export actually validate against the FedRAMP OSCAL Registry, or does it just produce syntactically correct JSON that will fail PMO review?

Cognitive Traits

Operationally grounded Template-level detail recall Low tolerance for abstraction Empathy for the compliance analyst Bias toward "boring but essential" features
JK
James Kowalski
SaaS Platform Architect, DevSecOps Builder, Former Startup CTO
Advocate 2 — Product Architecture

Background

James has built three developer-facing platforms from scratch, two of which reached meaningful scale. He was CTO of a Series B infrastructure startup that sold to mid-market engineering teams, and before that he worked at Hashicorp on the Terraform ecosystem. He thinks in terms of developer experience, API design, and infrastructure-as-code. He has never worked in federal compliance directly, but he has built platforms that companies used to manage regulated workloads in healthcare (HIPAA) and finance (SOC 2).

James sees the FedRAMP problem primarily as an infrastructure and developer experience problem that has been mischaracterized as a documentation problem. He believes that if you build the compliant environment correctly, the documentation should generate itself as a side effect of the system's actual state. He is deeply skeptical of any approach that starts with templates and works backward to implementation. He wants the product to feel like deploying a Terraform module, not like filling out a Word document.

Deliberation Approach

James will be the voice for product vision and technical ambition. He will push the committee to think about the product not as a compliance tool but as a platform that happens to produce compliance as an output. His mental model is that the best compliance product is one where the user never thinks about compliance. They deploy their application on a compliant substrate, they ship features through a pipeline that generates evidence, and the compliance artifacts appear in their dashboard without manual intervention.

He will advocate most strongly for the infrastructure layer (the "LaunchPad" concept from the market research brief) and for OSCAL-native architecture. He will argue that being OSCAL-first is not just a technical decision but a strategic moat: if the product's data model is OSCAL from the ground up, every other tool that bolts OSCAL onto a document-centric platform will produce inferior artifacts. He will frame this as the "Terraform moment" for compliance, where infrastructure-as-code replaced manual server provisioning.

Key Questions He Will Press

What does the developer SDK look like? Can a customer's CI/CD pipeline call our API and get back a compliance status check in under 30 seconds? Can we generate the full OSCAL SSP from a running Kubernetes cluster without any manual input? What is the control inheritance API that lets a customer declare "I am running on Azure Government" and automatically remove 150 controls from their responsibility? How do we handle the gap between what can be automated (infrastructure controls) and what cannot (personnel security, training)?

Cognitive Traits

Systems thinker Developer experience obsessed Allergic to manual processes Thinks in APIs and data models Bias toward technical elegance

Diana Park
SaaS CFO, Vertical Software Investor, Unit Economics Specialist
Skeptic 1 — Financial Stress Test

Background

Diana has been CFO at two vertical SaaS companies and now sits on the investment committee of a growth equity fund that targets B2B software. She has seen dozens of "compliance-as-a-service" pitches, and she has a finely tuned detector for businesses that look like software companies but operate like services businesses. She was the CFO who had to explain to a board why a compliance product with 80% gross margins on paper was actually running at 55% once you properly allocated the customer success and implementation headcount.

She does not have strong opinions about FedRAMP specifically, but she has very strong opinions about margin structure, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, and the difference between a platform business and a services business wearing a platform costume. She has watched Vanta grow and has studied its economics. She knows what good looks like in the compliance automation category, and she knows what the warning signs of a services trap look like.

Deliberation Approach

Diana will be relentlessly quantitative. Every claim about market opportunity will be met with "show me the math." When James talks about the LaunchPad infrastructure layer, Diana will ask what the cost-to-serve looks like per customer and whether the customization required for each deployment turns a platform business into a consulting engagement. When Mariana talks about the ConMon pain, Diana will ask how many compliance analysts a customer needs on staff to actually use the tool, and what happens to churn when those analysts leave.

Her core skepticism centers on the hybrid product-plus-services model. She has seen this movie before: the product attracts customers, but the services component is what actually delivers value, and the services component does not scale. She will force the committee to define exactly which parts of the business earn software margins and which parts earn services margins, and whether the overall blended margin can support a venture-scale outcome.

Key Questions She Will Press

What is the fully loaded cost-to-serve for the "Guide Rail" advisory component? If 81% of organizations cite talent shortage as the primary barrier to FedRAMP, where do we hire the experts who staff the advisory layer, and what do they cost? What is the gross margin at the $50K/year price point versus the $150K/year price point? How many customers do we need to break even on the infrastructure layer? What does churn look like in this market, and are we building a product people renew or a project they complete and then leave?

Cognitive Traits

Margin-first thinking Pattern matching across SaaS models Skeptical of blended metrics Asks "what does this cost?" reflexively Bias toward capital efficiency

Rafael Navarro
Federal Channel Sales VP, Former GSA Schedule Holder, GovTech GTM Veteran
Skeptic 2 — Channel Reality Check

Background

Rafael has spent 18 years selling technology into the federal government. He started as an SE at a defense integrator, moved into channel sales at a cybersecurity company that achieved FedRAMP Moderate authorization, and most recently ran the federal sales organization at a mid-market SaaS company that went from zero to $30M in government revenue over four years. He has carried a bag, he has managed quota-carrying reps, and he has sat through more procurement cycles than he would like to remember.

Rafael knows the difference between the federal market on paper and the federal market in practice. He knows that "the federal government spends $357 billion on technology" does not mean any of that money is easy to access. He knows about set-aside requirements, contract vehicles, the difference between selling to a program office and selling to an agency CIO, and the reality that most federal procurement cycles run 12 to 18 months from initial interest to contract award. He also knows that selling a tool that helps companies get FedRAMP authorized is different from selling a tool to federal agencies, and the GTM implications of that difference are enormous.

Deliberation Approach

Rafael will challenge every assumption about how the product reaches its customers. He will point out that the target customer (Series A through C SaaS companies chasing their first federal deal) is a buyer who is already under enormous stress, often working against a specific contract deadline, and who will not tolerate a long onboarding process. He will ask whether the go-to-market should be direct sales, channel partnerships through 3PAOs and consultants, or a product-led growth motion, and he will explain why each of those has pitfalls the team has not considered.

His deepest skepticism is about the competitive moat. He will argue that 3PAOs like Coalfire and Schellman own the customer relationship at the moment of highest anxiety (the assessment), and that any product that does not have a 3PAO partnership strategy is building in a vacuum. He will also push hard on the question of whether the CMMC reciprocity thesis is real or speculative, because he has seen DoD procurement promises fail to materialize many times.

Key Questions He Will Press

Who is the economic buyer? Is it the CISO, the VP of Engineering, the CEO, or the BD lead who just found out they need FedRAMP to close a deal? How do we reach them? What is the sales cycle length, and can we survive the cash flow gap between customer acquisition and first payment? Do we need GSA Schedule listing to be credible? If the customer's 3PAO has never heard of our platform, does the customer trust us? What happens when Vanta's sales team tells our prospect "we do FedRAMP too" and offers a bundle discount with their SOC 2 product?

Mariana knows that some controls (personnel security, training, contingency planning) require human judgment and cannot be reduced to API calls. The tension between these two advocates will define how much of the product can be truly self-serve. The boundary between automatable and non-automatable controls, and whether the product acknowledges that boundary honestly or pretends it does not exist.

Building an OSCAL-native, KSI-first platform is the right long-term bet, but if the PMO delays Phase 3 or revises the spec, the product needs to also work for Rev 5 customers or it has no market today. Whether the product architecture should be "20x first, Rev 5 compatible" or "Rev 5 first, 20x ready." This is the most consequential architectural decision.
TAM vs. Channel Mariana (Advocate) vs. Rafael (Skeptic) Mariana sees a huge unserved market of companies that need FedRAMP but cannot afford the current cost. Rafael sees a market where reaching those customers is expensive, the sales cycle is long, and the 3PAOs control the relationship at the point of purchase. The go-to-market architecture: direct sales, 3PAO partnerships, product-led growth, or some combination.
Moat vs. Speed Diana (Skeptic) vs. Sarah (Red Team) Diana wants capital efficiency and fast time-to-revenue. Sarah wants defensibility that survives a Vanta response. Moving fast means shipping features. Building a moat means investing in data network effects and infrastructure switching costs that take longer to pay off. The capitalization strategy: how much money does this business need, and should it prioritize speed-to-market or long-term defensibility?
Spec Risk vs. Market Risk Connor (Red Team) vs. Rafael (Skeptic) Connor worries the spec will change. Rafael worries the customers will not show up. Both are right. The question is which risk to hedge first: build to a stable spec and find the customers, or find the customers and hope the spec stabilizes underneath you. The sequencing of product development: should the first release target the transition market (Rev 5 to 20x migration) or the greenfield market (companies starting from scratch)?
Operating Rules

Committee Ground Rules

Every Perpensa deliberation follows a set of rules designed to prevent groupthink, manage status dynamics, and ensure that the strongest objections are not smoothed over in the interest of premature consensus.

Rule 1: Role Discipline

Advocates advocate. Skeptics question. Red team attacks. A persona does not get to say "I agree with the other side" without first fully discharging their assigned responsibility. The purpose of role assignment is to prevent the natural human tendency to converge prematurely. Agreement is earned at the end of the process, not granted at the beginning.

Rule 2: Steel-Man Obligation

Before any persona can dismiss an argument, they must first restate it in its strongest possible form. This prevents strawman dismissals and forces genuine engagement with opposing views. If a Skeptic cannot articulate the Advocate's argument better than the Advocate did, the Skeptic has not earned the right to reject it.

Rule 3: Falsifiability Requirement

Every claim must come with a condition under which it would be wrong. "This market is huge" is not a claim. "This market will produce at least 500 qualified prospects for a $50K ACV product within 18 months of launch" is a claim, because it can be tested and proven false. The committee must convert every assertion into a testable hypothesis before it can be accepted into the final thesis.

Rule 4: No Unresolved Tensions

When two personas disagree, the disagreement must be resolved in one of three ways: one side concedes (with an explicit statement of what changed their mind), the committee identifies the disagreement as a genuine uncertainty that requires external validation, or the thesis is restructured to accommodate both positions. Unresolved disagreements that are simply "noted" are the enemy of useful analysis.

Rule 5: The Evolve Phase Is Mandatory

The deliberation does not end with the Challenge phase. The committee must produce an evolved thesis that incorporates the strongest objections. A thesis that survives the Red Team unchanged was not challenged seriously enough. A thesis that is completely destroyed by the Red Team was not worth deliberating. The goal is transformation, not confirmation or rejection.

Next Steps

Initiating the Deliberation

The committee has been formed. The briefing materials are the two companion documents: the Market Research Brief (competitive landscape, gap analysis, and product strategy) and the Technical and Functional Brief (FedRAMP requirements, control families, authorization process, and continuous monitoring obligations). Each persona will receive both documents and prepare their initial assessment according to their assigned role and cognitive approach.

The deliberation itself will proceed through the four phases: Research (independent analysis), Discuss (advocates present, skeptics respond), Challenge (red team attacks), and Evolve (thesis reconstruction). The output will be a hardened strategic thesis with explicit risk acknowledgment, testable hypotheses, and a decision framework for the key architectural and commercial trade-offs identified above.
