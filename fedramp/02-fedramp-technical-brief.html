What It Takes to Get and Keep FedRAMP Authorization

A complete technical and functional requirements map, from FIPS 199 categorization through continuous monitoring, designed to inform the construction of a market-leading compliance product.
Program Foundations

What FedRAMP Actually Is

FedRAMP was established in December 2011 to provide a standardized approach for assessing, monitoring, and authorizing cloud computing products and services under the Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA), and to accelerate the adoption of secure cloud solutions by federal agencies.[1] It replaced the FISMA process (though not its underlying standards) as the primary vehicle for cloud authorization.[1]

A critical distinction that trips up newcomers: FedRAMP is not a point-in-time certification or accreditation. It is an assessment and authorization program that comes with provisions for continuous monitoring to ensure that deployed security controls remain effective in an evolving threat landscape.[1] This distinction matters because it means the post-authorization obligations are as significant as the initial authorization sprint, and any product targeting this space needs to treat continuous monitoring as a first-class concern rather than an afterthought.

The program is based on the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) SP 800-53 standard, augmented by FedRAMP-specific controls and control enhancements.[1] The mandatory NIST 800-53 standards establish security categories across three dimensions: confidentiality, integrity, and availability.[2] NIST 800-53 Rev. 5 contains 1,196 individual controls organized across 20 control families, with each control potentially having multiple enhancements that add specificity.[3]

FedRAMP is mandatory for all federal agency cloud deployments and service models at the low, moderate, and high-risk impact levels. Any federal agency that wants to engage a CSP may be required to meet FedRAMP specifications.[1] The Office of Management and Budget now requires all executive federal agencies to use FedRAMP to validate the security of cloud services.[2]

The Shared Responsibility Model

A foundational concept for any product in this space is the shared responsibility model. Microsoft's Azure documentation makes this explicit: regulatory compliance in Azure Policy provides built-in initiative definitions to view a list of controls and compliance domains based on responsibility, which can be customer, Microsoft, or shared.[1] For Microsoft-responsible controls, they provide audit result details based on third-party attestations and control implementation details.[1]

The Microsoft Service Trust Portal (STP) serves as the central repository for security, regulatory compliance, and privacy information related to Microsoft's cloud services.[4] Through it, organizations can access the Control Implementation Summary (CIS) and the Customer Responsibility Matrix (CRM). The CRM identifies which controls are inherited from the cloud service provider versus those that remain the responsibility of the customer (the tenant owner).[5] This inheritance model is the single most important technical concept in FedRAMP: a CSP building on Azure Government or AWS GovCloud inherits a significant number of controls from the underlying infrastructure provider, which means the product opportunity lies in helping companies handle the controls they cannot inherit.

Microsoft's Service Trust Portal provides several categories of documentation: Compliance Guides (including ISO, SOC, FedRAMP audit reports and bridge letters), Trust and Transparency Guides (whitepapers on cloud service design and operation), and Azure Blueprints that provide turn-key compliance solutions for regulated industries including FedRAMP.[6] Azure FedRAMP documentation, including the System Security Plan (SSP), continuous monitoring reports, Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M), and related materials are available under NDA from a restricted section of the STP.[1]

Impact Levels & Controls

The Three Tiers: Low, Moderate, and High

FedRAMP authorizations are granted at three impact levels based on NIST FIPS 199 guidelines. These levels rank the impact that the loss of confidentiality, integrity, or availability could have on an organization: Low (limited effect), Moderate (serious adverse effect), and High (severe or catastrophic effect).[1] The number of controls in the corresponding baseline increases with impact level.

Under NIST SP 800-53B (which separates baseline selections from the control catalog), a Low-impact system requires approximately 149 controls, a Moderate-impact system requires 287, and a High-impact system requires 370.[3] FedRAMP layers additional requirements on top of these NIST baselines: the Low baseline adds 1 additional control above NIST, Moderate adds 17 additional controls, and High adds 22 additional controls.[7] This brings the FedRAMP-specific control counts to approximately 150 for Low, 325 for Moderate, and 421 for High when including enhancements.[1][8]

The FedRAMP High authorization represents the highest bar for FedRAMP compliance, requiring nearly 30% more controls than Moderate.[8] Both Azure and Azure Government maintain FedRAMP High P-ATOs issued by the JAB, in addition to more than 400 Moderate and High ATOs issued by individual federal agencies for in-scope services.[1] Microsoft Azure supports more services at FedRAMP High Impact levels than any other cloud service provider.[9]

The 20 Control Families

The controls are organized into 20 families in Rev 5, up from 18 in Rev 4.[10] Two new families were added: Privacy and Transparency (PT), which formalized privacy requirements that previously lived in Rev 4's Appendix J, and Supply Chain Risk Management (SR), which responded to escalating supply chain threats.[10]

Azure provides built-in Azure Policy regulatory compliance initiatives that map directly to FedRAMP compliance domains and controls, available in both FedRAMP High and FedRAMP Moderate variants for both Azure public and Azure Government.[1] Each FedRAMP control is associated with one or more Azure Policy definitions that can help assess compliance, though Azure Policy is only a partial view of overall compliance status.[1]

The Authorization Journey

Three Paths to Authorization

Cloud Service Providers desiring to sell services to a federal agency can take three paths to demonstrate FedRAMP compliance.[1] The first is to earn a Provisional Authorization to Operate (P-ATO) from the FedRAMP Joint Authorization Board (JAB), which consists of CIOs from the Department of Defense, Department of Homeland Security, and General Services Administration.[1][13] The second is to receive an Authority to Operate (ATO) from a federal agency. The third is to work independently to develop a CSP Supplied Package that meets program requirements. Each path requires assessment by an independent 3PAO accredited by the program and a stringent technical review by the FedRAMP PMO.[1]

Once a P-ATO is granted, a CSP still requires an authorization (an ATO) from any government agency it works with. A government agency can use an existing P-ATO in its own security authorization process as the basis for issuing an agency ATO.[1]

Rev 5 Agency Authorization Process

The Rev 5 Agency Authorization path has three phases: Preparation, Authorization, and Continuous Monitoring.[13]

The Authorization Package in Detail

A FedRAMP authorization package documents the security and risk posture for a CSP's cloud service offering. It includes the SSP, which is the "security blueprint" for the system. The SSP defines the authorization boundary and describes the security controls in place to protect confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the system and federal data.[15]

The four core documents form the spine of the package:[16]

SSP: System Security Plan

Describes the system, its authorization boundary, and how every applicable control is implemented. As of January 2026, the FedRAMP SSP template is an Excel workbook with 12 sheets.[17] CSPs are required to use the official FedRAMP template, and they are prohibited from altering or removing content in the template.[15] Required appendices include security policies and procedures (Appendix C), the incident response plan (Appendix I), and the FIPS 199 categorization (Appendix K).[15]

SAP: Security Assessment Plan

Developed by the 3PAO, the SAP describes the scope, methodology, test plan, and rules of engagement for the assessment. Both the provider and the 3PAO sign the SAP, confirming agreement on what will be tested and how.[18] The SAP must be prepared using FedRAMP-provided templates.[15]

SAR: Security Assessment Report

Documents the results of the 3PAO assessment, including the effectiveness of security controls and identified vulnerabilities. Contains the Risk Exposure Table (RET) that catalogs all findings. Without a completed SAR, a provider cannot obtain an ATO.[18]

POA&M: Plan of Action & Milestones

Tracks remediation plans for correcting risks identified during assessments and ConMon activities (security control CA-5).[11] The POA&M submitted with the initial package must correspond to every risk identified in the SAR's Risk Exposure Table.[19] All open risks must be captured on the "Open" tab, even if not yet past due.[19]

FedRAMP authorization package documents must be submitted in designated formats (Microsoft Word and Excel), and some must use FedRAMP-provided templates.[15] FedRAMP also publishes OSCAL-based templates on their GitHub repository for the SSP, SAP, SAR, and POA&M in both XML and JSON formats.[20] Under FedRAMP 20x, these materials shift to machine-readable formats where CSPs submit real-time evidence using OSCAL JSON rather than long-form documents.[21]

Continuous Monitoring

The Obligations That Never End

Continuous monitoring is grounded in NIST SP 800-137, which defines Information Security Continuous Monitoring as ongoing awareness of information security, vulnerabilities, and threats to support risk management decisions.[22] FedRAMP specifies the details in the ConMon Playbook, implementing fixed monthly cadences rather than organization-defined frequencies.[22]

Four parties share responsibility: the CSP (full execution layer), the Authorizing Official (review and approval), the 3PAO (independent validation), and the FedRAMP PMO (oversight and standards).[22] The CSP is responsible for implementing ConMon processes and tools. Each federal agency that issues an ATO is responsible for reviewing the CSP's ConMon activities.[11]

Monthly Deliverables (Non-Negotiable)

Every 30 days, the CSP must upload to a secure repository:[11][14]

Monthly ConMon Package

Updated POA&M tracking all findings and their remediation status (control CA-5). Every open risk must be captured, including those not yet past due.[11][19]

Updated system inventory with a complete listing of all assets within the authorization boundary (control CM-8), updated whenever assets change.[11][23]

Raw vulnerability scan files and reports, when required by agreements with agency customers, covering 100% of system components.[11][14]

Monthly executive summary covering the overall security posture of the system.[12]

CSPs serving more than one federal agency must implement a collaborative ConMon approach, holding monthly meetings at least one week after deliverables are submitted. This is a binding control requirement under CA-7; non-compliance constitutes a finding.[11][22]

Annual Activities

Annual Assessment and Testing

3PAO annual assessment covering core controls plus one-third of remaining NIST control set, along with review of POA&Ms and remediation.[11]

Annual penetration testing conducted by the 3PAO, with oversight of scanning activities.[11] Microsoft's Azure penetration test reports are typically due in September for JAB submission.[1]

SSP and appendix updates reflecting any changes to the system or risk environment.[12]

Contingency plan and incident response plan testing required for Moderate and High-impact systems.[12]

Red team penetration testing and static code analysis required for Moderate and High systems.[12]

Remediation Timelines

FedRAMP enforces strict remediation windows. Critical and high-severity findings must be addressed within 30 days. Moderate findings have a 90-day window. Low findings have 180 days.[14] Missing these thresholds can lead to escalation up to ATO revocation.[14] CSPs should retain 12 months of monthly ConMon packages.[22]

Product Design Implication

The ConMon requirements define the operational reality of FedRAMP. Any product that focuses solely on initial authorization and neglects the continuous monitoring cadence will leave customers exposed to the most common compliance failures. Automating evidence collection for the monthly package can lower preparation costs by 75% according to practitioner estimates.[24] This is where the strongest product wedge exists: making the monthly grind painless rather than just helping with the initial sprint.

FedRAMP 20x

The New Authorization Model

FedRAMP 20x is a modernized authorization path that replaces point-in-time documentation and annual audits with continuous, machine-readable validation against running systems.[25] It was launched in March 2025 and is now in Phase 3, with GSA confirming the new authorization model is permanent.[26]

Under 20x, compliance documentation originates in the development pipeline. Providers submit structured data using OSCAL, generated automatically from the running environment through APIs, configuration files, and compliance-as-code systems. Evidence is produced and versioned alongside application code as a first-class output of the build pipeline.[25]

Key Security Indicators (KSIs)

The heart of 20x is the Key Security Indicator framework. There are 56 KSIs for Low baseline and 61 for Moderate.[27] Each KSI ties to a measurable security outcome that can be validated through automation.[27] KSIs in the "Authorization by FedRAMP" (KSI-AFR) theme tie to FedRAMP-specific standards and policies with government-specific requirements.[28]

Every single KSI requirement and recommendation must be addressed. The FedRAMP PMO uses language carefully: requirements use "MUST" and recommendations use "SHOULD," and both must be addressed in the submission.[28] If a recommendation is not implemented, an explanation and justification must be provided.[28]

20x Phase 2 Completeness Requirements

Phase 2 submissions must meet specific completeness thresholds:[28]

Mandatory Requirements

Automated validation must measure some aspects of the provider's goals for at least 70% of Key Security Indicators.[28]

Validation evidence must be embedded or linked directly from both human-readable and machine-readable submission formats and made available (non-redacted) for FedRAMP review.[28]

A machine-readable schema must be provided that can validate and interpret the machine-readable format.[28]

Information within human-readable and machine-readable formats should fully reconcile.[28]

The 3PAO's role shifts toward confirming that the automation actually works, rather than performing the traditional annual audit.[25]

OSCAL Mandate (RFC-0024)

RFC-0024, released January 13, 2026, mandates machine-readable authorization packages, including OSCAL, for all FedRAMP providers, not just 20x participants.[29] The proposed initial compliance deadline is September 30, 2026, and the final deadline is September 30, 2027. Providers who miss the final deadline could lose their FedRAMP certification entirely.[29]

OSCAL replaces static Word and Excel documents with machine-readable XML, JSON, and YAML, enabling automated validation before submission and dramatically faster SSP creation.[29] FedRAMP has published OSCAL resources and templates on GitHub for the SSP, SAP, SAR, and POA&M, in collaboration with NIST.[20]

The CR26 and Timeline

The Consolidated Rules 2026 (CR26), launched in May 2026, provides a predictable 2.5-year roadmap for cloud compliance. It replaces traditional agency sponsorships with a streamlined Significant Change Notification (SCN) process.[30] Draft policy from April 2026 signals 20x will become the default for new authorizations starting Q3 2026.[31] After Phase 5 (H2 2027), no new Rev5 authorizations will be granted.[27]

FedRAMP has also introduced a new security impact classification system for 2026: Class A (Pilot), Class B (Low), Class C (Moderate), and Class D (High).[32] Currently 28 products on the marketplace carry 20x certification type.[32]

Reference Architecture

How Microsoft Implements FedRAMP at Scale

Microsoft's approach to FedRAMP serves as the reference model for how a major CSP implements and maintains authorization. Understanding this architecture is essential for designing a product that helps smaller CSPs do the same thing.

Microsoft is required to recertify its cloud services each year to maintain its authorizations. To do so, it continuously monitors and assesses its security controls and demonstrates that security remains in compliance.[9] Both Azure and Azure Government maintain FedRAMP High P-ATOs with more than 400 additional Moderate and High ATOs from individual agencies.[1]

Azure Government provides additional safeguards beyond the standard Azure public cloud, including heightened screening of personnel, limiting potential access to systems processing customer data to screened US persons.[1] All US Azure public regions are in scope for FedRAMP High P-ATO, and Azure security controls and operational processes are the same everywhere Azure runs.[1]

Azure Policy as Compliance Infrastructure

Azure Policy provides built-in regulatory compliance initiative definitions that map to FedRAMP High and Moderate control sets for both Azure and Azure Government. These provide an aggregated view to evaluate the overall state of the environment, with drill-down to granular control status.[1] The FedRAMP High initiative definition maps each control to one or more Azure Policy definitions, allowing automated compliance assessment at scale.[33]

The Azure Security and Compliance Blueprint for FedRAMP helps automate deployment and configuration of Azure resources in a FedRAMP environment, providing turn-key compliance solutions tailored to regulated industries.[2]

Product Design Lesson from Microsoft

Microsoft's approach demonstrates that FedRAMP compliance at scale requires three things: a policy engine that continuously evaluates control status (Azure Policy), a shared responsibility framework that clearly delineates who owns what (the CRM[5]), and a documentation infrastructure that keeps evidence accessible under controlled access (the Service Trust Portal[4]). A "FedRAMP in a Box" product should replicate this pattern at a scale appropriate for Series A through C SaaS companies: an automated control evaluation layer, a clear inheritance model, and a secure evidence repository.

Product Requirements Synthesis

What the Ultimate FedRAMP Competitor Must Do

Synthesizing everything above, a product that wants to lead this market needs to cover every phase of the compliance lifecycle, not just the authorization sprint. Below is the complete functional requirements map, organized by lifecycle phase, with the corresponding controls and standards that drive each requirement.

Pre-Authorization Automation

FIPS 199 categorization wizard that walks users through data classification and impact level determination[13]. Authorization boundary definition tools that generate the boundary diagrams, data flow diagrams, and network diagrams required by the SSP[15][18]. Automated gap assessment against the applicable baseline (Low/Moderate/High) with priority scoring. NIST 800-53 Rev 5 control catalog with FedRAMP parameter values pre-populated[7]. Pre-built Customer Responsibility Matrix templates showing control inheritance from major IaaS providers (AWS GovCloud, Azure Government, Google Cloud)[5].

Documentation Engine

SSP generation using both the FedRAMP Word/Excel templates (Rev 5) and OSCAL JSON/XML (20x)[15][20]. Automated population of control implementation statements from system configuration data. SAP, SAR, and POA&M template management with version control[16]. All 12 required SSP appendices with pre-populated content where system data is available[17]. Machine-readable schema generation for 20x submissions[28]. KSI mapping and evidence linking for each of the 56-61 Key Security Indicators[27][28].

Assessment Support

3PAO collaboration workspace where assessors can review evidence, request clarification, and log findings directly within the platform. Evidence linking that maps each control to its supporting artifacts. Automated test execution for controls that can be validated technically (vulnerability scanning, configuration checks, access control verification). Risk Exposure Table generation for SAR findings[18][19]. POA&M auto-generation from SAR findings with one-to-one correspondence to the RET[19].

Continuous Monitoring Platform

Monthly deliverable automation: POA&M updates (CA-5), system inventory updates (CM-8), vulnerability scan aggregation, and executive summary generation[11][23]. Vulnerability remediation tracking with 30/90/180-day countdown timers tied to finding severity[14]. Significant Change Request (SCR) workflow management[11]. Deviation request tracking and approval workflow. Annual assessment scheduling and scope determination (core controls + 1/3 rotation)[11]. 12-month package retention with audit trail[22]. Collaborative ConMon support for CSPs serving multiple agencies (CA-7 requirement)[11][22].

Infrastructure Layer

Pre-hardened environment templates for AWS GovCloud and Azure Government with all applicable controls pre-implemented. Terraform/Pulumi modules for network segmentation (SC family), encryption at rest and in transit (SC-12, SC-13, SC-28), logging pipelines (AU family), and identity management (IA family)[10]. CI/CD pipeline integration that generates OSCAL artifacts as part of the build process[25]. Configuration drift detection mapped to CM-family controls. Automated KSI validation integrated with deployment pipelines (70% automation threshold for 20x)[28].
