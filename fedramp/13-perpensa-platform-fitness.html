Is Perpensa Fit to Be the Platform Under This Product?

An evaluation of Perpensa's architecture against the three product requirement documents: where the platform fits, where it does not, the strong/moderate/weak feature matrix, and the architectural work required to build the product on it.
The finding, stated plainly

No major re-architecture is required. Perpensa is a workflow-and-provenance platform, and a FedRAMP authorization is a workflow.

Perpensa's differentiated asset is not a deliberation engine. It is a flexible intelligent workflow with the ability to record and trace what happens inside every step, sitting over a domain-neutral, content-addressable, tamper-evident evidence store. A FedRAMP authorization is exactly such a workflow: pre-authorization, SSP assembly, assessment, POA&M, then continuous monitoring, each step consuming and producing evidence whose provenance must be verifiable.

Recording and tracing what happens inside those steps is not analogous to the audit trail. It is the audit trail. The platform is therefore a strong architectural fit for the product's hardest and most defensible layer, evidence integrity, immutable package assembly, continuous monitoring over a frozen baseline, and decision provenance, and a weak fit only for commoditized application plumbing, which is the best possible distribution of strengths. The substrate runs on AWS, where FedRAMP-authorized infrastructure already exists, and the evidence schema is domain-neutral, so OSCAL and compliance evidence are simply payloads on it. What remains is bounded engineering work, not re-architecture.

Baseline

Perpensa's distinctive architecture

Stripped of branding, the differentiated core is a domain-neutral workflow-and-provenance substrate. The deliberation agent is an application of it, not the architecture itself. Compute for the product is AWS; the local lab (NAS, Pi, Jetson, workstation) is development and inference, not the product's hosting.

Core · differentiated

Flexible intelligent workflow engine

Configurable multi-step workflows over arbitrary domains. The deliberation flow is one instance; an authorization lifecycle is another.

Core · differentiated

Intra-step recording & tracing

Captures and traces what happens inside each workflow step. This is the provenance/lineage engine, and it is domain-agnostic.

Core · differentiated

Domain-neutral evidence vault

Content-addressable store accepting any domain schema. OSCAL is just another payload.

Core · differentiated

Hash-chained manifest ledger

Append-only, tamper-evident record of what was captured, when, and in what state. Native integrity.

Core · differentiated

Frozen evidence plane beneath a mutable interpretation plane. Exactly the ConMon-over-a-baseline shape.

Application

Deliberation agent

One workflow configured on the engine above. Not the core; one of many possible workflows.

Conventional

AI orchestration + app shell

Model routing, agentic loops, capture plumbing, UI. Valuable, not unique.

What the matrix shows

Seven Strong, three Moderate, three Weak, four CRUD. The Strong rows include the authorization lifecycle itself and the single OSCAL model, and they cover the product's hardest and most defensible layer outright. The net-new work concentrates in three domain-specific Weak rows where Perpensa is still a strong store-and-trace layer, and the four CRUD rows are minor by definition.

The Real Work

Architectural change list

The work is bounded: a handful of genuine items and the commoditized minors. None requires re-architecting the platform.

Real
FedRAMP-grade AWS posture & boundary strategy. Choose the deployment posture (GovCloud vs commercial, which authorized services to inherit from) and resolve the one genuinely non-obvious question: if the platform stores and processes a customer's federal compliance evidence, does it fall inside the customer's authorization boundary, and does it therefore need its own authorization or a clear inheritance/equivalency story? This is a posture and scoping decision to make deliberately, not a re-architecture.
Real
External extension API / SDK. The primary enabling work. A stable, documented surface over the workflow engine, evidence vault, ledger, planes, and tracing, so the product's domain layer can be built on top of the platform rather than beside it. This is what lets the OSCAL and compliance functionality sit on the substrate and inherit its integrity and provenance guarantees.
Real
Scheduled / recurring workflow triggers. ConMon runs on a strict monthly cadence with 30/90/180 timers. The workflow engine needs reliable scheduled and time-based triggering. Bounded, and it extends the existing engine rather than adding a new subsystem.
Real
Multi-party RBAC with integrity-scoped sharing. Customer, 3PAO, and agency reviewer each need scoped access to the same evidence with integrity intact. Standard RBAC plus cryptographically verifiable shared views, which leans on the ledger Perpensa already has.
Minor
Retention & lifecycle management. 12-month package retention and evidence lifecycle rules. Commoditized policy-engine work atop the vault.
Minor
CRUD application layer. Multi-tenancy plumbing, auth, billing, dashboards, onboarding, the procurement-signal ETL. Commoditized by current AI coding tooling; never a gating risk.
Integration Model

How the product is built on the platform

The division of labor

The platform provides the workflow engine, the domain-neutral evidence vault, the integrity ledger, the immutable/mutable plane split, and the provenance tracing. The product's domain layer, OSCAL generation, KSI checks, cloud control inheritance, and the compliance-specific workflows, is built on top through the extension API. The commoditized CRUD (multi-tenancy, dashboards, billing) is cheap to add on either side. The gap between the platform as it stands and the substrate the product needs is the extension API, a deliberate AWS posture-and-scoping decision, and a few bounded workflow extensions.

The product does not require the platform to become something it is not. It requires the platform to expose what it already is, through an API, on infrastructure it already runs on. The hard, defensible parts of the product, evidence integrity, immutable packages, continuous monitoring, and decision provenance, are the platform's native strengths; the domain layer is built on top of them.

Fitness Verdict

Fit for purpose at the layer that matters, with one genuine investment and a pile of cheap ones.

There is no major re-architecture. Hosting is an AWS posture decision, and the domain-neutral schema needs no generalization. What remains is a single genuine piece of platform engineering, the extension API, plus a deliberate AWS posture-and-boundary decision and a few bounded workflow extensions (scheduled triggers, integrity-scoped RBAC). Everything else is commoditized CRUD that is a minor gap by definition.

The reframe is what makes this strong rather than merely workable. Perpensa is a workflow-and-provenance platform, and a FedRAMP authorization is a workflow whose per-step tracing is the audit trail. Seven of seventeen requirements are a direct architectural fit, and they are the defensible ones. The honest watch-item is the boundary question: storing federal compliance evidence may pull the platform into authorization scope, which is a scoping and posture decision to make deliberately, not a blocker.

The product is buildable on the platform without re-architecting it. The path is to build the extension API and the AWS posture as durable platform capabilities rather than one-off custom work, and to build the OSCAL and compliance domain layer on top. The product's hardest and most defensible requirements are the platform's native strengths; the rest is bounded or commoditized. The architecture says the fit is real.
