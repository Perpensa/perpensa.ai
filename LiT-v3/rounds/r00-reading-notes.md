## CRUCIBLE — Process Metallurgist / Separation Scientist

**Reading note.**

1. The platform is two stages, and they carry very different proof states. Stage One is a surface-chemistry physical separation claimed effective across the full particle size range including fine and ultrafine fractions; Stage Two is a sub-500C leaching process. The prospectus calls Stage One "bench-scale validated" across spodumene, REE hard rock, and tailings, and Stage Two "bench-proven for lithium" with REE still in R&D (Cited, prospectus and business plan).

2. The single headline performance number, 16 to 19 percent recovery improvement, is treated by the company as one figure spanning three feedstocks and the full size range (Cited, pitch slide 5). The sponsor stipulations establish this uplift as undefined and unvalidated, and make its lab and pilot validation the explicit use of the seed funds (Cited, stipulations). I read the entire revenue model as resting on one number that does not yet exist as a measured result.

3. A technical tension sits unaddressed in the evidence. The market research states Eriez HydroFloat is engineered for coarse particles above 0.5mm, while the fine and ultrafine field is held by the Jameson Cell, IsaMill, and Metso Concorde Cell, and that Virginia Tech's HHS process is described as having no lower particle size limit (Cited, market research section 3 and 5). LiT claims the fine and ultrafine range specifically. Whether LiT's mechanism is distinct from HHS at the bench is a physics question the evidence does not resolve.

4. "Feedstock-agnostic" is a strong claim. Real spodumene, REE hard rock, and tailings differ in mineralogy, liberation size, and surface chemistry. Bench validation on prepared samples is not evidence that one flowsheet recovers across all three at variable real-world feed. I tag the agnostic claim as Assumption, low confidence, pending the test protocol and feed characterization.

5. The Stage Two claim set (below 500C, roughly 30 percent energy reduction, 20 percent faster, no acidic byproducts) sits in a published low-temperature spodumene field that already reports over 99 percent extraction at 325C NaOH roasting (Cited, market research section 5). The claims are plausible against that literature, which cuts against novelty more than against feasibility.

6. The VT Attachment A scope of work is a three-month, 52,075 dollar conceptual literature-and-flowsheet study plus exploratory tailings preconcentration testing, completed June 2024 (Cited, VT agreement). This is the documented technical work product in the data room. It is a desk study and exploratory bench work, not pilot validation. The gap between this and "bench-validated across three feedstocks" needs to be reconciled.

**Research requests.**
- C1. Published bench data or a test report behind the 16 to 19 percent recovery figure: feed type, baseline method, particle size distribution, and grade-recovery curves. Likely NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL if not in the data room.
- C2. Technical distinction between LiT Stage One surface chemistry and Yoon HHS / conventional fine-particle flotation, drawn from public descriptions of HHS and the Concorde/Jameson/IsaMill fine-particle products.
- C3. State of published low-temperature spodumene leaching (sub-500C NaOH roasting routes) to benchmark the Stage Two novelty and the "no acidic byproducts" claim.

## SPOT — Critical-Minerals Economist

**Reading note.**

1. The revenue base is a 1 percent NSR royalty on operator total production value. Per the binding correction, this is fully indexed to commodity prices, so the royalty base carries full commodity-price exposure, not an omission of price (Cited, stipulations). The market research documents lithium moving from about 8,259 to about 26,278 dollars per ton inside twelve months (Cited, market research section 1). A revenue line riding that base inherits that swing directly.

2. The per-operator royalty rests on assumed production values: 229 million dollars per small or mid-tier operator and 2.29 billion dollars for the major, yielding 2.286 million and 22.86 million per year respectively (Cited, business plan, financial assumptions). Those production-value anchors are presented without derivation. They are load-bearing and I tag them Assumption, low confidence pending support.

3. The royalty is described as "1% of total mine production value," not 1 percent of incremental production attributable to LiT. The narrative simultaneously sells the operator "16 to 19 cents per dollar of recovery" against "one cent per dollar" (Cited, prospectus, business plan). A royalty on total production value is far larger than a royalty on LiT's incremental contribution, and an operator will see that immediately. The basis of the royalty is a commercial fault line.

4. The 2026 lithium balance is genuinely contested in the evidence: Morgan Stanley and UBS see deficits, Fitch and Wood Mackenzie see continued surplus (Cited, market research section 1). The model assumes a benign price environment across Years 3 to 5 without stating a price deck.

5. The REE market is strategically loud but small in tonnage, projected at roughly 197 to 273 kilotons over 2025 to 2031 (Cited, market research section 1). Strategic salience should not be read as revenue scale.

6. The penetration assumption is 10 percent of the accessible small and mid-tier universe plus one major within five years (Cited, business plan). The accessible universe is never sized, so 10 percent is a percentage of an unstated denominator.

**Research requests.**
- S1. Current lithium carbonate spot price as of mid-2026 and the latest 2026 to 2027 supply-demand balance calls, to test the implied price environment behind Years 3 to 5. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- S2. Typical total annual production value (NSR basis) of a representative small or mid-tier lithium or diversified operator, to test the 229 million and 2.29 billion anchors.
- S3. Standard process-technology royalty basis in mining: is royalty conventionally on incremental recovery value or on total production value, and at what rate.

## CLAIM — IP & Patent Strategist

**Reading note.**

1. The IP-moat weakness is settled fact, not a data-room contingency. The company position is a pending PCT application plus an examiner's written opinion, which preserves a priority date and a national-phase runway but confers no exclusive right anywhere (Cited, stipulations). Every deck and plan reference to "internationally patented" and "the only internationally patented platform" overstates this and must be read as a pending filing (Cited, prospectus, pitch slide 8, business plan).

2. The VT Sponsored Research Agreement is the most consequential document in the data room for my seat, and it cuts against the moat. Article 13.1 gives Virginia Tech entire ownership of IP developed by VT personnel; Article 13.4 gives the Sponsor only an option to elect a license within six months of a written disclosure; Article 13.6 says non-election lets VTIP license the IP to third parties; Article 13.5 expires the right if no license is signed within three months of election (Cited, VT agreement). LiT does not own VT-generated separation IP by virtue of the partnership. It holds, at most, a time-boxed option.

3. Article 13.9.a names LiT's background IP as "Extraction and Refinement of Lithium into Lithium Carbonate and Lithium Hydroxide Utilizing Flexible Feedstock and Non-Acidic Reduced-Temperature Leaching Methods" (Cited, VT agreement). That is the Stage Two chemical core. The agreement grants VT a nonexclusive royalty-free research license to it. Notably, the named background IP is the chemical stage, not the fine-particle physical-separation stage. The provenance of the Stage One surface-chemistry method is not established by this document.

4. The freedom-to-operate hazard is concrete. Yoon's HHS process is licensed worldwide to Minerals Refining Company, and Yoon/Luttrell patents are assigned to Virginia Tech Intellectual Properties (Cited, market research section 5). LiT's fine-particle surface-chemistry claim sits adjacent to IP that VT has already licensed to a third party. If LiT's Stage One reads on HHS-family art, freedom to operate is in question.

5. The low-temperature lithium space is densely published and partly patented by others (Cited, market research section 5). A pending PCT in a crowded field faces prior-art pressure during national-phase examination.

6. The IP prosecution budget is 235,000 dollars across US, Australia, India, and Brazil national phases (Cited, prospectus, financials). For contested multi-jurisdiction art this is thin, and the financials show Legal & IP declining to 75,000 dollars by Year 5 (Cited, financials).

**Research requests.**
- CL1. Public patent-database search for any published LiT PCT or priority application and its current status. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- CL2. Yoon / Luttrell / VTIP fine-particle separation patent family, claim scope, and the documented scope of the Minerals Refining Company worldwide HHS license. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- CL3. Typical cost and timeline for national-phase prosecution across four jurisdictions in a crowded mineral-processing art, to test the 235,000 dollar budget.

## ROYALTY — Licensing Deal Architect

**Reading note.**

1. The model is upfront-plus-NSR: 2,000,000 dollars upfront per small or mid-tier operator (250,000 retainer plus 1.75 million at signing) and 10,000,000 for a major, each with a 1 percent NSR royalty (Cited, prospectus, business plan). The comparable framing the company invokes is UOP/Honeywell, Hatch, and Metso process-licensing (Cited, prospectus exit section).

2. The structural choice of NSR on total production value rather than improvement-based royalty is defended on verifiability and dispute-avoidance grounds (Cited, business plan). I read the trade honestly: it removes measurement disputes but asks an operator to pay 1 percent of all production value for a technology that touches an increment of recovery. That is a hard sell into procurement, and the company's own "one cent versus 16 to 19 cents" framing depends on the operator accepting LiT's recovery attribution.

3. The pipeline-to-signature timeline in the plan is aggressive. First commercial license at Month 25, after two LOI retainers in Months 23 to 24, with second and third tier agreements following in Months 25 to 36 (Cited, prospectus milestones, business plan). Mining-major technology qualification is typically multi-year, and the plan compresses pilot data, technical due diligence, and commercial negotiation into a short window.

4. The DLE technology-as-a-service and build-own-operate precedent leads the adjacent market at 36 percent share, and the best-funded tailings comparator (Phoenix Tailings) runs a producer model capturing full metal margin (Cited, market research sections 2 and 3). LiT proposes to capture 1 percent via licensing where competitors capture the whole margin. The model choice is defensible on capital-lightness but weak on value capture.

5. The "no bridge round required" claim depends on the final tranche at Month 22 bridging to first LOI retainers in Months 23 to 24 (Cited, financials). Any slip in pilot data or pipeline conversion breaks that bridge, because there is no revenue before the retainers.

**Research requests.**
- R1. Benchmark deal terms for process-technology licensing comparables (UOP, Metso, Hatch, Jord/NovaCell, Jetti): upfront magnitudes, royalty bases and rates, and exclusivity mechanics. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- R2. Documented LOI-to-license conversion timelines for new processing technology at major mining operators.
- R3. Terms and value-capture structure of the Jetti catalytic licensing model in copper as the closest commercial analog.

## MILL — Mining Operator Voice (Customer)

**Reading note.**

1. I am the buyer in this story, and the plan never prices what it costs me to say yes. Qualifying a new process technology into a flowsheet means feed-specific test work, integration and tie-in engineering, metallurgical accounting changes, and capital approval. The market research flags that only about 25 percent of qualified persons have even evaluated tailings reprocessing, and that incumbents are already embedded in operator flowsheets (Cited, market research sections 1 and 3). The prospectus omits switching and qualification cost entirely.

2. The royalty asks me to pay 1 percent of total mine production value on an NSR basis (Cited, prospectus). As an operator, I would resist paying a royalty on tonnes that LiT's technology never touched. The natural counter is a royalty on incremental recovery, which the company explicitly rejected. This is a live negotiation gap, not a settled term.

3. The named primary targets, including BHP and Freeport, already fund competing technologies (Jetti, Ceibo) and run incumbent fine-particle equipment such as Metso Concorde at scale and Eriez HydroFloat at BHP Carrapateena (Cited, market research sections 3 and 4). I would evaluate LiT against tools I already trust and against vendors I already have relationships with.

4. "Modular mobile deployment" at an abandoned tailings site sounds clean on a slide. In practice an abandoned impoundment raises ownership, access, permitting, and liability questions before a single tonne is processed (Cited, prospectus tailings section). The plan treats abandoned sites as pure opportunity.

5. The advisory bench is genuinely relevant to adoption: de Lacy with Albemarle and governance experience, and Gupta with Freeport and Eriez experience (Cited, prospectus, business plan, pitch slide 12). That is real buyer-side access, and it is the strongest adoption asset in the file.

**Research requests.**
- M1. Documented timelines and stage-gates for a major miner to qualify and adopt a new processing technology, from first contact to commercial integration. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- M2. Evidence on whether majors prefer royalty-on-total-NSR or improvement-based / fee-for-service structures for licensed process technology.
- M3. Ownership and liability framework for reprocessing abandoned tailings impoundments in the US and allied jurisdictions: who holds the residual liability and who can authorize access.

## CAPITAL — Growth Investor & CFO Lens

**Reading note.**

1. The offer is 2,000,000 dollars for 13.33 percent at 13,000,000 pre / 15,000,000 post, common equity with protective provisions, information rights, and pro-rata (Cited, prospectus, business plan ask). The headline return is 27x to 34x on a Year 5 exit of 400 to 510 million dollars (Cited, prospectus, exit table).

2. The valuation is set on a single base case. Revenue is zero until Year 3, then 12.1, 24.9, and 51.1 million dollars in Years 3 to 5, at 83.5 to 92.7 percent operating margin (Cited, financials). The entire equity value depends on a revenue line that begins in Year 3 and on a recovery uplift that is currently unvalidated (Cited, stipulations). I see no published downside or break-even case in the file.

3. The exit multiples (8x to 10x revenue, 12x to 15x EBITDA) are applied to mature process-licensors UOP, Metso, and Hatch (Cited, exit table). Applying mature-licensor multiples to a five-year-old single-product company with concentrated, commodity-exposed royalties is generous, and the comparability needs testing.

4. The tranche structure is investor-protective on its face: 500,000 at close then seven milestone-gated tranches, with two formal reviews (Cited, financials, business plan). But several Year 2 tranches are "automatic upon receipt of a financial report" rather than gated on technical or commercial proof (Cited, financials tranche table). The strongest protection an investor has, the recovery-validation milestone, is not cleanly tied to a named tranche release in the schedule.

5. The "no bridge round, no further dilution" claim is a strong assertion for a pre-revenue company whose first cash inflow is two retainers in Months 23 to 24 (Cited, business plan). Any timeline slip creates a financing gap the plan does not provision for.

6. There is internal revenue-figure divergence to reconcile. The pitch shows 88.6 million dollars five-year revenue and a 32 million upfront plus 56.6 million royalty split (Cited, pitch slide 10), while the financials sum to a different annual path. The headline exit numbers must be reconciled to the projection file before they enter a deliverable.

**Research requests.**
- CA1. Revenue and EBITDA exit multiples actually paid in comparable process-technology licensor transactions (UOP, Metso, Hatch and similar), to test the 8x to 10x and 12x to 15x applied. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- CA2. Seed-stage valuation and structure benchmarks for pre-revenue deep-tech / critical-minerals startups, to test the 13 million pre-money.
- CA3. Comparable funding and any disclosed valuation for Phoenix Tailings (117.8 million raised) and Jetti, as capital-intensity and value-capture reference points.

## WATERSHED — ESG, Remediation & Regulatory Strategist

**Reading note.**

1. The environmental thesis is central to the company's identity, not a footnote: "no acidic byproducts," roughly 30 percent energy reduction, no direct fossil fuel use in extraction, and rehabilitation of abandoned tailings sites (Cited, prospectus, business plan, pitch slides 3 and 8). For the investor frame, the question is whether these are verified, scoped claims or mission framing.

2. The energy and acid-drainage claims attach to the chemical stage, which is bench-proven for lithium only (Cited, prospectus). Extending "no acidic byproducts" and "30 percent energy reduction" to REE and other streams is presented as platform-wide but rests on an active R&D program, not results. The claims should be scoped to verified operations.

3. The remediation-of-abandoned-sites narrative is the highest-impact and the highest-liability claim in the file. Taking a mobile unit to an abandoned impoundment can transfer or create environmental liability, and the prospectus treats abandoned sites purely as opportunity with no liability analysis (Cited, prospectus tailings and environmental sections). This is an asset-or-hazard question the investor must price.

4. The regulatory tailwinds are real and documented: the Global Tailings Management Institute established January 2025, tightening GISTM governance, and a US Interior commitment of 1.2 billion dollars to tailings valorisation across 60 commodities (Cited, market research section 2). The open question is whether tightening governance actually moves operator procurement or simply raises attention.

5. The China REE export controls and the MP Materials DoD offtake with a 110 dollar per kg NdPr price floor are a meaningful policy backdrop (Cited, market research section 1). For my seat, the relevant read is whether such offtake or price-floor mechanisms could ever underwrite LiT's royalty base, which today is fully market-exposed.

**Research requests.**
- W1. Whether GISTM / GTMI tailings governance and ESG procurement criteria have demonstrably changed operator technology-purchasing decisions, or remain reporting-and-disclosure pressure. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- W2. Environmental liability framework for reprocessing or removing abandoned tailings impoundments: does processing trigger owner or operator liability under US and allied regimes.
- W3. Status and durability of US critical-minerals and tailings programs (Interior 1.2 billion, DPA actions) and any government offtake or price-floor mechanisms relevant to a royalty base. Geo and policy angle, carried here because CHOKEPOINT is silent at Round 00.

## DISSENT — Red Team / Structured Adversary

**Reading note.**

1. My job is to try to break the thesis, and the file hands me the breaking point directly. The entire 27x to 34x return rests on a recovery uplift that the sponsor stipulations establish as undefined and unvalidated, where validation is the use of the seed funds (Cited, stipulations). The investment is being asked to fund the experiment that determines whether the central claim is true. That is the headline, and no amount of margin math changes it.

2. The "internationally patented" language across the deck and plan is, on the company's own stipulation, a pending PCT with no exclusive rights anywhere (Cited, stipulations, versus prospectus and pitch slide 8). I will hold every seat to stating this plainly and will flag any reasoning that quietly upgrades a pending filing into a moat.

3. The customer-concentration trap is acute. The named primary targets are also the financial backers and pilot partners of competing technologies, and operators of the incumbent fine-particle equipment LiT must displace (Cited, market research sections 3, 4, and the cross-cutting items). LiT must sell into accounts that already bet on rivals.

4. The value-capture contrast is unflattering. The best-funded comparator in LiT's own lead market, Phoenix Tailings at 117.8 million dollars, captures the full metal margin as a producer; LiT proposes to capture 1 percent as a licensor (Cited, market research section 3 and cross-cutting items). The narrative competes for the same "recover from waste" story and the same policy tailwinds while capturing a fraction of the value.

5. The neutrality hazard is live. These stipulations originate from the CEO (Cited, stipulations provenance). I accept items 1 to 3 as binding facts as instructed, and I will simultaneously police any tendency to let "the seed exists to retire that risk" become a reason to soften how load-bearing and unretired that risk is today.

6. The plan's self-funding claim and compressed timeline (first license Month 25, no bridge round) are the optimistic case stated as the only case (Cited, business plan, financials). A do-not-proceed option and a renegotiate-terms option both need to be live in Round 03, and I will insist on at least one non-base-case option.

**Research requests.**
- D1. Independent confirmation of LiT's actual IP status from public records (any published PCT, assignee, claims), to test the "internationally patented" language against the record. NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.
- D2. Phoenix Tailings and the broader tailings-recovery competitive set: funding, model, and any disclosed valuations, as the producer-versus-licensor counterfactual.
- D3. Base-rate evidence on pre-revenue process-technology startups achieving first major-miner license within roughly two years of seed, to test the timeline as an outlier or a norm.
