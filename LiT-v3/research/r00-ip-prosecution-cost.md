# RR-03 — National-phase prosecution cost and timeline (IP budget adequacy)

**Tier:** T3 Supporting (Web)
**Source request:** CL3 (CLAIM)
**Executed:** 2026-06-29
**Question.** Typical cost and timeline to prosecute a mineral-processing patent through US, Australia, India, and Brazil national phases in a crowded art.
**Unblocks.** Whether the model's $235,000 Year-1 IP budget, declining to $75,000 by Year 5, is adequate to build and hold a defensible international patent estate.

---

## Headline finding

Per-country national-phase entry and prosecution-to-grant is **retrievably benchmarked**, and on those benchmarks the **$235,000 Year-1 budget is adequate to file and begin prosecuting a single patent family across the four named jurisdictions, but only just, and only for one family with no contingency for the things that actually threaten this estate**: a crowded art that draws multiple office actions, third-party oppositions, claim-narrowing, or any freedom-to-operate dispute with the VTIP/MRC art identified in RR-02. The **decline to $75,000 by Year 5** covers maintenance annuities and slow-burn prosecution of one small family but leaves **effectively nothing for new filings, continuations, oppositions, or enforcement**. The budget is sized for *administration of a granted estate*, not for *winning contested ground*, and the art here is contested. Confidence: **moderate** on the cost benchmarks (Cited ranges), **moderate** on the adequacy judgment (Assumption built on those ranges plus the RR-02 FTO finding).

---

## Per-country national-phase cost (Cited ranges)

National-phase entry is the act of filing the PCT application into each country within the 30/31-month deadline; prosecution-to-grant then adds office-action responses on top.

| Jurisdiction | Initial national-phase filing (gov + attorney) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| United States | **$10,000–$15,000** | Includes USPTO fees and attorney filing; no translation. [UpCounsel; Triangle IP] |
| Australia | **~$4,500** | English-language; flat across 30- and 100-page specs. [Fenwick] |
| India | **$4,000–$5,500** | English-language; rises with page count. [Fenwick] |
| Brazil | **$5,600–$9,000** | Portuguese translation drives the spread; rises sharply with length. [Fenwick] |

**Initial filing, four countries combined: roughly $24,000–$34,000** before any prosecution. [Fenwick; UpCounsel]

General per-jurisdiction guidance puts **all-in national-phase cost at $2,000–$15,000 per country**, with $5,000–$10,000 typical once translation, local agent, and filing fees are summed. [UpCounsel; Teak IP; Triangle IP]

## Prosecution-to-grant adds materially (Cited ranges)

- Each examination report / office action costs **$1,000–$5,000 per response**; **$2,000–$4,000** is the common figure for a substantive response. [Daniel Law; Prokurio]
- Standard prosecution runs **18–24 months with at least one office action**; **the number of office actions is the single biggest cost variable**, and a crowded art reliably produces more of them (prior-art rejections, claim amendments, multiple rounds). [Prokurio; PatentArea]
- Total lifecycle for a single jurisdiction commonly reaches **$5,000–$15,000 per jurisdiction** when prosecution is included; multi-country prosecution scales roughly linearly before maintenance. [UpCounsel; Daeryun]

**Realistic all-in to grant across the four jurisdictions, one family, crowded art (Assumption, moderate confidence):** filing $24k–$34k + 1–3 office-action rounds per country at $2k–$4k each ≈ **$45,000–$90,000+**, before annuities. Add the PCT international-phase and any priority-application costs already incurred, plus translation contingencies, and a single well-prosecuted four-country family is plausibly **$60,000–$110,000 to grant**.

## Timeline (Cited)

- National-phase entry deadline: **30–31 months** from priority.
- Prosecution to grant: **commonly ~18–36 months after entry**, longer in backlogged offices (India and Brazil historically slower than the US/AU). [PatentArea; Prokurio]
- Net: a family entered now is **realistically granted in the relevant jurisdictions around Years 3–5** of the plan, which matters because **the estate is not "internationally patented" during the very window in which licensing conversations and the seed-stage valuation depend on it** (ties to RR-01: the LiT filing is pending, not granted).

## Budget-adequacy assessment (Assumption, moderate confidence)

**What $235,000 in Year 1 buys, and what it does not:**

1. **Sufficient for the base case of one family, four countries, filed and entered into prosecution.** $24k–$34k filing + early office actions + the PCT/priority spend fits inside $235k with room for FTO opinion work. On the benchmarks, the headline budget is **not unreasonable for a narrow, single-family strategy**.
2. **Thin for a crowded art.** RR-02 established that the underlying separation art is **VTIP-owned and already worldwide-licensed to Minerals Refining Company**, and RR-12 established that the low-temperature spodumene route is an **openly published category**. A crowded art is precisely where office-action counts multiply, claims get narrowed, and **third-party oppositions or pre-grant observations** appear. None of those is funded with margin here.
3. **No enforcement or opposition reserve.** A single patent-opposition or freedom-to-operate dispute routinely runs into the **hundreds of thousands of dollars**, an order of magnitude above the entire annual IP line. The budget assumes the estate is never contested. Given the provenance question in RR-01/RR-02, that assumption is fragile.
4. **The decline to $75,000 by Year 5 is an administration budget, not a growth budget.** $75k/year covers annuities/maintenance on a small granted family plus light continued prosecution. It does **not** fund new filings to extend claim scope, continuations to cover Stage Two engineering, or the wider jurisdictional footprint (China, Canada, Chile, Argentina, EU) that a genuinely "international" minerals-processing estate would need. The estate the budget sustains is **narrower than the "internationally patented" framing implies**.

**Net for the deliverable.** Treat the IP budget as **adequate for a minimal defensive filing strategy and inadequate for building a contestable moat in a crowded art**. This sharpens, rather than creates, the IP-moat Weakness already grounded in RR-01 and RR-02: the dollars confirm that the plan funds *holding paper*, not *winning a fight over it*. Carry as a Weakness-supporting / diligence-conditions input, moderate confidence. A diligence ask follows directly: a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction IP budget that separately reserves for office-action depth, opposition defense, and FTO clearance against the VTIP/MRC family.
