# RR-14 — Do tailings-governance regimes move operator technology procurement?

**Tier:** T2 High (Web)
**Source request:** W1 (WATERSHED)
**Executed:** 2026-06-29
**Question.** Have GISTM / GTMI tailings governance and ESG procurement criteria demonstrably changed operator technology-purchasing decisions, or do they remain reporting-and-disclosure pressure?
**Unblocks.** Whether the regulatory tailwind is a real demand driver (Opportunity) or attention only (a downgrade of a claimed Opportunity).

---

## Headline finding

On the public record, the tailings-governance regime is a **governance, disclosure, and audit framework, not a procurement mandate**. Across multiple targeted searches, no public evidence was found that GISTM or GTMI conformance has caused operators to purchase a specific class of processing, recovery, or remediation technology. The standard compels operators to *manage, monitor, audit, and disclose* tailings facilities to a defined safety standard; it does not require, score, or reward the adoption of any reprocessing or recovery technology, and it does not create a conformance-driven purchasing channel that a vendor like LiT could sell into.

This is a **negative finding stated with moderate-to-high confidence on the demand-driver question and high confidence on the structural point**: the structure of GISTM is documented and unambiguous, and the absence of a procurement linkage was robust across four differently-worded searches. It is moderate rather than high only because absence of public evidence is not proof of absence of effect.

**Implication for the deliverable.** Any Opportunity that reads "tightening tailings governance creates demand for LiT's technology" must be **downgraded from a demand driver to a background tailwind / attention driver**, or carry an explicit Assumption tag with a low confidence band. The regulatory regime raises the salience and disclosed cost of tailings, which can indirectly favor anything that reduces tailings volume or liability, but the causal chain from GISTM conformance to a purchase order for LiT is unestablished and should not be asserted as Cited.

---

## What the regime actually is (Cited)

- The **Global Industry Standard on Tailings Management (GISTM)** was published in **August 2020** by the ICMM, the UN Environment Programme, and the Principles for Responsible Investment, following the 2019 Brumadinho dam failure. It comprises **six topic areas, 15 principles, and 77 auditable requirements**, with the goal of "zero harm to people and the environment" and prevention of catastrophic tailings-facility failure. [ICMM; WSP]
- Scope of the 77 requirements is **safety and lifecycle management**: planning, design, construction, operation, maintenance, monitoring, closure, and post-closure. The obligations fall on the operator's *management system and engineering accountability* (designated Engineer of Record, Accountable Executive, technical review boards), not on the operator's *supplier selection*. [ICMM; WSP; AusIMM]
- **ICMM members committed** that facilities with "extreme" or "very high" potential consequences would conform by **August 2023**, and all other facilities by **August 2025**, with public conformance disclosure published by **5 August 2025**. ICMM members are ~one-third of global metals-and-mining output; non-members (the long tail of operators, including most owners of legacy/abandoned impoundments) are not bound. [ICMM 2025]
- The **Global Tailings Management Institute (GTMI)** was established to oversee independent assessment, audit, and certification of conformance through a multi-stakeholder board. It is a **conformance-verification body, not a procurement or technology-standards body**. [GTMI; SRK Consulting]
- "Regulatory trends suggest increasing government adoption of international tailings standards as **mandatory requirements** rather than voluntary guidelines" — i.e., the trajectory is toward mandatory *disclosure and safety conformance*, still not toward technology mandates. [Barr Engineering; Discovery Alert]

## Where conformance does create vendor demand (Cited, but not LiT's category)

The demand GISTM provably creates is concentrated in **compliance-adjacent services and software**, not in mineral-recovery process technology:
- **Monitoring instrumentation and governance software** (e.g., facility-monitoring dashboards marketed explicitly for "GISTM compliance"). [GDMS; LSI-Lastem G.Re.T.A.]
- **Engineering, audit, and assurance services** — the standard's new designated-role requirements drive demand for tailings engineers, independent technical reviewers, and audit teams spanning seven disciplines. [AusIMM; SRK; ICMM conformance protocols]
- **Education, training, and upskilling** across the industry to staff the new accountability structure. [AusIMM]

None of these is LiT's product. LiT is a fine-particle recovery / reprocessing technology; the GISTM-driven spend is in monitoring, governance, and assurance.

## The indirect (uncited, assumption-grade) channel

A defensible *indirect* argument exists and should be tagged Assumption (low confidence), not Cited:
- GISTM raises the **disclosed engineering, monitoring, closure, and post-closure cost** of holding a tailings facility, and increases scrutiny of new impoundments. Anything that **reduces tailings volume or shortens facility life** (reprocessing that removes mass) therefore reduces a now-more-visible liability. This *could* improve the internal business case for a reprocessing technology at the margin.
- But this chain runs through the operator's own cost-benefit and capital allocation, not through any GISTM requirement. There is **no conformance credit for reprocessing**, no procurement scoring, and no offtake. The regime makes the problem more expensive to disclose; it does not pay for or require LiT's solution.

## Confidence and residual gap

- **High confidence:** GISTM/GTMI is a safety-governance-and-disclosure regime with auditable safety requirements and no technology-procurement mandate.
- **Moderate confidence:** It is not currently a demonstrable demand driver for processing/recovery technology purchases (negative finding from absence of public evidence across four searches).
- **Residual gap (not blocking):** Operator-internal procurement criteria are private. It is possible individual majors fold tailings-reduction into ESG-weighted vendor scoring, but no public artifact confirms this. Flag as `NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL: operator ESG procurement scorecards / RFP criteria weighting tailings reduction — only obtainable from operator procurement teams or a buy-side diligence call`. Not required to open Round 01; the negative finding stands.
