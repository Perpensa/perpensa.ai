# RR-06 — Royalty-basis convention: total production value vs incremental (T2, Web)

**Status:** Executed by Moderator via WebSearch, 2026-06-29.
**Source requests:** S3 (SPOT), M2 (MILL). Tier 2 High.
**Question:** In mining process-technology licensing, is royalty conventionally levied on total production value or on incremental recovery value, at what rate, and which structure do majors accept?
**Unblocks:** The commercial fault line in the 1 percent NSR-on-total-value model and the live negotiation gap in adoption.

---

## Findings

### 1. Two distinct royalty worlds are being conflated by the LiT structure

The evidence separates into two conventions that LiT's model appears to merge:

**(a) Mineral-rights / NSR royalties — paid to the owner of the ground or the deposit.**
- **Cited.** A Net Smelter Return (NSR) royalty is a percentage of net revenue from sale of the metal product, after pre-negotiated deductions for transport, treatment, smelting, and refining. It is contingent on sale price and quantity sold.
- **Cited.** Standard NSR royalties run **2% to 5%** of net smelter return. These are paid to the holder of a mineral or property right, not to a technology vendor, and they are levied on the **total** production value because the royalty holder owns a claim on the whole orebody's output.
- **Cited.** Gross-revenue royalties (on total sales with no deductions) exist but are described as "simpler but often more burdensome" and are less common than NSR.

**(b) Technology / patent licensing royalties — paid to a process-technology provider.**
- **Cited.** Technology royalties are conventionally pegged to the **value the technology creates**, not the licensee's total output. In low-margin contexts the rule of thumb is explicit: if a licensee expects ~4% additional profit from deploying an invention, the royalty should sit in the **1–1.5% of net revenue earned from deploying that technology** range, i.e. a share of the incremental gain, not the gross.
- **Cited.** Licensing practice for process/efficiency technology "aligns with cost savings or productivity gains rather than product pricing"; licensees evaluate how the patented technology improves throughput, quality, or recovery, and price the royalty off that incremental contribution.
- **Cited / convention.** The broader licensing norm (the value-share or "25% rule" family of approaches) holds that a licensor captures a minority share of the incremental profit its technology enables, not a slice of the entire revenue line the licensee already had without it.

### 2. The commercial fault line in LiT's 1% NSR-on-total-value model

- **Assumption (high confidence) grounded in the above.** LiT proposes a **1% NSR levied on the licensee's total production value**. That borrows the *form* of a mineral-rights NSR royalty (a claim on total output) while the *economic basis* of a technology license is a share of the **incremental** recovery value the technology actually adds. A miner already producing lithium does not owe a technology vendor a slice of output the miner would have recovered anyway.
- **The negotiation gap.** A sophisticated major will argue the royalty base should be the **incremental tonnes/value attributable to the recovery uplift** (per Sponsor Stipulation 2, an unvalidated 16–19% improvement), not total production. On a process improvement of that size, value-share licensing convention points to a royalty on the increment, which is a far smaller base than total NSR. 1% of total production value can exceed a conventional share of the increment, giving the miner a direct incentive to resist, delay, or self-engineer around the structure.
- **Rate-vs-base interaction.** The headline "1%" looks modest against the 2–5% NSR norm, but the base is the lever: 1% on *total* value is not obviously below a value-share royalty on the *increment*. The structure's aggressiveness lives in the base definition, not the rate.

### 3. What majors actually accept

- **Cited / contextual.** Documented mineral-processing license terms in the public record use modest, increment- or recovery-linked structures (e.g. one agreement: 2% of the value of recovered precious metals, or a fixed annual minimum, whichever is greater), and government-set processing/mineral royalties (BLM sodium/potassium) sit at 5–6% of gross value. Process licensors typically price off the value their step adds and frequently pair a running royalty with upfront/milestone fees rather than claiming the operator's full NSR.
- **NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL:** exact confidential royalty bases and rates in recent lithium / critical-minerals process-technology licenses (e.g. specific Jetti, Nemaska, or converter-technology deals) are not in the public record at claim-level detail. Public proxy captured here; deal-specific base definitions remain a data-room item, overlapping RR-07.

---

## Implications for the deliberation (carried, not yet a deliverable claim)

1. **The 1% NSR-on-total model is a Weakness and a Threat to adoption speed.** It is out of step with technology-licensing convention, which prices off incremental value created. Expect base-of-royalty to be the central, time-consuming negotiation point with any major, feeding directly into the RR-08 adoption-timeline question.
2. **Sensitivity the model likely hides.** If, in negotiation, the base shifts from total production value to incremental recovery value, the per-license royalty revenue could fall by close to an order of magnitude (the increment is ~16–19% of recovered lithium versus 100% of production). This is a first-order risk to the revenue ramp, independent of the recovery claim itself being validated.
3. **Cross-link.** Pairs with RR-04 (commodity exposure of whatever base is agreed) and RR-07 (comparable deal structures). The royalty *rate* is not the issue; the royalty *base* is.
