# RR-07 — Process-technology licensing comparable deal terms

**Tier:** T2 High | **Retrieval class:** External-likely (public proxy captured; per-deal terms undisclosed)
**Merges reading-note requests:** R1 (ROYALTY), R3 (ROYALTY)
**Prepared by:** Moderator (WebSearch/WebFetch) | **Date:** 2026-06-29
**Question.** What are the benchmark upfront magnitudes, royalty bases and rates, and exclusivity mechanics for process-technology licensors (UOP, Metso, Hatch, Jord/NovaCell, Jetti), and how does LiT's "$2M / $10M upfront plus 1% NSR" structure compare, with the Jetti copper catalytic model as the closest commercial analog?

---

## Findings

### F1 — A running royalty as a percent of sales is the dominant licensing structure, and an upfront fee is the minority add-on, not the core of the deal. (Cited, high confidence)
Across general technology-licensing practice, a running royalty expressed as a percent of sales is the single most common payment form: roughly **62% of deals** use a running royalty as a percent of sales as the sole consideration, rising to about **72%** once per-unit running royalties are included. An upfront cash payment **in addition to** a running royalty appears in just under **22%** of deals. The structural implication for LiT is that its instinct to pair an upfront with a running royalty is in-market in form, but the upfront is conventionally a modest signing/access component, and the running royalty is where lifetime value is actually captured. This puts the weight of LiT's economics squarely on the royalty base and rate, not the headline $2M/$10M upfront.

### F2 — The royalty base, not the rate, is where LiT diverges from convention, and that divergence is the negotiation fault line. (Assumption, high confidence; reasoned from F1 and [[r00-royalty-basis]])
LiT levies 1% NSR on the operator's **total** production value. In mineral-rights and metal-streaming practice a 1-3% NSR on total value is a recognized form, but it is paid for granting access to an orebody or financing a mine, not for a marginal processing improvement. In process-technology licensing the convention is to price the royalty off the **value the technology creates** (incremental recovery or throughput), because that is what the licensee will agree to pay a perpetual cut of. LiT borrows the streaming *form* (total-value NSR) while selling a process *improvement*, which is the structural reason a sophisticated major's procurement and legal teams will push to reprice the base to incremental value. The rate (1%) is unremarkable; the base (total, not incremental) is the contested term. See [[r00-royalty-basis]] for the convention detail.

### F3 — Jetti is the closest commercial analog and validates the asset-light licensor structure, but its specific deal economics are not public. (Cited for model, NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL for terms)
Jetti Resources is the nearest in-market comparable: an asset-light licensor of a proprietary catalytic process deployed "plug and play" into a miner's existing circuit, selling the technology and integration rather than the metal (see [[r00-producer-vs-licensor]]). It demonstrates that a single-process mineral-recovery licensor can be highly valued ($2.5B, 2022). However, Jetti's actual contract mechanics with Capstone and others — royalty base, rate, upfront magnitude, exclusivity, and whether it charges on total or incremental copper value — are **not publicly disclosed**. This is the precise comp that would tell us whether LiT's 1%-on-total-value base is in-market for a catalytic/process licensor, and it remains a **NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL** (Jetti/Capstone licensing terms, obtainable only from proprietary deal databases or the parties).

### F4 — Named engineering/process licensors (UOP, Metso, Hatch) operate on a different model than LiT and are weak direct comparables. (Cited, moderate confidence)
The classic process licensors named in the request — UOP (refining/petrochemical process licensing), Metso and Hatch (metallurgical engineering and flowsheet/EPC) — license proven, reference-plant-backed flowsheets typically bundled with engineering, equipment, and performance guarantees, and monetize through a mix of license fees, engineering/equipment margin, and catalyst or consumables sales rather than a perpetual NSR on the operator's metal revenue. Public per-deal royalty terms for these firms are not disclosed. They are useful as evidence that **process licensing is an established, valuable category**, but they are poor numerical comparables for a pre-validation single-improvement NSR royalty: their leverage comes from proven reference installations and a bundled commercial offering LiT does not yet have.

### F5 — The credible LiT structure follows from the convention: a meaningful upfront and milestone payments de-risk the licensor, while the perpetual royalty is conventionally repriced toward incremental value. (Assumption, moderate confidence)
Reading F1-F4 together, the in-market shape of a deal LiT could actually close looks less like "1% of total production value in perpetuity" and more like a staged structure: an access/qualification upfront, milestone payments on demonstrated recovery at the licensee's site, and a running royalty whose base a major will press to define as incremental recovered value. The investor should price the model on the **repriced** convention, not the sponsor's total-value assumption, and treat the gap between the two as a primary diligence and valuation-sensitivity item. This compounds with the unvalidated recovery uplift ([[r00-recovery-evidence]]): the same number that is unproven technically is also the base a counterparty will contest commercially.

---

## Effect on the deliberation
- Confirms that LiT's pairing of an upfront with a running royalty is **conventional in form**, so the structure is not itself a red flag; the red flag is the **royalty base** (total vs incremental value), which feeds the Weaknesses quadrant and the central commercial risk in Strategy.
- Establishes Jetti as the **directional** validation of the asset-light licensor model while flagging that its actual terms are unretrievable publicly, so any claim that "1% NSR on total value is market" is **Unsupported** and may not enter a deliverable as fact.
- Tells the execution plan to model and stress-test a **repriced (incremental-value) royalty** scenario alongside the sponsor's total-value case, and to treat first-license term sheets as a diligence gate, not an assumption.
- Bounds value capture: convention puts lifetime value in the royalty, so the durability and defensibility of the royalty base over the asset's life is the value driver the investor must underwrite, not the one-time upfront.

## Residual gap
**NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL:** Jetti/Capstone (and other catalytic/process-licensor) per-deal terms — royalty base, rate, upfront, exclusivity — from proprietary deal-term databases or the contracting parties. UOP/Metso/Hatch per-deal royalty economics are likewise undisclosed. Public retrieval establishes the *conventions* (running royalty dominant, upfront a minority add-on, base priced off value created) but not a clean numeric comp for a 1%-NSR-on-total-value mineral-recovery license. Sufficient to ground the base-vs-rate critique at the SWOT/strategy level; precise comp deferred.
