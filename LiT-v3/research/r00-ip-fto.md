# RR-02 — Yoon/HHS patent family and freedom-to-operate exposure

**Tier:** T1 Critical | **Retrieval class:** External-likely
**Merges reading-note requests:** C2, CL2
**Prepared by:** Moderator (WebSearch/WebFetch) | **Date:** 2026-06-29
**Question.** What is the claim scope of the Yoon/Luttrell/VTIP fine-particle (HHS) separation family, the documented scope of the Minerals Refining Company worldwide license, and how does LiT's Stage One surface chemistry differ from HHS as publicly described?

---

## Findings

### F1 — HHS is VTIP-owned and licensed to Minerals Refining Company since September 2012. (Cited, high confidence)
The hydrophobic-hydrophilic separation (HHS) process was developed at Virginia Tech by Roe-Hoan Yoon and colleagues, assigned to **Virginia Tech Intellectual Properties, Inc. (VTIP)**, and licensed to **Minerals Refining Company (MRC), Richmond, Virginia**. Search results state MRC "reached an agreement in September 2012 with Virginia Tech Intellectual Properties, Inc. (VTIP) to license the HHS Process" for **worldwide distribution**. HHS exploits the differing surface physics of oil and water to recover ultrafine particles too small for froth flotation and deliver them at single-digit moisture.

### F2 — MRC's licensed field is not coal-only; it explicitly reaches minerals and critical minerals. (Cited — this is the FTO crux)
MRC's own materials state the field of use extends well beyond coal:
- "HHS is applicable to **any ore that is recovered via flotation**." (MRC minerals page)
- MRC's stated markets: core metallurgical and steam coal, with **"future applications in the broader minerals industry, such as copper, lead, zinc and silver, as well as industrial metals and rare earths."**
- MRC reports HHS "tested at lab scale on several cleaner-scavenger tails with good-to-excellent results" and "ready for both lab and pilot plant testing of copper and other metallic ores."
- Particle size: "HHS has **no known lower particle size**," "can efficiently capture particles that are less than a single micron," and "becomes more selective as particle sizes become smaller" (corroborated by NETL technical materials cited in the locked market research).

The investor-relevant implication: LiT's Stage One value proposition (selective recovery of fine/ultrafine particles by surface chemistry, including from tailings) sits squarely inside the technical field that VTIP has already licensed worldwide to a third party, and MRC is actively extending that license claim into minerals, rare earths, and tailings/scavenger streams — the same feedstocks LiT targets.

### F3 — A related low-temperature extraction family from the same VT team is also VTIP-owned. (Cited)
US20240368734A1 (priority Dec 4, 2020; inventors Yoon, Noble, Council-Troche, Liu; **assignee VTIP**) claims ≤100C base-plus-electrolyte recovery of rare earths from clays, monazite, apatite, xenotime, bastnasite, and coal byproducts. It is REE-focused, not spodumene/lithium, so it is adjacent art rather than a direct read on LiT's Stage Two lithium claim — but it confirms the pattern: the low-temperature critical-minerals chemistry lineage attributable to LiT's academic partners is held by VTIP, layering an ownership question on top of the FTO question.

### F4 — Stage One provenance and the difference from HHS cannot be established from public sources. (Assumption, low confidence pending claim text)
Public materials describe HHS as a general fine-particle surface-chemistry recovery method with no lower size limit. LiT's prospectus describes Stage One as a surface-chemistry method for selective fine/ultrafine separation. On the public record these read as the **same technical field**; whether LiT's Stage One is a patentably distinct method or a configuration that reads on HHS art cannot be determined without LiT's actual claim text and a claim chart. Treat any "Stage One is clearly differentiated / clear FTO" assertion as Unsupported until a claim chart exists.

---

## Effect on the deliberation
- Grounds the **Technology/IP: High** risk vector: the Stage One core overlaps VT IP already licensed worldwide to MRC, and MRC is expanding that license posture into exactly LiT's target minerals and feedstocks. This is both a **freedom-to-operate threat** (LiT may need rights it does not hold, or may infringe) and an **ownership/exclusivity threat** (the field may already be spoken for).
- Compounds Stipulation 1: even setting aside that LiT's own filing is a pending PCT, the surrounding field is occupied by a VTIP-owned, MRC-licensed estate. "Clean exclusive IP" is contradicted from two directions: LiT does not own a grant, and the adjacent field is already licensed to a competitor-adjacent party.
- Channel-conflict / partner-risk flag for Threats and Execution: VTIP is simultaneously LiT's IP source (via the sponsored-research agreement) and MRC's licensor. The same institution monetizing the same separation lineage to two parties is a structural conflict the investor must diligence.

## Residual gaps
**NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL (1):** the executed MRC-VTIP license agreement — exact field of use (coal-only vs all-minerals), exclusivity, and any carve-outs — to determine whether MRC's worldwide rights actually foreclose LiT's lithium/REE/tailings field or stop at coal. MRC's marketing asserts broad applicability; the binding grant language is not public.
**NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL (2):** a claim-chart comparison of LiT's Stage One (from its PCT/provisional claims) against the HHS patent family and against US20240368734A1, to assess infringement risk and patentable distinctness. Requires LiT's unpublished claim text.

Both gaps are condition-precedent diligence items: an independent FTO / claim-chart opinion and review of the VTIP-MRC and LiT-VTIP agreements should gate any investment.
