## Validation taxonomy (governs every cluster in this round)

Each Round 01 candidate exits convergence with one disposition:

- **CONFIRMED** — survives both steelman and challenge on the locked evidence; enters the matrix as a standalone finding.
- **CONFIRMED (load-bearing)** — confirmed and decision-determining; carries kill-switch standing if DISSENT reserved it and it is upheld here.
- **MERGED** — true but not standalone; folded into a master finding and cited as a contributing leg.
- **CONTESTED-SPLIT** — existence confirmed, severity genuinely disputed; both readings carried into the deliverable without forcing consensus.
- **DOWNGRADED** — narrowed in scope or confidence relative to how it was filed.
- **REJECTED** — not supported; drops out of the pool.

A cluster can produce a master finding (a new merged ID, prefixed `W-`, `T-`, `S-`, or `O-`) plus per-item dispositions and risk-register cross-links (prefixed `RR-`).

## Cluster 1 — IP rights: does LiT hold the rights its model sells?

**Candidates in cluster:** CRU-W2 (foundational separation stage is VTIP art, not named LiT background IP); ROY-W3 (sublicensable rights to the underlying VT/VTIP IP unestablished and contractually contingent); CHO-T2 (offshore-R&D vs. IP-defensibility / export-control tension); DIS-W1 (load-bearing, kill-switch standing reserved: the IP foundation may not be LiT's to license). Adjacent and cross-linked, not resolved here: CAP-W5 (R&D-delivery cost-base evidence gap).

This is the highest-stakes cluster in the deliberation. The company's entire commercial architecture is to license a process to mine operators and collect a running royalty. That architecture presumes one thing above all others: that LiT controls IP it is legally entitled to sublicense to third parties in the minerals field. If it does not, there is no product to sell, regardless of how well the science performs.

### The merged question

Strip the four candidates to their common spine and they ask one question in three parts:

1. **Ownership.** Is the foundational technology LiT's invention, or VTIP's? (CRU-W2, part of DIS-W1)
2. **Grant capacity.** Even where the technology is VTIP's, has LiT secured a license that includes the right to sublicense to operators in its field? (ROY-W3, part of DIS-W1)
3. **Third-party foreclosure.** Has VTIP already licensed the same core to another party in the minerals field, narrowing or closing what LiT could obtain? (the MRC question inside DIS-W1)

CHO-T2 is adjacent: it concerns how the *offshore development path* interacts with US export law and the IP-control story. It rides the same fact pattern but is a distinct vector and is resolved separately below.

### Steelman — the case that this is do-not-proceed-relevant as framed

The strongest version of the weakness is built entirely from the locked evidence, not from inference.

- **The default grant is the wrong license.** Under the only executed instrument in the base, Art. 13.4 grants the Sponsor, by default, a "non-exclusive, non-transferable, royalty-free license for internal research purposes" to Virginia Tech IP. Internal-research rights cannot be sublicensed to an operator. [Cited: VT Agreement Art. 13.4] **High.**
- **The sublicense right exists only on the exclusive path, and that path is unexercised.** Of the two elective alternatives, only the Exclusive License (Art. 13.4(b)) carries "the right to sublicense." The Non-Exclusive alternative (Art. 13.4(a)) is expressly "(without the right to sublicense)." To reach a sublicensable position, LiT must (i) elect the exclusive alternative in writing within six months of VTIP's disclosure, then (ii) negotiate and sign a separate, royalty-bearing license within three months of electing, failing which "the Sponsor's rights under this article will expire" (Art. 13.5, 13.6). Nothing in the base shows this election was made or that a separate license was signed. [Cited: VT Agreement Art. 13.4(a)(b), 13.5, 13.6] **High that the path is contingent and its exercise is unevidenced.**
- **The foundational separation stage is not named as LiT's.** The single piece of background IP the contract attributes to the Sponsor is "Extraction and Refinement of Lithium into Lithium Carbonate and Lithium Hydroxide Utilizing Flexible Feedstock and Non-Acidic Reduced-Temperature Leaching Methods" (Art. 13.9(a)). That is the chemical refinement stage. The physical fine/ultrafine separation core that the dossier calls the technical foundation is not in that clause; on the public record it maps onto Hydrophobic-Hydrophilic Separation, a VTIP-owned process. [Cited: VT Agreement Art. 13.9(a); `r00-ip-fto.md`] **High.**
- **A prior VTIP license may foreclose the field.** VTIP licensed HHS to Minerals Refining Company, whose stated field spans "coal and minerals." Both elective alternatives in Art. 13.4 are granted "subject to third-party rights, if any." If the MRC license is exclusive and worldwide in minerals, LiT cannot obtain rights to the same separation core in the same field. [Cited: `r00-ip-fto.md`; VT Agreement Art. 13.4] **Existence of the MRC minerals license: High; its exclusivity/scope: Assumption, Medium.**
- **F-1 removes the optimistic reading.** The sponsor stipulation establishes as fact that LiT has no clean, exclusive IP and holds at most a pending PCT with an examiner's written opinion, not a granted, enforceable right. The moat is confirmed absent. [Cited: F-1] **High.**

Put together: the technology LiT sells is, on the record available, substantially VTIP's; LiT's contractual route to a sublicensable position is an unexercised, time-boxed, royalty-bearing option that may already be narrowed by a prior MRC field license; and the one stage clearly attributed to LiT is the chemical refinement method, not the separation core. A licensor that cannot sublicense cannot grant a whole-mine royalty. On this reading the licensing model has no asset under it until the rights are secured, which is why DISSENT reserved kill-switch standing.

### Challenge — the case that this is a gating condition, not a terminal flaw

The strongest rebuttal does not deny the facts; it contests their finality.

- **The $52K study is not LiT's whole IP position.** The executed agreement is a three-month conceptual-and-preconcentration engagement, not the company's patent estate. F-1 itself confirms a PCT is pending. The pending PCT, not the sponsored-research contract, is LiT's asserted ownership vehicle, and its claim scope is simply not retrievable yet (U-4). Absence of a public LiT filing is consistent with a filing under 18 months old. [Cited: F-1; `r00-ip-fto.md`] **Medium-High that absence is not proof of non-existence.**
- **At least the chemical stage is asserted as LiT's own background property.** Art. 13.9(a) names the flexible-feedstock, non-acidic reduced-temperature leaching method as the *Sponsor's* background IP, used by Virginia Tech only nonexclusively and without compensation. So the contract itself treats the chemical refinement method as LiT-side property, not VTIP's. The ownership problem is concentrated on the *separation* stage, not the whole platform. [Cited: VT Agreement Art. 13.9(a)] **High that the chemical method is contract-named as Sponsor background IP.**
- **The defect is unconfirmed, not confirmed-absent.** Every load-bearing negative here rests on an unretrieved item: whether LiT elected and signed the exclusive license (U-1/U-4) and whether MRC's license forecloses the field (U-2). These are private legal documents, not public facts. Per the evidence-lock handling rules, they sit at Assumption, which cuts both ways: the rights are not established present, but neither are they established absent.
- **The remedy is bounded, known, and exactly what seed capital funds.** Securing an exclusive, sublicensable, field-of-use license from VTIP; obtaining a freedom-to-operate opinion on the VTIP/MRC carve-out; and confirming or negotiating around the MRC field are transaction and diligence tasks with named counterparties, not open scientific problems. They are gateable. [Cited: `r00-ip-fto.md`; VT Agreement Art. 13.3-13.5] **Moderate-High that the path is executable if pursued.**

DISSENT's kill-switch standing is therefore **UPHELD**, scoped as conditional: the rights gap is decision-determining and, until U-1 resolves favorably (an executed exclusive, sublicensable, field-of-use license from VTIP that survives MRC's prior rights), no strategy option may assume LiT can grant a whole-mine, sublicensed royalty.

## Cluster 2 — Recovery uplift: is the number that drives the revenue real, and does it sit on the royalty base?

**Candidates in cluster:** CRU-W1 (the 16-to-19 percent recovery improvement is undefined and unvalidated, a single-point dependency); ROY-W2 (the "1 cent for 16-to-19 cents" value-exchange holds only if the uplift and the royalty share the same base, and the uplift magnitude is itself unvalidated); DIS-W2 (load-bearing, kill-switch standing reserved: the single most important performance number is undefined and the whole revenue model rests on it). Adjacent and cross-linked, partially merged below: CLA-W1 (per-site tailings grade uncharacterized, so the input the uplift is computed against is unknown).

This is the second of the two load-bearing clusters, and it is independent of the first. W-RIGHTS asks whether LiT may legally sell its process. This cluster asks whether the process, once sellable, delivers the performance the price was set against. A favorable answer here does nothing to cure the rights gap, and a favorable answer there does nothing to cure a shortfall in the uplift. The two are independent single points of failure and must not be netted against each other.

### The merged question

The four candidates collapse to one question in three parts:

1. **Existence and magnitude.** Is there a defined, validated recovery improvement for any named LiT feedstock, and is it the 16-to-19 figure the model uses? (CRU-W1, DIS-W2)
2. **Base congruence.** Even if the uplift is real, does it apply to the same value base the royalty is charged on, so that the "1 cent for 16-to-19 cents" exchange actually holds? (ROY-W2)
3. **Input characterization.** Can the uplift even be valued at a given site when the residual grade, mineralogy, and particle-size distribution that determine recoverable value per impoundment are uncharacterized? (CLA-W1)

### Steelman — the case that this is do-not-proceed-relevant as framed

The strongest version is built from the sponsor stipulation and the commissioned research, not from inference.

- **F-2 settles existence as an open question, not a capability.** The sponsor stipulation establishes as fact that the recovery uplift is an unvalidated assumption and that validation is the explicit use of seed funds, not a present capability. The headline number is therefore not a measured result the company holds; it is the thing the raise exists to test. [Cited: F-2] **High.**
- **The number is undefined as well as unvalidated.** There is no stated baseline, no feedstock-specific result, and no statement of whether 16-to-19 is percentage points or a relative gain. A figure that cannot even be expressed against a named baseline cannot be audited, defended to a counterparty, or modeled with any precision. [Cited: F-2; CRU-W1] **High.**
- **The entire revenue architecture and the value-exchange pitch sit on this one number.** The royalty is sized to the uplift, the 27x-34x return depends on the royalty holding, and the "1 cent for 16-to-19 cents" sales argument is the uplift restated. A single unvalidated input carries the whole model, and it does not degrade gracefully: if validation lands materially below the assumed figure, the economics do not hold. [Cited: DIS-W2; F-2; cross-link CAP-W3/F-3] **High.**
- **The literature pins no number for any LiT feedstock.** The commissioned research confirms only direction: fine and ultrafine and mica-hosted lithium is a documented structural loss in conventional spodumene flowsheets, and REE residual value in tailings is real but heterogeneous. No published average validates LiT's specific uplift for any feedstock. The corroboration is of headroom, not of capture. [Cited: `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **High that direction is supported and magnitude is not.**
- **Even a validated uplift may not rescue the model, because of the base mismatch.** The value-exchange holds only if the uplift and the royalty share the same base. If the uplift applies to a recovered sub-stream while the 1 percent royalty applies to the operator's whole-mine NSR, the "1 cent for 16-to-19 cents" equivalence is false even when the uplift number is real. The technical success case and the commercial success case are not the same case. [Cited: ROY-W2; Business Plan Fee Structure] **High that the equivalence is unproven.**
- **The input cannot be valued site-by-site.** Tailings value depends on residual grade, mineralogy, tonnage, and particle-size distribution per impoundment, none of which is characterized anywhere in the base. An uplift percentage applied to an uncharacterized feedstock produces an unfounded dollar figure regardless of how well the percentage is validated in a lab. [Cited: CLA-W1; `r00-tailings-grades.md`; U-13] **High that site value is uncharacterized.**

Put together: the number that drives every revenue line is undefined, unvalidated, corroborated only in direction, possibly charged against the wrong base, and applied to feedstock whose value is uncharacterized. On this reading the revenue model has no measured foundation under it until the uplift is defined, validated against a named baseline and feedstock, and reconciled to the royalty base, which is why DISSENT reserved kill-switch standing.

### Challenge — the case that this is a validation gate, not a terminal flaw

The strongest rebuttal accepts every fact above and contests only what they imply for proceeding.

- **This is LiT's own work to do, not a third party's right to grant.** Unlike the rights gap, where the curative event is an instrument controlled by VTIP and constrained by a prior MRC license, the uplift is validated by LiT's own bench-and-pilot program. It is the normal pre-seed-to-validated-pilot technical risk every deep-tech company carries, and it is exactly what the seed round is structured to retire. [Cited: F-2; `r00-pilot-benchmarks.md`] **Moderate-High that the work is in LiT's control.**
- **Direction is corroborated by independent literature, so the prior is not speculative.** Fine and ultrafine and mica-hosted lithium is lost in conventional flotation as a structural matter (one DMS example loses ~8 percent of lithium before flotation begins), the underlying separation science is demonstrated at commercial scale (HHS at 2.8 TPH), and a low-temperature acid-free route class is reproducible in principle (a documented ~99 percent Li recovery on a 325C NaOH roast). The headroom the uplift aims at demonstrably exists; the open question is how much of it LiT captures. [Cited: `r00-tailings-grades.md`; `r00-ip-fto.md`; `r00-tech-reproducibility.md`] **Moderate-High that headroom exists.**
- **This is a magnitude risk, not an existence risk.** The probability the validated uplift is exactly zero is low given the corroborated losses; the live risk is that it lands below the commercial threshold the royalty math needs. That is a quantifiable, testable proposition with a defined pass/fail, not an open-ended scientific unknown. [Cited: `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **Moderate.**
- **The remedy is bounded, measurable, and gateable.** Define a baseline, run feedstock-specific bench then continuous-pilot validation, and report the uplift in percentage points against the named baseline on a named feedstock, then reconcile that result to the royalty base before any deal is priced. These are scoped milestones with a clear KPI, not unbounded research. The base-mismatch problem in particular is a pricing-design choice LiT controls, not a law of nature: pricing on incremental recovered value instead of whole-mine NSR makes the uplift and the royalty share a base by construction. [Cited: `r00-pilot-benchmarks.md`; `r00-license-structures.md`] **Moderate-High that the path is executable.**

## Cluster 3 — Royalty base: is a whole-mine NSR royalty the right instrument, and does it survive a sophisticated counterparty?

**Candidates in cluster:** ROY-W1 (a 1 percent royalty on the operator's total-mine net smelter return for a partial-stream process improvement has no located precedent and is structurally mismatched; counterparties will reprice); DIS-W4 (the royalty design is commercially aggressive and unprecedented at its base; a sophisticated counterparty reprices it to incremental value, collapsing the headline economics); ROY-W2 base-congruence leg (routed here from Cluster 2: the "1 cent for 16-to-19 cents" value-exchange holds only if the uplift and the royalty are charged on the same base). Cross-linked, resolved elsewhere: W-UPLIFT (the uplift-validation half of ROY-W2); ROY-W4 (no published comparable rate to anchor to, routed to the royalty-benchmarking cluster); CAP-W3/DIS-W6 (the whole-mine base is one of the three stacked assumptions behind the 27x-34x return, routed to the model-integrity cluster).

This cluster sits one layer below the two load-bearing clusters. W-RIGHTS asks whether LiT may legally sell the process; W-UPLIFT asks whether the process performs as priced. This cluster asks a third, distinct question: granting that LiT can sell a process that works, is the *instrument it proposes to charge on* the right one, and does it survive contact with the mining counterparties who will actually sign it. Unlike the first two clusters, the defect here is not a fact outside LiT's control (a third-party license) or a pending measurement (the validation result). It is a pricing-design choice LiT made and can remake. That difference governs the adjudication.

### The merged question

The three candidates collapse to one question in two parts:

1. **Instrument fit.** Is a whole-mine NSR royalty a precedented, defensible base for a partial-stream recovery improvement, or is it structurally mismatched to how this value is actually priced and therefore exposed to repricing by the operator? (ROY-W1, DIS-W4)
2. **Base congruence with the value pitch.** Does the royalty base coincide with the base the recovery uplift is measured on, so that the "1 cent for 16-to-19 cents" value-exchange is internally true rather than a comparison across two different denominators? (ROY-W2 base-congruence leg, DIS-W4 second leg)

These are two faces of one structural choice. A whole-mine NSR base is simultaneously the instrument most likely to be repriced *and* the instrument that breaks base congruence, because the uplift is generated on a recovered sub-stream while the NSR spans the operator's entire output. Choosing the base resolves both at once.

### Steelman — the case that this is a serious, value-determining weakness

The strongest version is built from the commissioned research and the company's own fee structure, not from inference.

- **No precedent exists for the proposed instrument.** The commissioned research returned a clear, consistent answer that runs against the dossier's framing: real process-technology and mineral-processing licenses are paid on the value the technology itself adds, never on the whole mine. The standard shape is an upfront fee plus a running royalty tied to throughput processed, metal recovered, or a discrete recovered stream. NSR royalties, by contrast, are an instrument of mineral-rights ownership and project finance (granted to the holder of the ground, to finders, to acquirers as deferred purchase price, or to JV and streaming financiers), not of process licensing. No precedent was located for a whole-mine NSR royalty granted in exchange for a partial-stream process improvement. [Cited: `r00-license-structures.md`] **High that the instrument is unprecedented for this use.**
- **The closest live comparator prices on the opposite base.** Jetti Resources, the single nearest analog (a bolt-on catalyst that lifts recovery at operating mines, the same archetype as LiT), is explicitly a value-sharing model: licensing fees plus royalties on *incremental copper recovered* plus service fees. Its royalty base is the metal the technology unlocks, not the mine's total output. The most useful precedent LiT has prices on exactly the base ROY-W1 says LiT should be using and is not. [Cited: `r00-license-structures.md`] **High.**
- **A sophisticated counterparty will reprice it.** The operators LiT must sign are the world's largest miners, advised by people who price royalties for a living and who will not pay a whole-mine NSR for a sub-stream gain when every comparable instrument is struck on incremental value. The whole-mine base is therefore best read as an aggressive opening anchor with no precedent behind it, not as a base the model can bank. The dossier banks it. [Cited: `r00-license-structures.md`; Business Plan Fee Structure] **High that the base is exposed to repricing.**
- **The base mismatch breaks the value-exchange pitch independently of the uplift number.** The "1 cent for 16-to-19 cents" argument compares a royalty charged on one base (whole-mine NSR) against an uplift generated on another (a recovered sub-stream). Even with a fully validated uplift, the equivalence is a comparison across two different denominators and does not hold. The technical success case and the commercial success case are not the same case, and the pitch conflates them. [Cited: Business Plan Fee Structure; ROY-W2; DIS-W4] **High that the asserted equivalence is unproven as constructed.**
- **The defect propagates into the headline return.** The whole-mine NSR base is one of the three stacked assumptions (with 10 percent penetration and a major-operator win) behind the 27x-34x return. Re-basing to incremental value compresses the modeled royalty income on every line, so the return as published is contingent on the unprecedented base holding. [Cited: CAP-W3; Business Plan Exit; F-3] **High that the return is contingent on the base.**

Put together: the central commercial mechanic is an instrument with no located precedent, priced on a base the nearest comparator explicitly does not use, exposed to repricing by the only counterparties who can sign it, and constructed so that its value-exchange pitch compares two non-congruent bases. On this reading the modeled royalty income is an aggressive anchor presented as a base case.

### Challenge — the case that this is a curable design choice, not a defect in the business

## Cluster 4 — Price exposure: the royalty base is fully indexed to a volatile commodity basket

**Candidates in cluster:** ROY-T1 (royalty income is fully indexed to commodity price by construction, because NSR is realized metal value, so the base carries full price-downside exposure that compounds with the other aggressive revenue inputs); SPO-T1 (commodity-price volatility is a first-order threat, demonstrated in real time by the H1-2026 round-trip in both baskets); SPO-T2 (lithium upside is structurally capped by fast swing-supply restarts, so the exposure is asymmetric); CHO-T1 (China's pricing power lets a hostile actor suppress the basket and compress the royalty regardless of LiT's technical success); DIS-T1 (the red-team twin of ROY-T1: the entire revenue base carries full price-downside exposure that compounds with the other aggressive inputs). This is the first Threat cluster to reach convergence, and it differs in kind from the three weakness clusters before it. W-RIGHTS is an external legal foreclosure; W-UPLIFT is a pending internal measurement; W-BASE is a self-curable pricing-design choice. This cluster is none of those. It is inherent market exposure: the revenue instrument is denominated in the realized value of a commodity basket, so its value moves with prices LiT does not set and cannot control. The defect is not in the science, the rights, or the contract drafting. It is in the asset class. That character governs the adjudication, because a threat that is structural to the instrument cannot be cured the way a design flaw can; it can only be mitigated, hedged, or modeled honestly.

### The merged question

The five candidates collapse to one question in three parts:

1. **Magnitude and timing.** Is commodity-price volatility a first-order, demonstrated threat to the royalty rather than background noise, and at what point in LiT's timeline does it actually bite? (SPO-T1, ROY-T1, DIS-T1)
2. **Asymmetry.** Is the exposure symmetric, so that bull years average out bear years, or is the downside full while the upside is structurally capped? (SPO-T2)
3. **Adversarial control.** Is the downside merely cyclical, or can a hostile actor with pricing power deliberately drive it against LiT, and how does that square with the strategic case that the same dominance is LiT's tailwind? (CHO-T1, against CHO-O2)

### Steelman — the case that this is a serious, structural threat to the revenue base

The strongest version is built from the sponsor stipulation and the commissioned price research, not from inference.

- **F-3a establishes the exposure as fact, not contention.** The sponsor stipulation corrects every earlier NSR reading: the royalty is fully indexed to commodity prices, so the concern is the full commodity-price exposure of the royalty base, not a model that ignores price. - **The volatility is first-order and was demonstrated in real time during this deliberation's own window.** In the first half of 2026 lithium ran to two-year highs near US$26,000/t and then moderated to roughly US$11,000/t LCE by mid-year, near its marginal-cost floor; NdPr rallied about 160 percent off its January low, peaked at end-April near US$137-140/kg, and gave back about a third to roughly US$90/kg by 1 June. On this reading, any royalty figure booked at a point estimate is fragile, and the headline economics must be shown at a trough basket, not a mid-cycle spot.

### Challenge — the case that the tailwind validates the category but largely bypasses LiT's revenue

The transmission from a unit-level floor to LiT's own royalty revenue is an Assumption, not a Cited fact, and the research tags it exactly that way. ## Cluster 8 — Feedstock characterization: is there a known resource under the recovery claim, and does feedstock heterogeneity break a one-size process pitch?

**Candidates in cluster:** CLA-W1 (the variable that decides whether any impoundment is worth treating, residual grade, mineralogy, tonnage, and particle-size distribution per site, is uncharacterized anywhere in the base; "decades of accumulated value" is supported only at the macro inventory level, never site-specifically); CLA-W2 (the only contracted test work touched lithium concentrates from mine and aggregate tailings around Virginia targeting a greater-than-6% Li2O concentrate; no REE ore body and no REE tailings work appears in the base, so the REE and multi-feedstock geological basis is unevidenced); CLA-W3 (the $229M per-operator and $2.29B major-operator "total mine production value" figures that drive the royalty have no per-site geological basis shown); CLA-T1 (heterogeneity is itself a threat: REE abundance in tailings varies by source ore, processing era, and weathering with no representative grade, so a feedstock-agnostic process claim collides with per-site reality and forces costly per-site characterization before each deal). Cross-linked, resolved elsewhere: CLA-S1 (the category-level value premise, routed to the remaining-strengths pass); CLA-O1 (a grade-led site-selection strategy has real, site-dependent upside, the bull twin of CLA-T1, routed to the market-category opportunity); W-UPLIFT (the uplift cannot be valued per site without characterization); W-MODEL (CLA-W3 feeds the headline revenue inputs); the liability cluster (per-site sampling is where CERCLA exposure attaches); CAP-W4 (the pilot budget that must fund characterization).

This cluster sits beneath the revenue and execution layers and underneath W-UPLIFT specifically. W-UPLIFT asks whether the recovery improvement is real and validated as a percentage. This cluster asks the prior question: a percentage of what. An uplift expressed against a named feedstock still produces no dollar figure until the residual grade, mineralogy, tonnage, and particle-size distribution of the impoundment it is applied to are known, and that input is uncharacterized everywhere in the base. The defect is not a third-party right, a pending lab result, or a market exposure. It is a missing resource foundation under the revenue claim and a structural property of the feedstock that resists a one-size pitch. The first part is a curable diligence-and-protocol gap in LiT's control; the second is an external characteristic of the world's tailings that cannot be cured, only turned to advantage by site selection or absorbed as a per-deal cost. That dual character splits the cluster into a master weakness and a companion threat.

### The merged question

The four candidates collapse to one question in three parts:

1. **Resource foundation.** Is there any site-specific characterization (grade, mineralogy, tonnage, particle-size distribution) under the recovery claim, or is "decades of accumulated value" supported only at the global-inventory level? (CLA-W1)
2. **Multi-feedstock basis.** Does the geological evidence extend to the REE and multi-feedstock story the model sells, or is it confined to a single lithium-concentrate test program around Virginia? (CLA-W2)
3. **Heterogeneity.** Does feedstock heterogeneity, with no representative grade across sites, break the feedstock-agnostic process pitch and force costly per-site characterization before every deal, and does the same heterogeneity also create a grade-led site-selection upside? (CLA-T1, against CLA-O1; with CLA-W3 as the financial symptom)

### Steelman — the case that the recovery claim has no characterized resource under it

The strongest version is built from the company's own documents and the commissioned grades research, not from inference.

- **The decisive variable is uncharacterized everywhere.** Whether any impoundment is worth treating depends entirely on residual grade, mineralogy, tonnage, and particle-size distribution per site, and none of these is established anywhere in the base. The commissioned research is explicit that tailings grades are highly heterogeneous by site, ore, era, and process, that REE abundance in particular has no single representative grade, and that per-impoundment characterization requires site sampling or an operator's characterization report that no general source provides. "Decades of accumulated value" is real only at the category scale (the global inventory of roughly 8,500 tailings facilities and 217 billion cubic meters, the 140,000-plus US abandoned hardrock sites), never at any site LiT would actually address. [Cited: CLA-W1; `r00-tailings-grades.md`; U-13] **High that site value is uncharacterized.**
- **The geological evidence covers one feedstock, not the multi-feedstock story.** The only contracted test work in the base touched lithium concentrates from mine and aggregate tailings in and around Virginia, targeting a greater-than-6% Li2O concentrate grade. No REE ore body and no REE tailings test work appears anywhere. The REE leg of the pitch, and the broader feedstock-agnostic claim, therefore has no demonstrated geological basis in LiT's own work; the REE value is supported only by third-party literature about other people's deposits. [Cited: CLA-W2; VT Agreement Attachment A Task 2; `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **High.**
- **The headline revenue figures float free of any resource.** The $229M per-operator and $2.29B major-operator "total mine production value" figures that the royalty is struck against are presented with no per-site geological foundation. They are model inputs without a resource under them, which means the revenue line inherits the characterization gap directly: a royalty percentage on a production-value figure that has no assayed basis is an assumption multiplied by an assumption. [Cited: CLA-W3; Business Plan Financial Plan; basis Unsupported] **High that the basis is absent.**
- **Heterogeneity actively undercuts the feedstock-agnostic pitch.** The selling premise is a process that works across feedstocks. But because REE and lithium residuals vary by source ore, processing era, and weathering with no representative grade, a single-process claim collides with per-site reality. Every deal requires its own characterization before the recovery and the royalty can be sized, which is a real cost and a real delay that the feedstock-agnostic story conceals. The heterogeneity is not background noise; it is the reason the one-size claim cannot be banked. [Cited: CLA-T1; `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **Moderate-High.**

Put together: the recovery claim rests on a resource that is uncharacterized at every site, evidenced for only one feedstock, and translated into headline figures with no geological basis, while the heterogeneity of the feedstock means no characterization at one site transfers to the next. On this reading the value premise is a category-level truth dressed as a site-level one.

### Challenge — the case that this is a screening-and-diligence discipline, not a defect in the business

The strongest rebuttal accepts every fact above and contests only what they imply for proceeding.

- **The category headroom is genuinely established, only the site sizing is not.** The commissioned research confirms in direction that the value exists: fine, ultrafine, and mica-hosted lithium is a structural loss in conventional flowsheets, REE residuals in tailings are real, and the aggregate framing (recovering roughly 90% of existing mining byproducts could supply almost all US critical-mineral needs) is Cited at category scale. The gap is not whether value is out there; it is which impoundments carry it. That is a screening problem, not an existence problem. [Cited: `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **Moderate-High that category headroom exists.**
- **Heterogeneity cuts in LiT's favor as much as against it.** The same variation that breaks a blind feedstock-agnostic claim is exactly what makes a grade-led site-selection strategy valuable: if grades vary widely with no representative average, then high-grading the best impoundments is a real and defensible edge, and the federal tailings-recovery data layer (the Interior and USGS program in O-POLICY) plus operator characterization reports give LiT a screening input. Heterogeneity is a reason to select sites, not a reason the business cannot run. [Cited: CLA-O1; `r00-tailings-grades.md`; cross-link O-POLICY] **Moderate-High that selection converts the threat to an edge.**
- **The cure is ordinary diligence the licensing model already requires.** Characterizing a site before pricing a deal is standard practice in mineral processing, and LiT must in any case run a site-specific pilot to validate the uplift (W-UPLIFT). The characterization step is therefore not an added burden but the front end of the validation it already plans, and it is gateable: no deal is priced until the target impoundment is assayed and its recoverable value sized. The figure that matters is per deal, sized against an assay, not a global average. [Cited: `r00-tailings-grades.md`; `r00-pilot-benchmarks.md`] **Moderate-High that the remedy is conventional and in LiT's control.**
- **The defect degrades precision, not viability.** A missing resource foundation makes the headline figures unbankable as presented and forces a per-site discipline, but it does not make the model impossible; it makes the model's revenue figures placeholders until each site is characterized. DISSENT reserved no kill-switch standing here. [Cited: CLA-W3; `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **Moderate-High that it is non-fatal.**

Both sides agree on the facts: per-site grade is uncharacterized, the geological evidence covers only lithium concentrate around Virginia, the headline figures have no resource basis, and the feedstock is heterogeneous with no representative grade. ## Cluster 9 — Liability and title: does the abandoned-site pitch carry an environmental and ownership exposure the model underweights, and can LiT control it?

**Candidates in cluster:** WAT-T1 (CERCLA disturbance exposure: an entity that disturbs a contaminated site can be pulled in as a potentially responsible party for the whole site, and the US retains title to unpatented mining claims, so recovered-product ownership at orphaned sites is not automatic); WAT-T2 (the 2024 Good Samaritan Act offers only a narrow, capped pathway and a profit-seeking reprocessor may not cleanly qualify); WAT-W1 (specific environmental claims, "no acidic byproducts" and abandoned-site remediation economics, are technical and commercial assertions not yet evidenced in the base; the ESG story outruns its proof); DIS-T3 (red-team articulation: the abandoned-tailings path exposes LiT to CERCLA PRP liability and unclear product title, the Good Samaritan pathway likely does not cover a profit-seeking reprocessor, and so the most distinctive part of the pitch, orphaned-site remediation, is the most legally fragile). Coupled and resolved elsewhere: W-GRADE (the per-site sampling its cure requires is the same disturbance that attaches CERCLA exposure on contaminated impoundments); O-POLICY (the Good Samaritan reform and EMRTAI are a leg of the policy tailwind, with transmission to LiT already downgraded to Assumption there); WAT-T3 (policy benefits flow to scale producers; routed to the go-to-market threat pass, not resolved here).

This cluster sits beside the feedstock-characterization cluster and shares its physical fact base: both attach at the moment someone disturbs an impoundment. Where W-GRADE asks whether there is characterized value in the tailings, this cluster asks who carries the environmental liability and who owns the recovered product once that value is disturbed and pulled out. The distinctive feature of the cluster, and the one that governs the adjudication, is that the severity of the exposure is not fixed by nature; it is set by an operating-model choice LiT controls, namely whether LiT itself takes possession of and operates at orphaned sites, or licenses its process to the operator or owner who already holds the title and the liability.

### The merged question

The four candidates collapse to one question in three parts:

1. **Environmental liability.** Does disturbing a contaminated impoundment to recover minerals expose the disturbing party to CERCLA responsible-party liability for the whole site, even when it did not create the contamination and is there to clean up? (WAT-T1 first leg, DIS-T3 first leg)
2. **Product title.** At an orphaned or abandoned site, particularly one on federal land under an unpatented claim, is ownership of the recovered product automatic, or does it turn on land title, claim status, surviving operator interest, and permit terms? (WAT-T1 second leg, DIS-T3 second leg)
3. **Permit cover and claim proof.** Does the 2024 Good Samaritan reform give a commercial reprocessor a usable liability shield, and are LiT's distinctive environmental claims ("no acidic byproducts," remediation economics) evidenced? (WAT-T2, DIS-T3 third leg, WAT-W1)

### Steelman — the case that this is a serious, model-shaping exposure

The strongest version is built from the commissioned research, not from inference.

- **CERCLA pulls in the party that disturbs the site, not only the party that polluted it.** Under CERCLA, an entity that disturbs a contaminated site can become a responsible party for all contamination at that location, even if it did not cause the pollution and is attempting to improve conditions, and the potential remediation cost can exceed the recovery revenue, which has a documented chilling effect on private legacy-site projects. The exposure is to the whole site, not to the increment LiT touches. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md` (EPA/ITRC)] **High on the framework.**
- **Bare title is enough for owner liability, which means orphaned sites are not unowned.** The Tenth Circuit held the United States liable as a CERCLA "owner" for releases on unpatented mining claims because it held bare legal title, "irrespective of any additional indicia of ownership." Under the General Mining Law of 1872 the US retains legal title to land worked under unpatented claims, so tailings deposited there sit on land the government never relinquished. At an orphaned site, who owns the recovered product is therefore not automatic; it can turn on land title, claim status, any surviving operator or successor interest, and the permit terms. A revenue model that assumes LiT or its partner simply takes and sells the recovered units underweights this. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md` (Jackson Kelly 10th Cir.; Colorado Lawyer)] **High that title and product ownership are not automatic at orphaned sites.**
- **The Good Samaritan reform is narrow, capped, and probably not built for a commercial reprocessor.** The Good Samaritan Remediation of Abandoned Hardrock Mines Act was signed December 17, 2024, but it directs EPA to run a pilot of up to 15 permits, terminating after seven years, and defines a "Good Samaritan" as a party that is not a past or current owner or operator of the site, had no role in creating the waste, and is not otherwise potentially liable. A business whose primary purpose is mineral revenue, and which by reprocessing disturbs the waste, sits in tension with both the eligibility test and the CERCLA-disturbance trap; permits also require demonstrated financial resources or third-party financial assurance. "LiT operates at abandoned sites under Good Samaritan cover" is therefore Assumption (low-to-moderate confidence), legally untested for a commercial reprocessor. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md` (Congress.gov S.2781; EPA; Womble Bond Dickinson; Fed. Reg. 2026-04386)] **Moderate that the pathway is usable for LiT; High that it is narrow and capped.**
- **The most distinctive part of the pitch is the most legally fragile.** Orphaned-site remediation is what differentiates LiT's ESG story (>140,000 abandoned hardrock sites, ~22,500 seriously hazardous), and it is precisely the path that lands LiT in the CERCLA-disturbance trap, the uncertain-title problem, and the untested Good Samaritan eligibility question at once. The breadth that makes the pitch compelling is the breadth that makes it fragile. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md`; DIS-T3] **Moderate-High.**
- **The environmental claims are stated ahead of their proof.** "No acidic byproducts" is a process-chemistry assertion and the abandoned-site remediation economics are a commercial assertion; neither is evidenced in the base as to magnitude. The acid-free direction is corroborated only in principle by the reproducibility research, not validated for any LiT feedstock, the same direction-only posture as the recovery uplift. An ESG and remediation story carried into a deck or a term sheet ahead of its evidence is a credibility exposure with a counterparty and a disclosure exposure with a regulator. [Cited: Prospectus; `r00-tech-reproducibility.md`; cross-link F-2] **Moderate-High that the claims outrun the evidence.**

Put together: the abandoned-site path carries a whole-site environmental liability that attaches on disturbance, a product-title question that is not resolved by simply recovering the metal, a permit shield that is narrow and probably not built for a profit-seeking reprocessor, and a set of environmental claims stated ahead of their proof. On this reading the most distinctive part of the pitch is the most legally exposed, and the model underweights all of it.

### Challenge — the case that LiT controls the exposure through its operating model

The strongest rebuttal accepts every fact above and contests only how much of the exposure LiT must actually carry.

- **The licensing model routes the liability to the party that already holds it.** The same research that establishes the CERCLA trap also draws the strategic conclusion: the liability framework favors LiT licensing its process to the operator or owner that already holds the title and the liability, rather than LiT taking possession of orphaned tailings itself. This aligns with the whole-mine licensing model that is LiT's actual revenue architecture and keeps LiT from becoming a CERCLA PRP. The exposure is highest on a path (LiT self-operates orphaned sites) that LiT does not need to take to have a business. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md`] **High that licensing to the title holder avoids PRP status.**
- **Active operating tailings, not orphaned sites, are the lower-risk near-term channel.** Where the operator is live and holds title and ongoing liability, LiT licenses a process improvement and the operator carries the environmental and title questions it already carries. The orphaned-site universe is the headline-expanding part of the addressable market, not the part LiT must serve first. Sequencing operator-held active sites ahead of orphaned sites mitigates the exposure by site selection, the same lever that mitigates the heterogeneity threat. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md`; cross-link T-GRADE] **Moderate-High that an active-site-first sequence lowers exposure.**
- **The policy door is opening on recover-during-remediation, even if narrowly.** The Good Samaritan reform explicitly contemplates reclaiming valuable minerals during remediation, and EPA's EMRTAI initiative assesses critical-mineral recovery from mine waste during cleanup. The pathway is narrow, but the direction of policy is toward, not against, recovery at legacy sites, which over the seven-year pilot and any successor program is a tailwind for an operator-partner that does qualify. LiT's role can be the process licensor to a qualifying remediator rather than the remediator of record. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md` (WRI; EPA EMRTAI)] **Moderate that the policy direction favors the licensed-process role.**
- **The remedy is diligence and structure, not an open problem.** Per-site title and liability due diligence (patented versus unpatented, claim status, surviving operator or successor, NPL status), an environmental-counsel opinion on Good Samaritan eligibility and CERCLA disturbance for LiT's specific model, and product-title confirmation as a deal precondition are bounded, named tasks. They are gateable: a liability gate before any LiT-operated site and a title-confirmation precondition before any deal are execution controls LiT writes into its own process. [Cited: `r00-tailings-liability.md`] **Moderate-High that the path is executable.**

### Adjudication

Both sides agree on the framework facts: CERCLA attaches on disturbance to the whole site, bare title makes orphaned-site product ownership non-automatic, and the Good Samaritan pathway is narrow and untested for a commercial reprocessor. They disagree only on how much of that exposure LiT must carry, and that is governed by an operating-model choice LiT controls. The existence of the threat is therefore **CONFIRMED** on Cited evidence (the framework is High; the application to LiT's specific model is Assumption, Moderate). DISSENT did not reserve kill-switch standing on DIS-T3 and none is created here; the threat does not behave like a single point of failure because its severity collapses on the licensing path LiT is already pursuing.

This makes T-LIABILITY a conditional, operating-model-dependent threat rather than a structural one. On the self-operate-orphaned-sites path it is High and concentrated; on the license-to-the-title-holder path, with an active-site-first sequence, it is largely avoided and reduced to diligence and deal-precondition controls. The threat is real, mitigable, and not curable to zero (the orphaned-site optionality cannot be both retained and de-risked without counsel sign-off), and its primary mitigation is a strategy decision routed to Round 03: make license-to-the-title-holder the default operating model and gate any LiT-operated orphaned-site work behind an explicit liability and eligibility opinion. WAT-W1 is a companion weakness of a different character (claims ahead of evidence) and is split below.

This produces one master finding, per-item dispositions, and risk-register openings.

## Cluster 10 — Royalty benchmarking: can LiT anchor the rate it charges on the base it chooses?

**Candidates in cluster:** ROY-W4 (LiT cannot anchor its running-royalty rate to a published comparable; exact terms for the closest deals (Jetti, NovaCell, Jameson) are confidential, so any specific rate it cites is Assumption pending primary benchmarking). Cross-linked, resolved elsewhere: W-BASE (Cluster 3, the *base* the rate is charged on); W-UPLIFT (Cluster 2, the validated value the rate is sized against); W-MODEL (Cluster 6, the stacked-return integrity the rate feeds). Adjacent: ROY-S1, ROY-S2 (the channel and the structures are precedented; carried to the remaining-strengths pass).

This cluster closes the royalty triad. Cluster 3 resolved the *base* (whole-mine NSR is unprecedented; re-base to incremental recovered value or a discrete stream). Cluster 2 resolved the *value* the price is set against (the uplift is unvalidated and must clear a gate). This cluster resolves the third and last royalty variable: the *rate* itself, the percentage LiT writes on whichever base it adopts. It is the narrowest of the three and the least dangerous, but it is distinct, because re-basing the royalty to a precedented base does not by itself tell LiT what number to put on that base, and the deals that would tell it are private.

### The merged question

ROY-W4 reduces to one question in two parts:

1. **Anchorability.** Can LiT cite a market-validated running-royalty rate for its archetype, or are the only comparable rates confidential, leaving any specific percentage it quotes as an internally chosen Assumption rather than a benchmarked figure? (the core of ROY-W4)
2. **Consequence.** If the rate is unanchorable from public data, what does that actually put at risk, given that the base is being re-based (Cluster 3) and the value is being validated (Cluster 2) on their own tracks?

### Steelman — the case that this is a real, distinct gap

The strongest version is built from the commissioned research, which is explicit on the point.

- **The closest comparable rates are confidential, by name.** The research that establishes the licensing channel also establishes that the exact economics of every nearest deal are not public: Jetti's percentage of incremental copper recovered, its upfront, and its service fee are "not public"; NovaCell (Jord) and Jameson Cell (Glencore Technology) commercial terms are confidential. The single most useful precedent for LiT confirms the *structure* and withholds the *rate*. [Cited: `r00-license-structures.md` (c); Sources 1, 4, 5] **High that the comparable rates are not publicly observable.**
- **The public anchors that do exist are illustrative, not archetype-matched.** What the record yields is a 5 percent gross-revenue royalty on a recovered tailings stream, a representative recovery-technology license at a $500,000 upfront plus 5 percent royalty, and a generic "low-single-digit-to-5 percent" running-royalty band. These are real, but the research itself tags the numeric anchors as illustrative or Medium and reserves High confidence only for the *structure*. None is a like-for-like mineral-processing catalyst deal struck on incremental recovered metal. [Cited: `r00-license-structures.md` (a), (c)] **High that only illustrative, non-archetype anchors are public.**

Put together: the rate LiT will charge cannot be anchored to any public comparable for its own archetype; the only public numbers are illustrative or off-archetype; and the binding confidence rule already classifies any rate LiT quotes as an Assumption. The model's revenue lines are multiplied by a number the company chose, not one the market validated.

The strongest rebuttal accepts that the comparable rates are private and contests only how much that matters.

LiT cannot cite "Jetti charges X," but it can price inside a Cited band rather than in a vacuum. The gap is the precision of the anchor, not the absence of one. [Cited: `r00-license-structures.md` (c) Jetti value-sharing; cross-link W-UPLIFT] **Moderate-High.**

The first deal is both the cure and the evidence. [Cited: `r00-license-structures.md` Sources, NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL 3; U-5..U-7] **Moderate-High that the path is executable.**
- **The consequence is bounded by the other two cures.** With the base re-based and the headline return already being restated and stress-tested on that base (RR-6, W-MODEL), the rate uncertainty is a sensitivity inside an already-flagged range, not a fresh hole. The model integrity cluster carries the stacked-return restatement; this cluster simply names the rate as the third input that must be quoted as a range, not a point, until benchmarked. ### Adjudication

Both sides agree on the fact: the comparable rates are confidential and any specific rate LiT quotes is an Assumption, not a Cited figure. The disagreement is only about severity, and it is narrow. ## Strengths quadrant pass — which claimed strengths survive the evidence base, and what do they actually establish?

DISSENT filed no strengths, so there is no opposing slate to steelman against. The discipline that replaces it is anti-overstatement. [Cited: Market Research Sec. 3; `r00-valuation-comps.md`; DIS-W5 competitive leg, DIS-T2] **High that majors fund competitors; Moderate-High on the competitive consequence.**
- **A better-funded archetype captures more of the same value.** The best-funded tailings comparator, Phoenix Tailings, about $148M raised, runs a build-own-operate producer model that captures the full metal margin from the units it recovers, while LiT's thin royalty captures a sliver of the same value. [Cited: Market Research Sec. 3; `r00-valuation-comps.md`] **Moderate-High.**
- **Government demand-pull concentrates on scale producers, and LiT's access to the policy tailwind is mediated by operators.** The MP Materials template shows federal capital and offtake flowing to scale producers and allied national champions, not to a pre-commercial process licensor. Put together: LiT must win a slow, conservative, bespoke sale, against embedded incumbents, in an under-evaluated category, through a thin near-term channel, while the buyers fund its competitors, a better-capitalized producer archetype captures more of the same value, and the government underwrites the producers rather than LiT. On this reading the go-to-market path, not the science, is the most likely place the plan-as-written breaks on timeline.

### Challenge — the case that these are structural market conditions LiT manages by channel choice and positioning, not defects that sink the business

The strongest rebuttal accepts every fact above and contests only whether they are LiT-specific failure modes or the ordinary, navigable terrain of the category.

- **The slow sale is the category, not a LiT defect, and the proven channel discounts it.** Every entrant in this space faces the same conservative operator sale; it is priced into the archetype LiT is copying, and the same Jetti precedent that shows 3-of-23 attrition also shows that the channel does convert and does attract major-miner capital once a technology is proven at a site. The remedy is a realistic first-revenue timeline and runway sized to the actual cycle, not a different business. [Cited: `r00-adoption-deployment.md`, `r00-license-structures.md`; cross-link S-CHANNEL] **Moderate-High that the cycle is navigable with realistic timing.**
- **Channel sequencing converts the thin-pipeline and incumbency problems into a site-selection lever.** The thin new-mine channel (SPO-T3) is an argument for leading with active operating tailings at producing sites rather than greenfield licensees, which is the same active-site-first sequence that mitigates T-LIABILITY and T-GRADE. Leading where there is a live operator, a characterized stream, and an existing flowsheet to bolt onto reduces the novelty and the qualification burden simultaneously. The constrained channel narrows the target list rather than closing the market. [Cited: Market Research Sec. 1; cross-link T-LIABILITY, T-GRADE] **Moderate-High that sequencing mitigates.**
- **The competitor-funding fact cuts both ways: the funder is also the customer.** That BHP and Freeport fund Jetti and Ceibo proves the majors will both pay for and adopt bolt-on recovery technology; the same buyers are the addressable demand. LiT does not need to displace those bets everywhere, only to win on the fine, ultrafine, and mica-hosted fraction those technologies do not principally target, which is the S-SCIENCE differentiation. The competition is real but it is not a winner-take-all market, and the funders are the channel. [Cited: `r00-valuation-comps.md`; cross-link S-SCIENCE] **Moderate that differentiation carves a defensible lane.**
- **The producer-versus-licensor value-capture gap is a strategy choice, not an imposed defeat.** Phoenix's richer capture is the build-own-operate trade: more value, but eight-figure plant capital, full operating and environmental liability, and a far larger raise. LiT's licensing model deliberately trades capture for capital-lightness and liability avoidance (S-FUNDABLE, T-LIABILITY mitigation). The two are different risk-return positions, and a licensor that sells to the producer rather than competing with it turns the better-funded producer from a rival into a customer. [Cited: `r00-valuation-comps.md`; cross-link S-FUNDABLE, T-LIABILITY] **Moderate-High that the gap is a deliberate trade.**
- **The policy tailwind reaches LiT precisely by selling to the producers the government underwrites.** If federal capital and offtake flow to scale producers, the licensing model's response is to be the process inside those producers' plants, so the government money that lands on the producer pulls the producer toward LiT's technology. The transmission boundary is real, but it argues for an operator-partner go-to-market, not against the business; non-dilutive channels (DPA Title III, the consortium RFPP, WAT-O3) are accessible through a qualifying operator partner even where LiT cannot apply directly. [Cited: `r00-policy-2026.md`; cross-link O-POLICY, WAT-O3] **Moderate that the operator-partner route re-opens the tailwind.**

### Adjudication

Both sides agree on the facts: the operator sale is slow, conservative, non-standardized, and incumbency-defended; the near-term licensee channel is thin; the majors fund LiT's competitors; a better-funded producer archetype captures more of the same value; and government demand-pull concentrates on scale producers while LiT's policy access is operator-mediated. They disagree only on whether these are navigable category terrain or plan-breaking risks, and the honest answer is that they are both, and they split into two distinct master threats with different cures.

The demand-side cluster (sales cycle, novelty, incumbency, thin channel) is **CONFIRMED** as a master threat on Cited evidence at High confidence. It is structural, not curable, because the conservative non-standard operator sale is a permanent property of the market, but it is mitigable by realistic timeline assumptions and channel sequencing, and its primary danger is not that the market is closed but that the plan's compounding is timed against a first-revenue date the cycle does not support. It is therefore the same risk as the model-integrity timing inconsistency (CAP-W2) seen from the market side, and the two must be reconciled to one realistic timeline. No kill-switch standing was reserved on any of these items and none is created.

The supply-side cluster (competitor funding, value-capture asymmetry, producer-concentrated government pull) is **CONFIRMED** as a separate master threat on Cited evidence at Moderate-High confidence. It is structural and not curable to zero, because LiT cannot stop the majors funding rivals or the government favoring producers, but its severity is set by a positioning choice LiT largely controls: a licensor that sells to the producer rather than competing with it converts the better-funded rival and the government-underwritten producer from adversaries into the channel, and differentiates on the fine-particle fraction the funded competitors do not principally target. The threat is highest if LiT positions as a producer-substitute competing for the same offtake and capital, and lowest if it positions as the process inside the producers' plants. This makes it asymmetric across the same operating-model and positioning decision that governs T-LIABILITY, and it routes to the same Round 03 strategy debate.

The transmission boundary (WAT-T3, CHO-T3) is resolved here as the named hinge between O-POLICY and LiT revenue: the policy tailwind is real at the category level but reaches LiT only through operators, which is why its transmission to LiT revenue was already downgraded to Assumption in O-POLICY and why it now anchors the supply-side threat's government-capital leg. It is recorded once, here and in O-POLICY, and must not be double-counted as both a discount on the opportunity and an independent threat; it is the same boundary read from both quadrants.

This produces two master findings, per-item dispositions, and risk-register openings.

#### Master finding 1 — adoption and sales cycle

**T-ADOPT (master threat; High; structural, not curable, mitigable by timeline realism and channel sequencing; no kill-switch standing).** LiT's path to first and repeating revenue runs through a slow, conservative, non-standardized operator sale that is the binding constraint on the plan's timeline. Against all of this the Month-25 first-license assumption is aggressive, and because first revenue is the event the runway and the model's compounding depend on, a realistic cycle pushes the whole revenue schedule later than the plan shows. The mitigation is to assume a realistic multi-year qualification timeline, size runway to survive it, and lead with active operating tailings at producing sites where a live operator, a characterized stream, and an existing flowsheet reduce the novelty and qualification burden at once.
**Confidence: High that the sale is slow, conservative, incumbency-defended, and runs through a thin near-term channel (Cited). The specific Month-25 date is the company's assumption and the actual first-revenue date is unretrieved; the realistic timeline is Assumption pending LiT's own pipeline experience.**
**Fragility note:** T-ADOPT is a timeline threat, not a market-existence threat. The danger is a runway sized to the optimistic timeline meeting a sale that runs on the conservative one, so the company runs out of capital during qualification rather than failing on the merits.

#### Master finding 2 — competition and value-capture asymmetry

**T-COMPETE (master threat; Moderate-High; structural, not curable to zero, severity set by positioning; no kill-switch standing).** LiT competes for share and for the domestic-recovery narrative from the thinnest value-capture position in the field. The policy tailwind validated at the category level reaches LiT only through its operator customers, and its trade-protection leg is contingent on a Section 232 negotiation unresolved as of the July 2026 status report. The threat is highest if LiT positions as a producer-substitute competing for the same offtake and capital, and lowest if it positions as the process inside the producers' plants, selling to the better-funded producer and the government-underwritten national champion rather than against them, and differentiating on the fine, ultrafine, and mica-hosted fraction the funded competitors do not principally target.
**Confidence: Moderate-High that the competitor-funding, value-capture, and producer-concentration facts hold (Cited); the degree to which positioning neutralizes them is Assumption pending LiT's go-to-market choice and first deals.**
**Fragility note:** T-COMPETE is asymmetric across LiT's positioning, the same axis as T-LIABILITY. Read as "LiT is a domestic-recovery producer-substitute," it faces better-capitalized rivals its own customers fund and a government that underwrites those rivals, and it loses the capital-and-offtake contest structurally. ### Steelman — the case that the category, the geography, and the exit are real, large, and genuinely available to LiT

The strongest version is built from the commissioned research and the market data.

- **The resource category is physically vast and commercially under-exploited.** The world produces about 16 Bt of tailings annually against an accumulated inventory on the order of 282 Bt, with roughly 217 billion cubic metres held in about 8,500 facilities globally, and only about 25 percent of Qualified Persons have evaluated reprocessing at all. The addressable category is therefore both enormous and early, with most of the resource never assessed for recovery and the competitive field thin relative to the resource base. [Cited: Market Research Sec. 1 and 4; `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **High at the category level; site value is Unsupported.**
- **The underlying commodities carry genuine structural demand.** Lithium demand growth and a battery-share rise toward storage are real long-run pulls, with BESS demand up about 51 percent in 2025 to roughly a fifth of battery demand. The baskets LiT targets are not speculative end-markets; they are demanded by the energy transition and by storage in particular. [Cited: Market Research Sec. 1; `r00-price-update.md`] **Moderate-High on demand direction.**
- **The supply and demand balance is tightening in both target baskets.** Lithium's 2026 surplus is narrowing toward balance, with credible deficit calls of about 80kt from Morgan Stanley and about 22kt from UBS, a structural spodumene deficit is forming, and NdPr is in a second consecutive year of deficit. The medium-term direction of the price exposure the royalty sits on is upward-biased, not downward. [Cited: `r00-price-update.md`] **Medium-High on the direction.**
- **The critical minerals LiT targets are genuinely recoverable from tailings.** REEs occur in tailings as discrete minerals, clay-adsorbed, and organically complexed, all recoverable forms, and category research suggests that recovering about 90 percent of existing mining byproducts could supply nearly all US critical-mineral needs. The premise that valuable units are sitting in tailings waiting to be recovered is supported at the category level, so a grade-led site-selection strategy has a real, if site-dependent, target to aim at. [Cited: `r00-tailings-grades.md`] **Moderate at the category level; site sizing is Assumption pending U-13.**
- **The allied-R&D geography is low-friction.** India holds Strategic Trade Authorization Tier 1 status, is a Major Defense Partner, and is in an active US-India strategic trade dialogue, and IIT (ISM) Dhanbad is a credible, government-backed critical-minerals hub. [Cited: `r00-export-control.md`, `r00-ip-fto.md`] **Moderate-High that the destination is favorable; the specific LiT-IIT arrangement is Assumption pending U-17.**

[Cited: `r00-valuation-comps.md`] **Moderate.**

Put together: LiT addresses a vast, early, structurally demanded, and tightening resource category in which the target minerals are genuinely recoverable, operates its R&D from a favored allied destination, and can tell a two-stage exit story anchored to real comparables. On this reading the upside is large and the category timing is favorable.

### Challenge — the case that every item is a category-level truth whose transmission to LiT revenue is bounded by a confirmed weakness, and none may be cited to soften it

The strongest rebuttal accepts every fact above and contests only the leap from category to company.

- **A vast category is not a captured site, and site value is the uncharacterized variable.** The 282 Bt inventory and the 8,500 facilities are macro facts; none of them establishes that any single impoundment LiT can reach has the residual grade, mineralogy, tonnage, and particle-size distribution to be worth treating. The opportunity's transmission to revenue runs straight into W-GRADE, the master weakness that the per-site value is uncharacterized everywhere in the base, and T-GRADE, the threat that heterogeneity forces costly per-site characterization before each deal. The large category is the reason to build a grade-screening protocol, not evidence that the screening will pay. [Cited macro: `r00-tailings-grades.md`; site-level Unsupported, U-13; cross-link W-GRADE, T-GRADE, W-UPLIFT] **High that the category is large; the site value is Unsupported.**
- **The tightening balance is the upside twin of the price threat, not a separate hedge against it.** SPO-O1 supports the direction of LiT's price exposure, but it is the same instrument as T-PRICE read from the bull side: a royalty fully indexed to commodity price benefits when the balance tightens and suffers when it loosens, and the same H1-2026 record that shows the deficit calls also shows lithium round-tripping two-year highs to near its marginal-cost floor and NdPr falling about 35 percent in two months. The tightening cannot be banked as a floor and must not be netted against T-PRICE; it is the upside of the same exposure, capped on the lithium leg by fast swing-supply restarts (SPO-T2). [Cited: `r00-price-update.md`; cross-link T-PRICE, the $110/kg bull benchmark at RR-7] **Medium-High on direction; not a hedge.**
- **The recoverable-minerals premise still rests on the unvalidated uplift.** That REEs are present in recoverable forms in tailings does not establish that LiT's process recovers them at the rate the royalty math needs; the category presence of the units and LiT's ability to capture them are different claims, and the second is W-UPLIFT, the unvalidated recovery uplift carried with kill-switch standing. The opportunity sets the target; it does not validate the shot. [Cited: `r00-tailings-grades.md`; cross-link W-UPLIFT, F-2] **Moderate at category level; capture is unvalidated.**
- **The category being large and early does not solve the slow sale.** Under-exploitation and a thin competitive field are real, but the same low evaluation rate that makes the category early (only about 25 percent of Qualified Persons have looked at reprocessing) is also the category-novelty half of the adoption threat (T-ADOPT): an under-evaluated category is one LiT must educate as well as sell into. The size of the opportunity and the difficulty of converting it are two readings of the same 25 percent figure. [Cited: Market Research Sec. 1; cross-link T-ADOPT] **Moderate-High that under-exploitation cuts both ways.**
- **The favored R&D geography is bounded by the IP-control tension and the unevidenced cost base.** A low-friction allied destination is an advantage only if the offshore R&D does not collide with the IP-defensibility story (CHO-T2, the deemed-export and US-origin-IP tension that sharpens the more proprietary the work becomes) and only if the cost plan it underwrites is real, which it is not yet: the 75 percent India R&D split rests on an "upcoming" IIT (ISM) Dhanbad engagement with no executed agreement in the base (CAP-W5). The geography is favorable in principle; the specific arrangement and its cost base are Assumption. [Cited: `r00-export-control.md`, `r00-ip-fto.md`; cross-link CHO-T2, CAP-W5; U-17] **Moderate-High that the destination is favorable; the arrangement is Assumption.**
- **The exit framing is defensible only if it is not the inflated single number the model actually carries.** CAP-O1 is a cure, not a description of the plan as written: the near-term process-licensing multiple is defensible, but the headline 27x-34x return and the 8x-10x revenue framing are the overstated forms that W-MODEL and CAP-T1 flag, and the royalty re-rate is explicitly conditional on a diversified producing portfolio that does not yet exist. The opportunity is the disciplined two-stage story, and it is available only if LiT replaces the stacked headline with it. [Cited: `r00-valuation-comps.md`; cross-link W-MODEL, CAP-T1] **Moderate, and conditional on adopting the framing.**

### Adjudication

Both sides agree on the facts: the category is vast, early, structurally demanded, and tightening; the minerals are recoverable in principle; the R&D geography is favorable; and a two-stage exit is defensible on the right comparables. They disagree only on how much of each transmits to LiT revenue, and the honest resolution is the same one O-POLICY reached: each opportunity is CONFIRMED at the category level on Cited evidence, each is DOWNGRADED on its transmission to LiT revenue to Assumption, and each is bounded in the matrix by the named, already-confirmed weakness or threat that owns the gap, so that no opportunity may be cited to soften the weakness on the far side of its boundary. This produces three master opportunities, not one, because the three have different sources, boundaries, and owners and would lose precision if merged.

The market-category cluster (SPO-O2, SPO-O1, CLA-O1, the SPO-S1 demand fact) is **CONFIRMED** as a master opportunity at High confidence at the category level, with site value Unsupported and transmission to LiT revenue downgraded to Assumption. Its bounding constraints are explicit and load-bearing: the per-site value is W-GRADE and T-GRADE, the capture rate is W-UPLIFT, the conversion is T-ADOPT, and the demand-pull is the upside face of T-PRICE and may not be netted against it. The allied-R&D item (CHO-O3) is **CONFIRMED** as a separate, bounded opportunity at Moderate-High that the destination is favorable, bounded by CHO-T2 and CAP-W5 and Assumption as to the specific arrangement. The exit-framing item (CAP-O1) is **CONFIRMED** as a separate opportunity at Moderate, available only as the disciplined two-stage replacement for the stacked headline, bounded by W-MODEL and CAP-T1 and conditional on the portfolio the re-rate requires. No opportunity carries kill-switch standing, none opens a new risk-register line because opportunities track upside and the conditions that bound them are already on the register, and the same double-count discipline applied to the Strengths pass governs here: the demand fact, the geopolitical facts, and the transmission boundary are each recorded once across the quadrants they touch.

This produces three master findings, per-item dispositions, and routing.

#### Master finding 1 — market category and demand

**O-MARKET (master opportunity; High at category level; transmission to LiT revenue downgraded to Assumption; no kill-switch standing).** LiT addresses a physically vast and commercially under-exploited resource category, about 16 Bt of tailings produced annually against a roughly 282 Bt inventory in about 8,500 facilities, with only about 25 percent of Qualified Persons having evaluated reprocessing, whose underlying commodities carry genuine structural demand (BESS demand up about 51 percent in 2025 to roughly a fifth of battery demand) and whose supply and demand balance is tightening in both target baskets (lithium's 2026 surplus narrowing toward balance with about 80kt and about 22kt deficit calls, a structural spodumene deficit forming, NdPr in a second consecutive year of deficit), with the target minerals genuinely recoverable from tailings in discrete, clay-adsorbed, and organically complexed forms. The category is large, early, demanded, and tightening, and a grade-led site-selection strategy has a real target to aim at. What transmits to LiT revenue is bounded: the per-site value is uncharacterized (W-GRADE) and heterogeneous (T-GRADE), the capture rate is the unvalidated uplift (W-UPLIFT), the conversion runs through the slow under-evaluated operator sale (T-ADOPT), and the tightening balance is the upside face of the same price exposure that is T-PRICE on the downside and must not be netted against it.
**Confidence: High that the category is vast, under-exploited, structurally demanded, and tightening (Cited). Site value is Unsupported (U-13); the transmission to LiT revenue is Assumption, bounded by W-GRADE, W-UPLIFT, and T-ADOPT.**
**Fragility note:** O-MARKET is a category truth, not a company result. Its danger is that a large addressable number reads as a large reachable one: the 282 Bt inventory says nothing about whether any impoundment LiT can reach is economic, and the same low evaluation rate that makes the category early is the novelty LiT must educate against to sell. The opportunity justifies building a grade-and-demand-led screening discipline; it does not pre-validate that the screening pays.

#### Master finding 2 — allied-R&D footprint

**O-ALLIED-RD (opportunity; Moderate-High that the destination is favorable; the specific arrangement Assumption; no kill-switch standing).** LiT's R&D footprint sits in a favored, geopolitically low-friction allied destination: India holds Strategic Trade Authorization Tier 1 status, is a Major Defense Partner, and is in an active US-India strategic trade dialogue, and IIT (ISM) Dhanbad is a credible government-backed critical-minerals hub, so the offshore-R&D plan aligns with the allied-supply narrative rather than cutting against it. The advantage is bounded by two confirmed items: the IP-control tension that the more proprietary and restricted the offshore work becomes, the more squarely it sits under US export law (CHO-T2), and the unevidenced cost base, with the 75 percent India R&D split resting on an "upcoming" engagement and no executed IIT agreement in the record (CAP-W5).
**Confidence: Moderate-High that the destination is favorable and low-friction (Cited); the specific LiT-IIT arrangement and the cost base it underwrites are Assumption pending U-17, bounded by CHO-T2 and CAP-W5.**
**Fragility note:** O-ALLIED-RD is favorable in principle and unproven in particular. The geography reduces friction only if the arrangement exists and is managed against the deemed-export and US-origin-IP obligations; the same proprietary depth that builds the moat is what draws the export-control exposure, so the opportunity and CHO-T2 are two readings of one offshore-R&D decision.

#### Master finding 3 — exit framing

**O-EXIT (opportunity; Moderate; available only as the disciplined two-stage framing; no kill-switch standing).** LiT can present a defensible two-stage exit rather than a single inflated multiple: a near-term exit at 12x to 15x EBITDA sits at-to-just-above the realistic process-licensing and specialty-process central tendency of about 9x to 13x, and a higher royalty re-rate toward the 15x to 25x public EV/EBITDA range becomes available only after a diversified producing portfolio exists. Carried this way, the two framings give the equity story a defensible floor and a conditional ceiling and strengthen it. The opportunity is available only as the replacement for, not an addition to, the stacked headline: the 27x-34x return and the 8x-10x revenue framing are the overstated forms flagged by W-MODEL and CAP-T1, and the re-rate is conditional on the portfolio that does not yet exist.
**Confidence: Moderate that the two-stage framing is defensible on the cited comparables (Cited); the near-term multiple is anchored to process-licensing comps, the re-rate is conditional and Assumption, bounded by W-MODEL and CAP-T1.**
**Fragility note:** O-EXIT is a cure presented as an opportunity, and it only helps if LiT actually adopts it in place of the headline the model carries. Presented alongside the stacked return rather than instead of it, it does not de-risk the equity story; it decorates it.
