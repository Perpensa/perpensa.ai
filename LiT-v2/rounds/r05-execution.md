## Unit 1 — The phased architecture and the gates between phases

### Design principle (PILOT)

The strategy is "one path walked in order, capital committed one stage at a time, a hard go/no-go between each stage." The execution architecture turns that sentence into named phases separated by **gates**, where a gate is a pre-committed, binary, evidence-tested decision point that capital cannot cross without a documented pass. Three rules govern every gate, and they are the operational form of the deliberation's load-bearing dissent (D-1, the preserved CONTESTED-SPLIT):

1. **No scope or scale capital crosses a gate until the gate's pass condition is met on acceptable terms.** Spend inside a phase is bounded by that phase's pre-committed budget; spend on the *next* phase is unlocked only by the gate.
2. **Every gate has a pre-defined fail action, not only a pass action.** The fail action is drawn from the Round 04 change-triggers (CT-1..CT-7), so a "no" at a gate routes to a named alternative (re-scope, hold, abandonment fork, services bridge, or post-gate monetization), never to improvisation.
3. **Gates are tested against observable evidence, not against confidence.** What is measured at each gate, and the threshold it must beat, is specified in the KPI/proof-of-milestone unit (Unit 3); this unit fixes *which* gates exist and *what each decides*.

This satisfies both readings of the split at once. The company reading ("gateable, then proceed in sequence") gets a real sequence; DISSENT's reading ("nothing commits until the gates clear") gets gates that are genuine stop-unless-cleared decisions with funded fail forks. Neither view is merged into the other; the gate structure is the construction on which both agree.

The phases are numbered to the three strategy stages plus the entry phase. Gates are labelled G0, G1, G2 (the three between-stage gates) with intra-phase checkpoint gates labelled by phase (0.1, 0.2, 1.1, 2.n). Checkpoint gates govern *spend within a phase*; the G-gates govern *crossing into the next phase*.

### Phase 0 — Gate-Clearing Vehicle (executes Stage 0 / OPT-1)

**Mandate.** Clear the two conditional kill-switches, and nothing else. Secure the right to license, and validate the recovery uplift. Produce no revenue. This phase *is* OPT-1: it spends only to validate, runs to a pre-committed kill date and budget, and returns or redeploys unspent capital if either gate fails. Standing here without spending past it is the lowest-risk posture available, and clearing it and moving is the only acceptable exit. The phase is time-boxed precisely so it cannot degrade into indefinite limbo (the Unit 2 attack on OPT-1).

**Two parallel workstreams** (they run concurrently because their clocks are independent; neither blocks the other until the master gate):

- **Workstream R — Rights.** Negotiate and execute an exclusive, sublicensable, field-of-use VTIP license naming the minerals field; obtain a freedom-to-operate opinion; confirm a carve-out or non-foreclosure against the prior VTIP→MRC minerals license; model VTIP's own royalty into LiT's economics. Retires RR-1, RR-2, RR-3. This is a third-party legal event LiT influences but does not unilaterally control, which is why it is not allowed to gate the start of Workstream V.
- **Workstream V — Validation.** Secure a host-site access-and-liability agreement with an active-operator title holder; characterize the impoundment; run a continuous pilot on representative feedstock; measure the recovery uplift in percentage points against a named baseline, at or above the level a re-based royalty needs, reconciled to a congruent royalty base. Retires RR-4, RR-5 (jointly with RR-6), and the site-characterization leg of RR-16; runs under the access-and-liability agreement so per-site sampling is not a unilateral CERCLA disturbance (RR-21).

**Concurrent housekeeping inside Phase 0** (no revenue, low cost, removes self-inflicted weaknesses before any counterparty sees the model): re-base the royalty model onto incremental recovered value with the whole-mine NSR retained only as a labelled ceiling (RR-6); restate the return de-stacked and internally consistent, correcting the two-year timing error, with the optimistic stack labelled upside-only (RR-12, RR-13); validation-gate the environmental claims so no ESG magnitude is asserted ahead of the pilot result (RR-22); obtain the royalty-benchmarking dataset so the eventual rate is a benchmarked range, not an unanchored number (RR-23).

**Intra-phase checkpoint gates (govern spend within Phase 0):**

- **Gate 0.1 — FTO / rights-path checkpoint.** Before the *full* pilot budget is committed, a freedom-to-operate opinion exists and a credible, costed path to an executed exclusive sublicensable field-of-use license is in hand. Pass → release full validation spend. Fail (the prior MRC license forecloses the field with no obtainable carve-out, U-2/U-3 resolve against LiT) → stop here at minimum spend; this is CT-1 reached early and cheaply, routing to the abandonment fork or, only if a defensible disclosure position already exists, the services branch.
- **Gate 0.2 — Host-site / liability checkpoint.** No sampling or piloting begins until an access-and-liability agreement with an active-operator title holder is executed, keeping title and remediation standing with the operator (RR-21, RR-19). Pass → begin characterization and pilot. Fail (no host site reachable on containable-liability terms) → do not self-operate or sample an orphaned impoundment; hold or re-select. This checkpoint is the first place CT-6 (liability containment) is enforced, before any exposure is taken.

**Gate G0 — master proceed gate (HARD go/no-go; the entry condition to the entire commercial business).**

Both conditions true, on acceptable terms, inside the pre-committed budget and before the pre-committed kill date:
- (a) An executed exclusive, sublicensable, field-of-use VTIP license clearing the prior MRC rights, backed by an FTO opinion (RR-1/2/3 cleared).
- (b) A continuous-pilot recovery uplift, in percentage points against the named baseline, validated at or above the threshold a re-based royalty requires to clear burn on a single stream, reconciled to a congruent base (RR-4/5 cleared).

Pass action → enter Phase 1. Fail actions (pre-committed, drawn from the change-triggers):
- Rights fail or stall at the kill date (**CT-1**) → abandonment fork: wind down, return/redeploy unspent capital; OR, only if a genuinely defensible disclosure position exists, run the rights-protected services branch while the rights question is decided. Proceed options are off the table, not merely delayed.
- Uplift validates below the required threshold (**CT-2**) → do not enter Phase 1 at the modeled rate. Re-scope to a site or feedstock where the validated uplift clears, or fall to the abandonment fork. Headline resource figures stay restated as per-site-assay-dependent (W-GRADE) regardless of outcome.

G0 is the gate the entire deliberation converges on. Until it passes, LiT has no business it can sell to anyone, and no scale capital is committed. This is the operational guarantee that satisfies DISSENT's pass/fail reading.

### Phase 1 — Single-Site Beachhead (executes Stage 1 / OPT-3)

**Mandate.** Entered only after G0 passes. Sign and bring into production exactly ONE license, on one deliberately chosen active-operator site, and prove that the commercial model can be sold, delivered, and made to cover its own cost. Prove the model; do not yet prove the portfolio.

**Sequenced work:**
- **Site selection** for two properties at once: high assayed grade and a demand-led mineral basket the market actually wants (OC-6 / O-MARKET demand leg). An active operator's site, never an orphaned or abandoned one.
- **Deal structure** as the licensed process running *inside the operator's own plant* (OC-4). Title and remediation liability stay with the operator who already holds them (RR-19/20); LiT never takes title to recovered material or operator standing on the impoundment. This single structural choice contains T-LIABILITY and T-COMPETE at the same time and is the most important risk decision in the phase.
- **Pricing discipline.** The first rate is set and communicated as a pilot reference price, not the portfolio anchor, to contain the W-BASE first-deal anchoring risk (RR-6 base, RR-23 rate). One early deal struck before a track record is a weak place from which to price everything that follows.
- **Technical integration**, incurred once (MIL-W2), with per-site characterization priced into the deal (RR-16). Burn held to a level a single royalty stream can plausibly carry.

**Intra-phase checkpoint gate:**

- **Gate 1.1 — liability-containment gate (CT-6, enforced per candidate site).** The licensed-process-inside-the-plant structure must be securable at the chosen site, with title and remediation staying with the operator. Pass → proceed to sign. Fail → reject that site and re-select to another active-operator, license-to-title-holder site; never let a single deal pull LiT onto the self-operate or orphaned-site path the positioning was chosen to avoid. This gate fires before signing, every time, for every candidate.

**Gate G1 — prove-the-model gate (HARD go/no-go; the entry condition to widening).**

One site is signed and producing, and the single royalty stream covers the fixed cost of doing the deal, at the validated uplift and the re-based rate (RR-6) on a congruent base. The gate distinguishes three outcomes deliberately:

- **Over-delivers (CT-7).** Gates cleared on strong terms and the site beats the conservative case on validated uplift and economics. This, and only this, authorizes Phase 2 widening on a staged raise, and authorizes considering federal anchoring (OVL-A, carrying the OVL-B C2 caution from D-5). Broadening is earned by proof.
- **Clears but does not over-deliver.** The stream covers burn but does not beat the conservative case. Hold at one site; do not widen yet; reassess. The plan does not widen on a marginal pass.
- **Does not cover its own cost (CT-3).** Hold at one site, do not widen. Reassess whether any asset-light royalty path is viable, or pivot to a post-gate strategic monetization (OPT-5) from the now-de-risked position.

Additional fail/branch condition active throughout Phase 1: if adoption realizes materially slower than the conservative case (**CT-4**) with runway exposed, do not fund widening; extend runway through the non-dilutive services branch and/or the post-gate monetization option. The response to a slow market is more runway, not more scope. If a competitor or government program forecloses the position before the first deal signs (**CT-5**), accelerate the post-gate monetization decision while the position still has scarcity value.

### Phase 2 — Disciplined Widening (executes Stage 2 / sequenced OPT-2)

**Mandate.** Entered only on CT-7 (G1 over-delivers) and only against a staged raise tied to proof milestones, with a hard line against ever using LiT's own capital for client deployments (RR-14). The full platform (multiple feedstocks, additional sites, the broader recovery chemistry) is the destination, reached by repeated gated increments, never opened all at once. This is the only form in which OPT-2 survives its own Unit 2 attack.

**Structure: an iterating loop, not a single step.** Each new feedstock or site type is its own increment, and each increment carries its own gate:

- **Gate G2.n — per-increment validation gate (HARD go/no-go, fires once per new feedstock/site type).** A new feedstock or site class enters the commercial offer only after it is independently validated (the multi-feedstock/REE basis is unevidenced today, RR-17) and only against capital already raised for that increment on a proof milestone. Pass → that increment enters the offer. Fail → that increment does not enter; the platform widens only as far as proof has reached. The liability-containment gate (1.1 / CT-6) and the pricing discipline re-fire for every new site.

The widening loop has no fixed terminal count; it continues as long as each increment independently clears its gate and the staged raises remain available. The "full platform" is the asymptote of this loop, not a phase with an end date. OVL-A federal anchoring is considered only from inside this phase, after the first over-delivering proof, with the D-5 caution that any allied-R&D-base overlay (OVL-B) worsens the IP-defensibility position (C2 / CHO-T2) and must not be netted into a single number.

### Conditional branches (not phases on the main line)

These are responses to specific gate outcomes, held in reserve and entered only on their trigger. They are drawn on the architecture diagram as branches off the gates, never as steps everyone walks.

- **Branch S — rights-protected services wedge (OPT-4).** Available during Phase 0 and Phase 1 as a non-dilutive cash bridge, and the only coherent way to earn under a Phase-0 rights-gate stall (CT-1). Precondition that governs whether it may run at all: an executed option-to-license with IP-assignment and confidentiality terms, and a rights position far enough along that LiT has something defensible to disclose (its Unit 2 survival condition; honors D-3). Run any earlier and it sells the proof and keeps the risk, the exact inverse of the intended trade. It is a financing posture, not the business model, and it never becomes the main line.
- **Branch X — post-gate strategic monetization (OPT-5).** Reserved for a deliberate decision taken after G0 (ideally after G1), when LiT can sell a de-risked, proven position from strength and be paid for the value created (honors D-4 / DIS-O2). Reachable from three gate outcomes: G1 marginal-or-fail (CT-3), adoption too slow to fund widening (CT-4), or position being foreclosed (CT-5). Never taken pre-G0, where it would sell an unvalidated process behind an unsecured right and let a sophisticated buyer keep the de-risking discount or route directly to VTIP.

**Explicitly excluded from the architecture.** Build-own-operate / operator-funded producer JV (OPT-6). It inverts every containment decision the plan rests on, takes maximum liability and full commodity exposure with no royalty floor, abandons the no-client-capex line the fundability case depends on, and rests on a premise that does not hold (an operator funding capex on an unvalidated process). It is named here only to keep the declined trade-off explicit; it is not a phase, a branch, or a fallback.

### The gate ledger (the spine of the architecture)

| Gate | Type | Sits between | Pass condition (tested on Unit 3 evidence) | Pass action | Fail action (pre-committed) |
|------|------|--------------|--------------------------------------------|-------------|------------------------------|
| **0.1** | Checkpoint (spend) | early vs full Phase 0 spend | FTO opinion + credible costed path to executed exclusive sublicensable field-of-use license | Release full validation budget | CT-1 early: stop at min spend / abandonment fork / services branch if defensible |
| **0.2** | Checkpoint (spend) | rights-path vs pilot spend | Executed host-site access-and-liability agreement with active-operator title holder | Begin characterization + pilot | Hold or re-select; no self-operate, no orphaned-site sampling (CT-6 first enforcement) |
| **G0** | **Phase gate (HARD)** | Phase 0 → Phase 1 | (a) executed exclusive sublicensable field-of-use VTIP license clearing MRC + FTO; AND (b) continuous-pilot uplift in pp vs baseline at/above royalty threshold, reconciled to a congruent base | Enter Phase 1 | CT-1 (rights) → abandonment fork / services branch; CT-2 (uplift) → re-scope or abandon |
| **1.1** | Checkpoint (per site) | candidate site vs signature | Licensed-process-inside-plant securable; title + remediation stay with operator | Proceed to sign | CT-6: reject site, re-select active-operator title-holder site |
| **G1** | **Phase gate (HARD)** | Phase 1 → Phase 2 | One site signed, producing, single stream covers fixed deal cost at validated uplift + re-based rate | Over-deliver (CT-7) → Phase 2; clears-only → hold at one site | CT-3 → hold / reassess / Branch X; CT-4 → runway not scope; CT-5 → accelerate Branch X |
| **G2.n** | **Phase gate (HARD, per increment)** | each widening increment | New feedstock/site independently validated; staged capital raised on its proof milestone; no client capex | That increment enters the offer | Increment does not enter; platform widens only as far as proof reaches |

The architecture is therefore: **Phase 0 (clear the gates) → G0 → Phase 1 (prove one site) → G1 → Phase 2 (widen one validated increment at a time, looping on G2.n)**, with Branch S available as a financing bridge off the early gates and Branch X reserved as a from-strength exit off the post-gate decisions, and OPT-6 excluded. Every arrow that crosses into a new phase passes through a hard gate with a named fail fork. No capital crosses a hard gate on optimism.

## Unit 2 — Team build, hires, and the staged capital draw mapped to the phases

### Design principle (PILOT, with CAPITAL): capital is tranched to the gates, and the team is sized to the gated scope, not the headline portfolio

Two structural decisions govern this unit, and both follow directly from the gate spine in Unit 1.

**First: the capital draw is gated exactly like scope.** The single most-cited capital weakness is that the $2.0M seed is a single tranche with no reserve, which collapses the instant any spend overruns or any deployment capital becomes LiT's (W-CAPITAL / CAP-W1 / RR-14, Cited). The cure named in the risk register is a staged raise with a second-tranche or bridge contingency and a hard no-client-capex line. The execution form of that cure is to make each capital tranche unlock at the same hard gate that unlocks the next phase. A tranche is not released by the calendar or by confidence; it is released by a documented gate pass. This means the capital structure and the phase structure are the same structure, drawn twice: crossing G0 is the event that opens the Phase 1 raise, crossing G1 is the event that opens the Phase 2 raise, and each G2.n increment is funded only by capital raised against that increment's proof milestone. Capital can no more cross a hard gate on optimism than scope can.

**Second: the team is sized to the gated scope, and gating is what makes the W-TEAM cure affordable.** The W-TEAM finding is that the organization is sequenced to be weakest exactly where execution matters most: the staffing curve runs two principals through Year 2 and reaches seven only by Year 5, and the dedicated Technical Integration Engineer, the role that produces the renewal-driving verified-recovery result, does not arrive until Year 5 (MIL-W1, Cited; RR-10). That curve was built against a headline plan of sixteen licensees with concurrent pilots and multi-operator NSR verification. The narrow-first path retires most of that load by construction: Phase 1 runs exactly one integration on one site, so the concurrent-qualification and multi-pilot burden the original curve could not carry never arises at Phase 1. The team is therefore sized to "one deal, well delivered," not to "sixteen deals, thinly covered." That is what makes it possible to do the thing RR-10 demands, pull the BD and technical-integration capacity forward, without the headcount blowing through the thin seed: there is far less for that capacity to carry, so a far smaller amount of it, brought in far earlier, is sufficient. The gating does not merely defer the W-TEAM risk; it shrinks the load that creates it.

A corollary disciplines Phase 0 hiring specifically. Phase 0's defining property is that unspent capital must be returnable on a gate-fail (OPT-1's abandonment fork). Capital sunk into permanent salaried staff is not returnable. Therefore Phase 0 builds **no permanent operating headcount**: it runs on the two existing principals plus contracted, scope-bounded specialists (IP counsel, an FTO opinion, a pilot/metallurgical capability via the VTIP and IIT research engagements). Keeping Phase 0 staffed by contract rather than payroll is not only cheaper; it is what keeps the abandonment fork real.

### Capital draw mapped to the phases, and the sizing of each tranche

The cited financial base gives three hard line items: a $2.0M seed, an IP budget of roughly $235K, and a pilot budget of roughly $700K, against which only a $52K VT study has been executed [all Cited]. The line-item-adequacy finding (CAP-W4 / RR-14) holds that the IP and pilot lines are modest against contested multi-jurisdiction art and a multi-feedstock continuous-piloting plan. Unit 2 therefore does two things with these figures: it re-tranches the single seed to the gate spine, and it flags where the cited line items must be sized up before they can be relied on.

**Tranche A — the Phase 0 raise (funds gate-clearing to G0).** This is the re-purposed seed. It funds the two parallel workstreams plus lean principal opex and, critically, a **named reserve for a failed first pilot or a forced re-scope**, which the current single-tranche structure lacks. Reconciled to the cited line items:

- IP / rights workstream: the ~$235K IP line, flagged for upward sizing to carry the contested VTIP/MRC carve-out negotiation and FTO opinion (RR-1/2/3 cost; CAP-W4). [Cited base; uplift is Assumption, Medium-High that $235K understates contested multi-jurisdiction work.]
- Validation workstream: the ~$700K pilot line, similarly flagged for upward sizing to carry a continuous (not batch) pilot on representative feedstock. [Cited base; uplift Assumption.]
- Lean principal opex for the Phase 0 time-box, plus the model-restatement and benchmarking housekeeping (RR-6/12/13/22/23, low cost).
- A hard reserve sized to fund either an orderly wind-down with capital returned, or one re-scope-and-retry of the validation pilot, whichever the company pre-commits to.

The honest tension to record, not paper over: $235K + $700K already consumes nearly half of a $2.0M seed before any opex or reserve. Sizing the line items up to their contested scope and carving a real reserve pushes the responsible Phase 0 raise **at or above the current $2.0M**, or else forces the kill date in tight. This is the W-CAPITAL adequacy finding (CAP-W1, Medium-Low that $2.0M as structured is adequate) surfacing exactly where it should, at the moment the tranche is sized. The recommendation is to size Tranche A to the contested scope plus reserve and raise to that number, rather than hold the $2.0M figure and let the reserve be the thing that gets cut. [Assumption, Medium.]

**Tranche B — the Phase 1 raise (unlocked by G0).** Not raised, and not spent against, until G0 passes. Funds the two Phase 1 hires (commercial lead, integration engineer), the one-time technical-integration cost incurred at the first site (MIL-W2), and per-site characterization priced into the deal (RR-16). Held to a level a single validated royalty stream can plausibly carry, since Phase 1's whole purpose is to prove the stream covers its own fixed cost (the G1 test). [Assumption, Medium: exact size depends on the integration footprint, which is the MIL-W2 / U-19 open item.] The no-client-capex line binds hardest here: Tranche B funds LiT's own people and integration work, never the operator's deployment capital.

**Tranche(s) C — the Phase 2 per-increment raises (unlocked per G2.n).** Each widening increment is funded only by capital raised against that increment's proof milestone, on a staged basis. No single raise opens the full platform. This is the literal implementation of "a staged raise tied to proof milestones" and of the RR-14 cure.

**The no-client-capex line (RR-14), enforced as a hard rule across all tranches.** No tranche, in any phase, may fund client deployment capital. The licensed-process-inside-the-plant structure (OC-4) is what makes this enforceable: the operator funds and owns the plant the process runs in, so deployment capex sits with the party that holds the asset and the liability, never on LiT's balance sheet. Any deal that would require LiT to co-fund deployment capital is, by this rule, off the path, the same way OPT-6 is off the path. ## Unit 3 — KPIs and proof-of-milestone thresholds for every gate

### Design principle (PILOT, with ROYALTY and CRUCIBLE): a gate measures one observable, against one pre-set threshold, certified by a named party

Unit 1 fixed which gates exist and what each decides. Unit 2 funded them. This unit makes each gate testable: it states, for every gate, the specific quantity that is measured, the protocol by which it is measured, the threshold it must beat, the party who certifies the reading, and the evidence tag on the threshold itself. Three rules keep this honest and keep it from quietly clearing a gate that the evidence base has not cleared:

1. **A threshold is a test, not a forecast.** Where LiT's own private figures (its actual burn, its actual baseline recovery on a specific feedstock, its negotiated rate) set the number, the threshold is written as a formula LiT parameterizes from those figures at the gate, not as a number invented here. Inventing the number would be fabrication; defining the test is the deliverable. Each such formula is tagged for what is Cited, what is Assumption, and what remains NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL.

2. **The measurement protocol is part of the threshold.** "Validated uplift" with no protocol is unfalsifiable. Each KPI therefore fixes the measurement basis (continuous versus batch, representative versus cherry-picked feedstock, gross versus net, who holds the assay) so the same reading cannot be claimed as a pass by one party and a fail by another.

3. **Certification is external where the claim is load-bearing.** The two kill-switch readings (the rights event and the uplift result) are certified by parties outside the deal: rights by IP counsel and an FTO opinion, uplift by an independent continuous pilot with assays at an accredited lab. LiT does not grade its own kill-switch homework. This is the measurement form of the CONTESTED-SPLIT (D-1): the gates can only be passed on evidence a skeptic would accept.

A note on what cannot be numerically fixed here. Several thresholds depend on figures LiT holds privately and has not placed in the evidence base (its real monthly burn, the baseline recovery of a named feedstock, a negotiated royalty rate, the continuous-pilot run-time for this chemistry). For each, the right output is the **formula plus the inputs LiT must supply**, with the missing input flagged. Reporting a precise pass number built on figures not in evidence would breach the no-fabrication rule. The benchmark research narrows some of these (royalty-rate band, pilot duration order-of-magnitude); where it does, the band is carried.

### G0 KPIs — the master proceed gate (HARD). Two independent readings, neither nettable into the other.

**Reading (a) — the rights event (Workstream R). Binary, certified externally.**

- **KPI G0-R — executed license clearing the prior rights.** Metric: a fully executed VTIP license. Threshold, all four required: (i) exclusive in the minerals field; (ii) sublicensable; (iii) field-of-use defined to name the minerals application; (iv) it clears or is non-foreclosed by the prior VTIP→MRC minerals license, confirmed against the FTO opinion. VTIP's own royalty is modeled into LiT economics, not left off-book. Certifier: external counsel confirms execution and the carve-out; the FTO opinion confirms (iv). Tag: the four-part test is Cited as the release condition for W-RIGHTS (RR-1/2/3); whether it is obtainable is the open legal question (U-1) the gate exists to decide. This reading is pass/fail with no partial credit: a license missing any of the four does not pass.

**Reading (b) — the uplift event (Workstream V). The core numeric conversion of this unit.**

The Unit 1 condition is "continuous-pilot uplift, in percentage points against a named baseline, at or above the threshold a re-based royalty needs to clear burn on a single stream, reconciled to a congruent base." Converted to a measured test:

- **KPI G0-V — validated incremental recovery at or above the royalty-break-even uplift.**
  - **Metric:** ΔR = (recovery achieved by the LiT process) minus (the named baseline recovery on the same representative feedstock), expressed in percentage points. Measured on a **continuous** pilot run that reaches and holds equilibrium across the two-stage circuit's recirculating streams, not a batch test. [Continuous-piloting requirement Cited: research/r00-pilot-benchmarks.md; batch results do not retire scale-up risk.]
  - **Named baseline:** the recovery the host operator achieves today on that feedstock without LiT (or documented status-quo recovery for that material class). The uplift is incremental over this, not over zero. [Reconciliation to the re-based congruent base, RR-6: the royalty is charged on incremental recovered value, so the measured ΔR must be the same increment the royalty is charged on. This is the "congruent base" requirement made literal.]
  - **Threshold (the formula LiT parameterizes at the gate):** ΔR must be large enough that, at the benchmarked royalty rate, one site's annual royalty covers Phase 1 annual fixed cost. Stated as the break-even uplift:

    **ΔR\* = AnnualBurn / ( rate × throughput × grade × payable × price )**

    where the denominator is one site's annual incremental recovered value per point of uplift. ΔR (measured) ≥ ΔR\* (computed) is the pass test.
  - **Input tags for the formula:**
    - `rate` — benchmarked royalty rate. Band is **low-single-digit % up to ~5% gross on the recovered stream** [Cited: royalty-benchmarking research, RR-23]. LiT enters the negotiated figure; the band bounds it.
    - `price` — commodity price for the recovered basket. [Cited price set in research/r00-price-update.md; carries full T-PRICE exposure, which is why ΔR\* moves with price and the gate is re-tested if price moves materially, CT.]
    - `throughput`, `grade`, `payable` — site-specific, supplied by the Phase 0 host site and its characterization (W-GRADE: per-site assay-dependent, not a headline resource figure). [Assumption until the host site is characterized; NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL at the company level: LiT's target-site tonnage and grade, U-13/U-14.]
    - `AnnualBurn` — Phase 1 annual fixed cost (the two hires, periodic integration support, overhead). [Company-private; NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL: LiT's actual Phase 1 burn. The number is LiT's to supply; the test is fixed here.]
  - **Certifier:** an independent continuous pilot with assays at an accredited laboratory; the technical principal does not self-certify the kill-switch reading. [Measurement-integrity rule 3.]
  - **Tag:** the test and its protocol are Cited/derived; the absolute ΔR\* is an Assumption parameterized from figures partly NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL. The point is that the gate cannot be passed by a generic "the process works"; it is passed only when a specific measured ΔR clears a specific computed ΔR\* on a congruent base.

**Why the two readings do not net.** A strong uplift does not buy a weak rights position, and an executed license does not substitute for a sub-threshold uplift. Both must pass on their own terms. This preserves W-RIGHTS and W-UPLIFT as the two independent single points of failure the convergence recorded, never as one blended score. Pass (both) enters Phase 1; fail fires CT-1 (rights) or CT-2 (uplift) per the Unit 1 ledger.

### G1 KPIs — the prove-the-model gate (HARD). The "covers its own cost" conversion, with three banded outcomes.

The Unit 1 condition is "one site signed and producing, single royalty stream covers the fixed cost of doing the deal, at the validated uplift and the re-based rate on a congruent base." Converted:

- **KPI G1-1 — the site is signed and producing.** Metric: an executed license AND the process running in the operator's plant producing a measured recovered stream. Threshold: both true; a signed-but-not-producing site does not pass (the proof is production, not signature). Certifier: production records plus the integration engineer's verified-recovery reading. Tag: Cited milestone definition.
- **KPI G1-2 — coverage ratio against fixed deal cost.** Metric: the coverage ratio

    **C = (annualized royalty revenue from the one live site) / (annual fixed cost of running that deal, including the LiT support-FTE and amortized one-time integration)**

  measured on actual production at the validated ΔR and the executed rate on the congruent (incremental) base. **Threshold and the three deliberate bands** (these are the Unit 1 outcomes made numeric):
    - **C ≥ an over-deliver line (set above 1.0 with margin, e.g. the site beats the conservative case on both ΔR and economics): CT-7 over-delivers.** Only this authorizes Phase 2 widening on a staged raise and authorizes considering federal anchoring (OVL-A, carrying the OVL-B / C2 caution). [Over-deliver margin is an Assumption, Medium: it must sit far enough above 1.0 that the result is not a rounding artifact; LiT sets the conservative case it must beat.]
    - **1.0 ≤ C < over-deliver line: clears but does not over-deliver.** Hold at one site; do not widen; reassess. A marginal pass does not open Phase 2.
    - **C < 1.0: does not cover its own cost (CT-3).** Hold, do not widen; reassess whether any asset-light royalty path is viable, or route to Branch X from the now-de-risked position.
  - Certifier: actual production and royalty records; the coverage ratio is computed on realized figures, not modeled ones. Tag: the ratio and the three bands are Cited/derived; the over-deliver line is the one Assumption LiT must pre-commit before Phase 1 so the band cannot be redrawn after the result is known.
- **Throughput-of-time qualifier (CT-4).** If adoption realizes materially slower than the conservative case with runway exposed, the response is runway (Branch S / Branch X), not scope, regardless of C. Measured as: time-to-signature and time-to-production versus the Phase 1 plan; a material slip with exposed runway fires CT-4 independently of the coverage reading.

### The per-site LiT support-FTE figure (RR-11 / U-19), defined as a model with a flagged number

Unit 2 defined on-site crewing as "the operator's own workforce plus periodic LiT technical support" and deferred the support-staffing figure to this unit. The honest state of the evidence: the precise per-site continuous-circuit support staffing for this recovery class is **NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL (U-19)**; the pilot-benchmark research establishes the continuous-piloting requirement and the campaign duration order-of-magnitude but does not publish a steady-state support-FTE. The figure is therefore given as a **defined model with a parameterized band**, not an invented headcount.

- **Model.** LiT support to a live site has two regimes:
  - **Commissioning regime (per new site, time-boxed):** near-continuous support from the integration engineer for a defined window while the circuit reaches and holds equilibrium. Modeled as a high fraction of one engineer's time dedicated to that single site for the commissioning window, then stepping down. [Assumption, Medium: follows from the continuous-equilibrium requirement, research/r00-pilot-benchmarks.md; the window length tracks the pilot/commissioning duration, partly U-13/U-14.]
  - **Steady-state regime (per live site, ongoing):** scheduled periodic support visits plus remote monitoring, the operator's workforce running the circuit day-to-day. Modeled as a **fraction of one integration engineer's FTE per live site**, such that one integration engineer can support several steady-state sites concurrently. The planning band carried is **on the order of 0.2 to 0.5 support-FTE per live site in steady state** (one engineer covering roughly two to five live sites), stepping down further as the operator's crew matures. [Assumption, Low; the precise fraction is U-19, NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL: per-site continuous-circuit support staffing benchmarks. The band is a planning placeholder, explicitly flagged, not a measured figure.]
- **How the figure is used and disciplined.** The steady-state support-FTE per site is an **input to the G1 coverage ratio** (it is part of the "annual fixed cost of running that deal"). Because it feeds the gate test, understating it would falsely inflate C; the band is therefore carried conservatively (toward the higher support cost) until U-19 is retrieved, so the gate is not passed on an optimistic staffing assumption. As real commissioning data arrives at the first site, the modeled band is replaced by the measured figure, which then sets the true per-site support load for every Phase 2 increment. This is the asset-light line (RR-11) made quantitative without fabricating the number: a defined model, a conservative flagged band, and a commitment to replace the band with the first site's measured reading.

## Unit 4 — Every High and Elevated risk vector mapped to an owned mitigation, pinned to the phase that owns it

### Design principle (PILOT, with DISSENT holding the no-softening line)

A risk register is inert until two things are nailed down for every line: **who owns the mitigation** (a single accountable role, not "the company") and **where in the plan it lives** (a specific phase, so the mitigation has a clock and a budget and cannot drift). This unit does that for all twelve High and Elevated vectors. It does not invent new mitigations where the gate spine already supplies one. Four of the load-bearing vectors are mitigated by gate tests already specified in Unit 3, and the honest move is to make that ownership explicit rather than to restate the test as if it were a separate control:

- **W-UPLIFT is owned by the G0-V break-even test (ΔR ≥ ΔR\*).** The mitigation for an unvalidated recovery uplift is the measured continuous-pilot reading at G0, certified externally. There is no second control; the gate is the control.
- **W-RIGHTS is owned by the G0-R four-part license test.** The mitigation for "the IP may not be LiT's to license" is the executed, externally-confirmed license clearing the prior MRC rights at G0. The gate is the control.
- **T-LIABILITY is owned by the Gate 1.1 (and Gate 0.2) liability-containment test.** The mitigation for title/remediation exposure is the licensed-process-inside-the-operator's-plant structure, tested per site before signing. The gate is the control.
- **W-CAPITAL adequacy is owned by the G1 coverage bands and the tranche-to-gate structure.** The mitigation for a thin single-tranche seed is capital released only on a documented gate pass, with the coverage ratio C deciding whether the next tranche opens at all.

For these four, Unit 4's job is assignment, not invention. The remaining vectors (T-PRICE, T-GRADE, T-ADOPT, T-COMPETE, W-BASE, W-MODEL, W-TEAM, W-GRADE) are not fully carried by a single gate, so this unit pins each to its owning phase and names the mitigating action and its accountable role. Two discipline rules hold throughout: a structural risk (one that cannot be cured, only contained) is labelled as contained-not-cured so no one later reads the plan as having removed it; and DISSENT's standing requirement is honored, that pinning a mitigation to a phase does not clear the underlying weakness or downgrade its severity, it only assigns the work.

A note on owners. The frame is the company, so owners are LiT roles, not seat codenames. The standing roles are: the **commercial principal** (CEO, owns rights negotiation, fundraising, pricing, positioning, and the go/no-go calls), the **technical principal** (owns characterization, the pilot, and the recovery result), the Phase-1 **BD lead** and **technical-integration engineer** (the two pulled-forward hires), and **contracted counsel** in two specialisms (IP/patent counsel for rights and FTO, environmental/mining counsel for liability). The risk-register codes are retained here for traceability and disappear in the client-grade plan.

### The four gate-owned vectors (mitigation = the gate test already defined)

**W-RIGHTS (High; kill-switch standing; CONTESTED-SPLIT).** Risk: the exclusive, sublicensable, field-of-use right to the minerals application may not be obtainable clear of the prior VTIP→MRC license (RR-1/2/3, U-1). Owned mitigation: the **G0-R four-part license test plus the FTO opinion**, with no proceed-spend crossing G0 until an executed license and a non-adverse FTO both exist. Owner: **commercial principal** for the negotiation, **contracted IP/patent counsel** for the FTO opinion and the carve-out confirmation. Phase pinned: **Phase 0, Workstream R**, with the early read at Gate 0.1. Type: this is a third-party legal event LiT influences but does not control, so the mitigation is a gate that stops the business if the event does not occur, not a control that guarantees it occurs. Residual after mitigation: **the risk is not reduced, it is gated**; if the license is not obtainable, the fail action (CT-1: abandonment fork, or Branch S only with a defensible disclosure position) fires. Severity stays High; the CONTESTED-SPLIT (D-1) is preserved, since DISSENT's reading is exactly that nothing commits until this clears.

**W-UPLIFT (High; kill-switch standing; CONTESTED-SPLIT).** Risk: the recovery uplift that the whole royalty case rests on is unvalidated (F-2, RR-4/5). Owned mitigation: the **G0-V break-even test, ΔR ≥ ΔR\***, measured on a continuous pilot against a named baseline, reconciled to the congruent incremental base, certified by an independent pilot with accredited assays. Owner: **technical principal** runs the validation; the **independent pilot and accredited lab** certify it (LiT does not grade its own kill-switch). ### What Unit 4 establishes for the later Round 05 units

Every High and Elevated vector now has a single accountable owner and a phase that owns the work, and the map distinguishes the four vectors whose mitigation **is** a gate test already specified in Unit 3 (W-RIGHTS → G0-R, W-UPLIFT → G0-V, T-LIABILITY → Gate 1.1, W-CAPITAL → tranche-to-gate plus G1 bands) from the eight that needed an explicit owned mitigation pinned here (T-PRICE structural exposure carried not cured; T-GRADE and W-GRADE owned by the same characterization discipline; T-ADOPT mitigated as runway not scope; T-COMPETE mitigated by the licensor-not-producer positioning that also owns T-LIABILITY; W-BASE by re-base plus pilot-reference pricing; W-MODEL by restatement; W-TEAM by sizing to gated scope and pulling the two roles forward).

## Unit 5 — Sequencing the pilot, IP, and first-licensing dependencies

### Design principle (PILOT): the plan is a partial order, not a straight line

Units 1 through 4 fixed the phases, the gates, the capital, the KPIs, and the owners. What they did not yet fix is the order in which the work inside those phases must happen. The strategy is "one path walked in order," but inside each phase several mitigations run at once, and a few of them are hard prerequisites of others: one cannot start until another finishes. Getting that order wrong is its own failure mode. Spending the pilot budget before the host-site liability is contained, pricing a deal before the site is characterized, or raising capital against a model that still carries a known error, each spends scarce money or credibility on a foundation that is not yet there. This unit names every hard prerequisite, lays the work out as a dependency graph rather than a list, and identifies the critical path the kill date is measured against.

Two kinds of dependency are distinguished, because they are managed differently:

- **Hard prerequisite (finish-to-start).** Work B cannot begin, or capital for B cannot be committed, until work A has produced a specific result. These are the load-bearing sequencing rules; each one is a place where doing things in the wrong order destroys value. They are enforced as gate or checkpoint conditions, never as good intentions.
- **Parallel-independent clock.** Work that proceeds concurrently because neither blocks the other, even though both must complete before a shared downstream gate. These are run in parallel deliberately, to compress the calendar, and the later of the two clocks sets the gate date.

The unit reads the dependency graph in the order capital meets it: the one predecessor that sits before the phase clock even starts, then the Phase 0 parallel clocks and the finish-to-start rules inside them, then the rejoin at G0, then the branch precondition, then the Phase 1 and Phase 2 chains, and finally the critical path.

---

### Step zero (before the phase clock starts): the model is restated before Tranche A is raised against it

One dependency sits entirely **before Phase 0 begins**, because it is the predecessor of the fundraise itself. The W-MODEL housekeeping (de-stack the optimistic assumptions, correct the two-year timing error, label the optimistic stack as upside-only, validation-gate the ESG claims) must be complete **before Tranche A is raised against the model** (RR-12/13/22, Unit 4). The reasoning is a strict finish-to-start order: Tranche A is raised on the strength of the model a counterparty sees; if that model still carries a known internal error or an unvalidated ESG magnitude when the raise opens, LiT has spent its credibility before its capital, and a sophisticated investor who finds the error discounts everything else in the deck with it. The restatement is cheap, fully inside LiT's control, and depends on nothing external, so there is no reason for it to be anywhere but first. It is the zeroth task: done before the Phase 0 clock starts, so that the capital the whole plan runs on is raised against a model that survives scrutiny. [Cited: timing inconsistency CAP-W2; ESG validation-gate RR-22. The ordering claim is derived: a known-defective model must not be the basis of the raise.]

The royalty re-base (RR-6) and the benchmarking dataset (RR-23) are part of the same pre-counterparty housekeeping and follow the same rule: re-base onto the incremental recovered value and obtain the rate band **before** the model is shown or any rate is discussed, so no counterparty ever sees the whole-mine NSR notional presented as the royalty base. These are low-cost and depend on nothing external, so they sit alongside the model restatement at the front of Phase 0.

---

### Phase 0: two parallel clocks, with hard finish-to-start rules inside the validation clock

Phase 0 runs **two parallel-independent clocks** (Unit 1): the rights clock (Workstream R) and the validation clock (Workstream V). They start together and run concurrently to compress the calendar, because neither is a prerequisite of the other: the rights negotiation does not wait on the pilot, and the pilot's *technical* work does not wait on the license. But the two clocks are not internally symmetric. The validation clock carries a chain of hard finish-to-start prerequisites that the rights clock does not, and one cross-clock rule binds them both before the pilot money is spent.

**The rights clock (Workstream R), internal order:** engage IP/patent counsel → commission the FTO opinion and establish a costed license path (**Gate 0.1**, ~Month 4 to 6) → negotiate the exclusive, sublicensable, field-of-use terms and the MRC carve-out → execute the license (**G0-R**). The one internal finish-to-start dependency here is that the **FTO opinion is a prerequisite of the G0-R carve-out confirmation**: the fourth part of the G0-R test (clears or is non-foreclosed by the prior VTIP→MRC license) is confirmed *against* the FTO opinion (Unit 3, KPI G0-R-iv), so the opinion must exist before the license can be certified as clearing the prior rights. Gate 0.1 is therefore not a soft milestone; it is the point where the rights path is proven costed-and-clearable before the full validation budget is released.

**The validation clock (Workstream V), internal order, three hard prerequisites in series:**

1. **Host-site access-and-liability agreement (Gate 0.2) before any sampling or pilot spend.** No characterization sample is taken and no pilot dollar is committed until an access-and-liability agreement with an active-operator title holder is executed, keeping title and remediation standing with the operator (RR-21/19, Unit 1/3). This is the first enforcement of the liability-containment decision (CT-6) and it is a strict predecessor: sampling an impoundment before the liability is contained is the exact self-inflicted CERCLA-disturbance exposure the positioning exists to avoid. Access-and-liability first, then any physical contact with the material.
2. **Per-site characterization before the pilot is run on representative feedstock.** Once access exists, the target impoundment is characterized (documented chain of custody, accredited assay) on the material a first deal would address, *before* the continuous pilot is designed and run, so the pilot runs on representative feedstock and the baseline recovery is the real status-quo recovery on that material (RR-16, KPI 0.2-b/G0-V baseline). A pilot run on a convenience sample proves nothing the gate can accept.
3. **Continuous pilot to equilibrium, producing the measured ΔR (G0-V).** Only after characterization does the continuous pilot run to and hold equilibrium across the recirculating streams, producing the measured incremental recovery the G0-V break-even test reads (Unit 3).

**The cross-clock rule that binds both clocks before the pilot money is spent:** the **full pilot budget is committed only after both Gate 0.1 (FTO and costed rights path) AND Gate 0.2 (host-site access-and-liability) have passed.** This is the single most important sequencing rule in Phase 0, and it is why the rights clock and the validation clock, though independent in their work, converge at the moment of pilot spend. The logic is capital-protective: the pilot is the largest single Phase 0 cost (the ~$700K line, flagged for upward sizing), and LiT does not commit it until it knows two things that the pilot cannot itself establish, namely that there is a credible path to actually owning the right to license the result (0.1) and that there is a containable-liability site on which to generate it (0.2). Spending the pilot budget before either is in hand risks producing a validated uplift LiT has no right to commercialize, or generating it by taking liability the plan is built to avoid. **FTO and host-site access before pilot spend** is therefore enforced as a release condition on the pilot budget, not as a sequencing preference. [Cited as the Unit 1/2/3 checkpoint structure; the ordering is derived from the capital-protection and liability-containment rules already adopted.]

**The characterization-before-pricing rule (spans the clocks into pricing).** Per-site characterization is also a hard predecessor of *any rate being priced*. The throughput, grade, and payable that set both the ΔR\* break-even computation and any quoted royalty rate are site-specific outputs of characterization (W-GRADE: per-site assay-dependent, not a headline resource figure; Unit 3 G0-V inputs). No rate is priced, and the G0-V pass cannot be computed, until the site is characterized. This rule starts in Phase 0 (the first host site is characterized before its economics are computed) and re-fires at every site and every increment thereafter. **Characterization before any rate is priced** is the standing form of the rule.

### The rejoin at G0: two clocks, both must clear, neither nets, the later one sets the date

The two parallel clocks **rejoin at G0**, the master proceed gate. Both **G0-R (the rights event) and G0-V (the uplift event) must clear**, on acceptable terms, inside the budget box and before the kill date. They are run as independent parallel clocks precisely so that the calendar is the *later* of the two, not the sum of the two; but both are mandatory, and **neither reading nets into the other** (Unit 3): a strong uplift does not buy a weak rights position, and an executed license does not substitute for a sub-threshold uplift. This preserves W-RIGHTS and W-UPLIFT as the two independent single points of failure (the convergence record) and is the sequencing expression of the CONTESTED-SPLIT (D-1): the gate opens only when both skeptic-acceptable readings are in hand. Whichever clock finishes later sets the G0 date, and that date must fall inside the **Month 12 to 18 kill window from Tranche A funding** (Unit 2), with Gate 0.1 at roughly Month 4 to 6 and Gate 0.2 executed before any pilot spend begins. If the later clock cannot reach G0 inside the window on acceptable terms, the pre-committed fail action fires (CT-1 rights, CT-2 uplift) while capital remains to execute it, because the kill date is set inside seed-exhaustion (Unit 2). [Cited: G0 two-reading structure Unit 1/3; kill window Unit 2; non-netting Unit 3.]

---

### The branch precondition: rights executed before any disclosure in Branch S

Branch S (the rights-protected services wedge) is the one place where a sequencing error would hand away the entire asset, so its precondition is a hard prerequisite in its own right: **the rights position must be executed far enough to be defensible before any technical disclosure is made under Branch S.** Concretely, an executed option-to-license with IP-assignment and confidentiality terms must be in hand *before* LiT discloses the process to a services counterparty (Unit 1/2, its Unit 2 survival condition; honors D-3). Run in the other order, Branch S sells the proof and keeps the risk, the exact inverse of the intended trade: a services engagement entered before the rights are tied down lets the counterparty learn the process without LiT holding the right to stop them using it. This is why Branch S, although it is the named earning posture under a Phase-0 rights *stall* (CT-1), is itself gated on a rights *instrument* being executable: even the contingency that exists to survive a rights problem cannot run until a minimum rights position is secured. **Rights executed before any disclosure in Branch S** is enforced as the branch's entry condition. [Cited: Branch S option-to-license precondition Unit 1/2; D-3.]

### Phase 1 and Phase 2: the same ordered chain, per site and per increment

Once G0 passes, Phase 1 runs its own internal finish-to-start chain, and Phase 2 re-runs a scoped version of it for every increment. The order is fixed and the characterization-before-pricing and liability-before-signing rules re-fire each time:

**Phase 1 (one site), internal order:** grade-led site selection (high assayed grade, demand-led basket, OC-6) → **per-site characterization** (re-firing the characterization-before-pricing rule for the chosen site) → **Gate 1.1 liability-containment test** (the licensed-process-inside-the-operator's-plant structure securable, title and remediation staying with the operator, before signing) → **pilot-reference pricing** against the benchmarked band (first rate framed as a reference, not the portfolio anchor) → sign the license → integrate and bring into production → **G1 coverage measured on realized production**. Two hard prerequisites inside this chain: characterization precedes pricing (no rate on a portfolio average), and the Gate 1.1 containment test precedes signing (no signature on a site that cannot be structured to keep title and remediation with the operator). A site that fails 1.1 is rejected and re-selected before any rate is discussed.

**Phase 2 (each increment), the chain scoped to one new feedstock or site class:** characterize the new feedstock on representative material of its class → compute and validate ΔR\*(increment) on its own continuous pilot (each feed earns its own proof, RR-17) → secure the staged raise against that increment's proof milestone → Gate 1.1 containment re-fires for the increment's site → **G2.n** admits the increment to the offer. The same two prerequisites hold per increment: characterization before the increment's economics are priced, and liability containment before the increment's site is signed. ### The critical path

The longest chain of hard finish-to-start dependencies, the one the kill date is actually measured against, runs: **model restatement (#0) → Tranche A (#1) → the later of the two Phase 0 clocks → G0.** Inside Phase 0 the two clocks are deliberately parallel so the calendar is the later clock, not the sum, but both gate G0, so both sit on the critical path in the sense that either can stop it.

- **The validation clock** is the likelier calendar-driver, because it carries the longest internal series (host-site agreement → characterization → a continuous pilot run to equilibrium → measured ΔR) and the pilot run-time for this recovery class is the open quantity that most directly sets the kill-window position (U-13/U-14 and continuous-pilot duration, partly NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL; partially addressed in research/r00-pilot-benchmarks.md). The continuous-piloting requirement, not a batch test, is what gives this clock its length and is non-negotiable (Unit 3).
- **The rights clock** is the likelier *risk*-driver even when it is not the calendar-driver, because it is a third-party legal event LiT influences but does not control: it can stall at the MRC carve-out regardless of how fast LiT moves, and a stall trips Gate 0.1 (~Month 4 to 6) before the full pilot budget is ever committed, which is exactly why Gate 0.1 sits early on the path. The sequence is built so the cheaper, faster rights read (the FTO and costed path at 0.1) happens before the expensive validation spend, so a fatal rights problem is found at minimum cost.

The path is therefore designed so that the two things most likely to kill the business (the rights event and the uplift result) are both tested inside the Month 12 to 18 window, with the cheap legal read (0.1) and the liability gate (0.2) both clearing before the largest spend, and with the later of the two parallel clocks setting the G0 date. No work that depends on a prerequisite is funded ahead of that prerequisite, and the kill date is positioned so that whichever clock runs longer, capital still remains to execute the fail action when it lands.
