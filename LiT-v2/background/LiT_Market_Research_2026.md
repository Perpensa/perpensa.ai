## How to read this document

This is research, not analysis. Each section summarizes the state of the evidence and closes with a Perpensa risk-vector block. It contains no SWOT, no strategy, and no recommendations; those belong to the downstream deliverable. The risk vectors are evidence-surfaced flags, not strategic judgments.

Sources are attributed inline in parentheses and listed in full at the end. Market-size figures drawn from commercial research aggregators vary by methodology; where estimates conflicted, the range is given rather than a single number.

### Perpensa risk-vector legend

Six vectors, each flagged directionally as Low / Moderate / Elevated / High based on the evidence surfaced:

- **Demand** — market pull and timing
- **Competition** — incumbents and substitutes
- **Technology/IP** — defensibility and freedom to operate
- **Adoption** — customer behavior and sales cycle
- **Regulatory** — policy tailwinds and exposure
- **Execution/Capital** — scale-up and funding

## 1. State of the target market

LiT's named target market is mineral processing technology applied to two feedstock families (lithium and rare earths) plus tailings, sold to mining operators on a licensing-plus-royalty basis. The state of each component follows.

### Lithium

The market just came through a violent correction. Global lithium carbonate output surged 192 percent between 2020 and 2024 while demand lagged, leaving supply in excess of demand by more than 150,000 metric tons in both 2023 and 2024 (Nasdaq, INN). Prices fell more than 80 percent from late-2022 highs, bottoming at about $8,259 per ton in China by June 2025 (Investing News, MiningVisuals). A sharp recovery followed. Chinese spot prices rebounded 57 percent between June and November 2025, from $8,259 to $13,003 per tonne (MiningVisuals), and into early 2026 battery-grade lithium carbonate nearly doubled to about US$26,278 per ton on supply delays and speculative buying (INN).

Forecasters disagree on the 2026 balance. Morgan Stanley forecasts a deficit of around 80,000 tons LCE while UBS expects a smaller deficit of around 22,000 tons (GoldInvest). Fitch, by contrast, believes the market will remain oversupplied in 2026 unless significant capacity cuts occur, and Wood Mackenzie projects the surplus expanding to 153,000 tons (NAI500).

The downturn hollowed the project pipeline. Feasibility studies dropped from dozens annually to fewer than 10 in 2025, with delays of at least 12 months for many developments (INN). Demand structure is shifting from pure EV toward storage: EVs consume roughly 70 percent of lithium output, but battery energy storage demand grew 51 percent in 2025, lifting storage to about a fifth of total battery demand (Investing News).

### Rare earths

This market is defined by Chinese supply control and a 2025 to 2026 weaponization of exports. China is the leading refiner for 19 of 20 strategic minerals at an average market share of 70 percent, and rare earth supply is the least geographically diversified of all critical minerals (IEA). China dominates approximately 60 percent of global rare earth mining and 91 percent of refining capacity (Mexico Business).

After two waves of export controls in April and October 2025, with the second wave suspended until November 2026 (European Parliament), European rare earth prices reached up to six times those in China (IEA). Western capacity is being mobilized but remains thin. In May 2025 Lynas became the first company outside China to produce commercial quantities of dysprosium oxide (CSIS), and the US Department of Defense invested $400 million in MP Materials in July 2025 with a 10-year offtake at a $110/kg NdPr price floor, about $50/kg above spot (RFF). Market size is modest in tonnage: the rare earth market is projected at 196.97 kilotons in 2025 rising to 273.30 kilotons by 2031, a 5.61 percent CAGR (Mordor Intelligence).

### Tailings (LiT's stated lead value proposition)

A large physical opportunity with early commercial adoption. Approximately 16 billion tons of tailings are produced every year, with an existing global inventory of around 282 billion tons, many containing copper, manganese, REE, or titanium (GLOBSEC).

Market sizings vary by definition. The mining tailings management market is valued at USD 18.85 billion in 2025, reaching USD 32.28 billion by 2035 at 5.53 percent (Global Growth Insights). Within the circular-economy framing, tailings reprocessing and metal recovery dominated with over 35 percent revenue share in 2024 of a circular-economy-in-mining market of USD 12.90 billion growing to USD 25.30 billion by 2033 at 8 percent (Grand View Research).

Adoption is still early-stage. Only a quarter of Qualified Persons surveyed have evaluated reprocessing of tailings, and implementation is limited (ScienceDirect). Capital is now moving: Hindustan Zinc announced a USD 437.5 million advanced tailings reprocessing plant in August 2025 (Grand View Research).

> **Perpensa risk vectors — Section 1**
>
> - **Demand: Moderate.** Structural long-term pull is real, but lithium price collapsed 80%+ and the 2026 balance is contested.
> - **Competition:** see Section 3.
> - **Technology/IP:** see Section 5.
> - **Adoption: Elevated.** Tailings reprocessing has been evaluated by only ~25% of qualified persons.
> - **Regulatory: Low / favorable.** China controls and US tailings programs create tailwinds.
> - **Execution/Capital: Elevated.** The pipeline of new lithium projects, the source of upfront-fee customers, collapsed to fewer than 10 feasibility studies in 2025.
>
> Note: LiT's 1% NSR royalty is levied on production value, which is directly exposed to the commodity-price volatility documented above.

## 2. State of adjacent target markets

### Direct lithium extraction (DLE)

The fastest-moving adjacent space, though oriented to brine rather than LiT's hard-rock and tailings focus. Over USD 3 billion has been committed to DLE projects globally since 2020 (GII Research). Market sizings run from USD 881.1 million in 2024 to USD 1,902.5 million by 2032 at 10.1 percent (Credence Research) up to USD 1.54 billion in 2026 to USD 5.72 billion by 2036 at 14.0 percent (FactMR).

Notably for LiT's commercial model, the technology-as-a-service or build-own-operate segment leads with 36.0 percent share, and lithium producers and miners lead the customer segment at 52.0 percent (FactMR). Players include EnergyX, with $450 million-plus committed and GM Ventures and POSCO backing; Standard Lithium, the only publicly traded pure-play DLE company; and Lilac Solutions, whose ceramic beads have 4,000-plus cycles of track record (NatureTechMemos).

### Mineral processing equipment and technology

Processing is increasingly the bottleneck. The biggest limitation in modern mining is no longer underground but above ground in the processing plant, where geology is transformed into industrial value, making processing technology providers and chemical refiners among the most influential companies in the next cycle (Mining South East Europe). This is the home turf of the incumbents profiled in Section 3.

### Tailings management and circular economy

Governance is tightening, which raises operator attention to the waste streams LiT targets. The Global Tailings Management Institute was established in January 2025, co-founded by ICMM, UNEP, and PRI, mandating third-party audits and elevating governance standards under the Global Industry Standard on Tailings Management (ConnectOre). Policy capital is flowing: the US Interior Department committed $1.2 billion to domestic supply-chain security through tailings valorisation, targeting 60 strategic commodities (Discovery Alert).

### REE refining and separation onshoring

A parallel adjacent build-out, mostly producer-model rather than licensing. Ucore Rare Metals is advancing proprietary RapidSX separation toward mid-2026 Louisiana production with DoD funding, and Energy Fuels restarted White Mesa Mill with REE separation capability in October 2025 (Crux Investor).

> **Perpensa risk vectors — Section 2**
>
> - **Demand: Low / favorable.** Adjacent markets are growing 8 to 14 percent.
> - **Competition: Elevated.** The adjacent technology-as-a-service and build-own-operate model in DLE competes commercially with a pure-licensing model for the same operator wallet.
> - **Technology/IP:** see Section 5.
> - **Adoption: Moderate.**
> - **Regulatory: Low / favorable.**
> - **Execution/Capital: Moderate.** Adjacent players are raising hundreds of millions; the capital-intensity benchmark is high.

## 3. Direct competitors: performance, structure, geography

No single company offers LiT's exact two-stage integrated package, but the two stages map onto well-populated competitive fields, and the licensing model has a clear precedent set.

### Stage One (fine/ultrafine separation) incumbents and challengers

A crowded equipment-and-technology field. The closest reference points:

| Company | Structure / HQ | Relevant tech | Scale signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Metso | Public (Nasdaq Helsinki), Espoo, Finland | Concorde Cell for fine/ultrafine; flotation, filtration, tailings, comminution | ~$5.91B trailing revenue, ~18,000 employees, ~50 countries (PitchBook, CompaniesHistory) |
| FLSmidth (FLS) | Public, Denmark | Full mineral-processing value chain | ~$712.1M quarterly revenue, Q2 2024 (Pestel-analysis) |
| Eriez | Privately held, Erie, PA, USA | HydroFloat, StackCell, column flotation | 1,000+ flotation systems and 1,200+ column installations worldwide (Eriez) |
| Woodgrove | Private, Canada | Staged Flotation Reactor (SFR), Direct Flotation Reactor (DFR) | Gaining interest in large-scale installations (E&MJ) |
| Glencore Technology | Subsidiary of Glencore | Jameson Cell, IsaMill | Regarded as essential components of modern flowsheets (E&MJ) |

A key technical distinction surfaced. Eriez's HydroFloat is engineered for coarse particle recovery, recovering particles exceeding 0.5 mm (SME Mining Engineering), whereas LiT claims fine and ultrafine recovery. The fine/ultrafine field is held by different products: the Jameson Cell for fine-particle recovery, the IsaMill for fine grinding, and Metso's Concorde Cell for fine and ultra-fine particle recovery (E&MJ). Note also a direct example of the flotation-licensing model: Graeme Jameson's latest invention, the NovaCell, is licensed to Jord International of Australia (MEI Online).

### Stage Two (low-temperature lithium extraction) competitors

Conventional processing runs hot; multiple lower-temperature alternatives exist and some are commercializing. The LieNA process, developed by Lithium Australia with ANSTO Minerals, reacts fine spodumene with caustic soda in an autoclave as an alternative to high-temperature conversion, and the Metso-Outotec high-pressure sodium carbonate leaching process is set for industrial-scale implementation at the Keliber project in 2025 (MDPI).

### Business-model precedent: catalytic surface-chemistry leaching in copper

The closest analog to LiT's combined value proposition (surface chemistry that alters mineral particles, deployed at existing mines, recovering otherwise-discarded metal) sits in copper.

- Jetti Resources developed a proprietary catalyst that alters the surface of crushed minerals to help acid extract copper, deployed commercially at Capstone Copper's Pinto Valley mine since 2019, recovering 40 to 70 percent from chalcopyrite versus 15 to 30 percent for conventional leaching (CB Insights). Jetti raised $50 million in a round led by Mitsubishi with Freeport-McMoRan, BHP Ventures, Orion Resource Partners, and T. Rowe Price (SMM). Colorado, USA; VC and strategic-backed; roughly $100M-plus raised in total.
- Ceibo, founded 2021, secured $36 million including BHP Ventures and began pilot tests with Glencore's Lomas Bayas in Chile (Grist). Chile and US; VC-backed.

### Tailings-recovery direct competitors (LiT's lead market)

The most-funded comparator runs a producer model, not licensing. Phoenix Tailings, founded 2018, extracts and refines rare earth metals and critical minerals from mining waste using a clean electrochemical process and has raised $117.8 million across four rounds (StartupIntros), backed by In-Q-Tel, BMW, Yamaha Motor Ventures, ARPA-E, and MIT, operating a commercial facility and constructing a New Hampshire plant to scale production (BusinessWire). The structural contrast matters: Phoenix is vertically integrated (build-own-operate refineries), where LiT proposes to license. Other tailings-recovery activity includes U.S. Strategic Metals reprocessing historic lead-zinc tailings at the Madison Mine in Missouri to recover cobalt, nickel, and copper (INL).

> **Perpensa risk vectors — Section 3**
>
> - **Demand:** see Section 1.
> - **Competition: High.** Fine/ultrafine flotation is held by Metso, Glencore, Eriez, and Woodgrove; low-temp lithium has LieNA and Metso-Outotec commercializing; tailings recovery has a $117M-funded Phoenix Tailings.
> - **Technology/IP:** see Section 5.
> - **Adoption: Elevated.** Incumbents are already embedded in operator flowsheets and procurement.
> - **Regulatory: Low.**
> - **Execution/Capital: High.** Competitors are backed by the exact majors LiT names as customers, for example BHP and Freeport funding Jetti and Ceibo.

## 4. Indirect competitors on the sidelines

### The majors' own R&D and venture arms

The customers LiT targets are also funding competing technology. BHP Ventures and Freeport-McMoRan are investors in Jetti (SMM), and the world's top lithium producers Albemarle and SQM planned to test their own DLE technologies, with carmakers GM and BMW backing technology developers (IEEE Spectrum). In-house and venture-funded substitutes therefore sit inside the same accounts LiT must sell into.

### Brine DLE as a lithium-supply substitute

Although feedstock-different, DLE competes for the same downstream lithium demand at recovery rates exceeding 90 percent versus 40 to 60 percent for conventional methods (GII Research), with backers across a 70-company field spanning Lilac Solutions, EnergyX, Standard Lithium, and IBAT (IDTechEx).

### Ligand and polymer-based recovery from dilute streams

A research-stage but federally funded substitute for recovering critical minerals from waste. ARPA-E's RECOVER program is funding Phoenix Tailings to create ligands that complex with critical minerals in dilute brine, and the University of Missouri to deploy AI-augmented ion-imprinted polymers to capture neodymium, praseodymium, and terbium from mining waste (ARPA-E).

### Coarse-particle flotation as a tailings-reduction substitute

Incumbent coarse-particle technology already markets tailings benefits. HydroFloat coarse particle recovery delivers higher water recovery and allows dry-stacked tailings instead of a tailings dam (SME Mining Engineering), addressing part of the same operator pain point from a different angle, with a HydroFloat plant commissioned at BHP's Carrapateena mine (E&MJ).

> **Perpensa risk vectors — Section 4**
>
> - **Demand:** see Section 1.
> - **Competition: High.** Substitution pressure comes from in-house major R&D and a 70-company DLE field.
> - **Technology/IP:** see Section 5.
> - **Adoption: Elevated.** Substitutes are funded inside target accounts.
> - **Regulatory: Low / favorable.** Federal money is flowing to multiple competing approaches simultaneously, including substitutes.
> - **Execution/Capital: High.**

## 5. IP required, and who holds similar IP

### What the idea requires

Two patentable cores: a surface-chemistry method for selective fine/ultrafine particle separation, and a low-temperature leaching method for lithium and, prospectively, REE. Both fields are heavily prior-arted.

### The Virginia Tech IP backbone

LiT partners with Virginia Tech's Department of Mining & Minerals Engineering, whose surface-chemistry separation IP is among the most developed in the world. Dr. Roe-Hoan Yoon, director of Virginia Tech's Center of Advanced Separation Technologies, has more than 70 U.S. and international patents, and mineral-processing manufacturers have licensed equipment, additives, and processes based on his inventions to produce billions of dollars of raw materials (Minerals Refining Company).

The most relevant is the hydrophobic-hydrophilic separation process. Yoon's hydrophobic-hydrophilic separation (HHS) process was developed for fine particle recovery and licensed to Minerals Refining Company in Richmond, Virginia, for worldwide distribution (Virginia Tech News), and technical materials describe HHS as having no lower particle size limit (NETL). Assignment sits with the university's IP vehicle: patents naming Yoon, Gerald Luttrell, and others are assigned to Virginia Tech Intellectual Properties, Inc. (Justia Patents).

This raises a freedom-to-operate and ownership question, since overlapping fine-particle surface-chemistry methods are already patented and at least one branch (HHS) carries a worldwide license to a third party, Minerals Refining Company.

### Low-temperature lithium IP is crowded

The "below 500°C, no acidic byproducts" claim sits in an active patent-and-publication field. A two-stage NaOH roasting at 325°C followed by room-temperature water leaching has been published with over 99 percent lithium extraction, eliminating the need for 1100°C calcination and sulfuric acid baking (ScienceDirect), and a patented direct lithium extraction process from alpha-spodumene using low-temperature NaOH roasting, room-temperature water leaching, and CO2 precipitation reduces global warming potential by 59 percent (ScienceDirect). Additional low-temperature composite-salt routes are published, for example synergistic roasting of alpha-spodumene with lepidolite that reduces the phase-transition temperature by 150°C (ScienceDirect).

### LiT's own filed IP

LiT's prospectus claims an internationally filed PCT patent. Two facts from the public record temper the framing. First, LiT's own website page describing its patent position is hosted at a "provisionalpatent" path and states the company is currently in its seed round of fundraising (lithiumtechnologies.co), consistent with provisional-stage rather than granted IP. Second, a public-database search did not surface a published LiT PCT, which is expected if the application is recent, since PCT applications publish roughly 18 months after the earliest priority date; the search instead returned unrelated third-party lithium-processing patents. A PCT application establishes a filing date and a 30-month national-phase runway; it is not a granted, enforceable patent in any jurisdiction.

> **Perpensa risk vectors — Section 5**
>
> - **Demand:** see Section 1.
> - **Competition:** see Section 3.
> - **Technology/IP: High.** The fine-particle surface-chemistry core overlaps Virginia Tech IP already licensed worldwide to a third party; the low-temperature lithium core is densely published and partly patented by others; LiT's own position appears to be provisional or PCT-stage, not granted.
> - **Adoption: Moderate.** Academic validation strengthens negotiations but does not confer freedom to operate.
> - **Regulatory: Low.**
> - **Execution/Capital: Elevated.** IP prosecution across US, Australia, India, and Brazil is budgeted at $235K, modest for contested multi-jurisdiction art.

## "And more": three cross-cutting items the questions did not name

1. **Royalty base is commodity-price-exposed.** LiT's revenue is a 1% NSR royalty on production value. Section 1 documents that the principal feedstock, lithium, saw price swings from about $8,259 to about $26,278 per ton inside twelve months. The royalty mechanism inherits that volatility directly.

2. **Customer concentration overlaps competitor funders.** The named primary targets (BHP, Freeport, Glencore, Teck) are, on the record, also financial backers or pilot partners of competing process technologies (Jetti, Ceibo) and operators of incumbent fine-particle equipment (Metso Concorde at scale, Eriez HydroFloat at BHP Carrapateena).

3. **Model-structure divergence from the best-funded tailings comparator.** The most-capitalized tailings-recovery company, Phoenix Tailings at $117.8 million, runs a vertically integrated producer model that captures the full metal margin, while LiT proposes to capture 1 percent via licensing. These are different value-capture architectures competing for the same "recover critical minerals from waste" narrative and the same federal and policy tailwinds.
