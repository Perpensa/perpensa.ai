# Lithium Technologies, Inc.: Execution Plan

## The plan in one view

LiT executes its strategy as a sequence of phases separated by hard go/no-go decisions, with capital raised one tranche at a time against those same decisions, the team sized to the scope each phase actually carries, every major risk assigned to a single accountable owner and pinned to the phase that owns it, and the work inside each phase ordered so that no dollar and no disclosure ever runs ahead of the result it depends on. The architecture is built on one rule applied everywhere: nothing crosses into the next phase, and no capital is committed to it, until the current phase has cleared its gate on evidence a skeptic would accept.

There are three phases on the main line and two branches held in reserve.

| Phase | What it does | Team | Capital | Cleared by |
|-------|--------------|------|---------|------------|
| **Phase one: gate-clearing** | Secure the right to license and validate the recovery uplift. No revenue. | Two principals plus contracted counsel and pilot capability. No permanent hires. | First tranche (the re-purposed seed), sized at or above $2.0M with a ring-fenced reserve. | The foundation gate: an executed, clear license and a validated uplift that clears the economics. |
| **Phase two: single-site beachhead** | Sign and bring into production exactly one well-chosen site; prove the model covers its own cost. | Add a business-development lead and a technical-integration engineer, both brought forward. On-site work crewed by the operator. | Second tranche, opened only by the foundation gate. | The proof gate: one live site's royalty covers the fixed cost of the deal. |
| **Phase three: disciplined widening** | Add feedstocks and sites one validated increment at a time. | Incremental capacity added per cleared increment. | Per-increment tranches, each raised against that increment's proof. | A per-increment gate, fired once for each new feedstock or site class. |
| **Reserve: services bridge** | Non-dilutive cash, run only with the rights protected. | Self-funded delivery only. | Self-financing. | Available if the rights question stalls and a defensible disclosure position exists. |
| **Reserve: strategic sale** | Sell a de-risked position from strength. | Transaction advisors. | A transaction, not an operating raise. | A deliberate post-gate decision. |

The remainder of this document specifies each phase, the decision at each gate and exactly what it is measured against, the team build and the capital draw mapped onto the phases, the owner and home phase of every significant risk, and the order in which the work must be done.

## Phase one: the gate-clearing program

The first phase has one mandate and no other: clear the two conditions on which the entire business rests, and spend on nothing else. Secure the right to license the technology, and validate the recovery uplift. This phase produces no revenue by design. It runs to a pre-committed budget and a pre-committed kill date, and any capital not spent is returned to investors or redeployed if either condition fails to hold. Standing here without spending past it is the lowest-risk posture available to the company; clearing the conditions and moving is the only acceptable way out of it. The phase is time-boxed precisely so that a disciplined start cannot decay into indefinite limbo.

Two workstreams run in parallel, because their clocks are independent and neither needs to wait on the other.

**The rights workstream.** Engage patent counsel, commission a freedom-to-operate opinion covering the minerals field, and negotiate and execute an exclusive, sublicensable, field-of-use license from the university that names the minerals application and clears the prior license granted to another party. The university's own royalty is modeled into LiT's economics rather than left off the books. This is a third-party legal event LiT can influence but cannot unilaterally control, which is exactly why it is allowed to run on its own clock and is never permitted to delay the start of the validation work.

**The validation workstream.** Secure a host-site access-and-liability agreement with an active operator that holds title to its impoundment, characterize that impoundment, run a continuous pilot on representative feedstock, and measure the recovery uplift in percentage points against the operator's real current recovery on that material, at or above the level the royalty economics require. The work runs under the access agreement so that taking samples does not create environmental-cleanup standing for LiT.

Alongside these two workstreams, the company completes a set of low-cost housekeeping items that remove self-inflicted weaknesses before any counterparty or investor sees the model: re-base the royalty onto the incremental recovered value the company actually creates, with the whole-mine notional retained only as a labeled ceiling; restate the financial model so it is internally consistent and de-stacked, correcting a two-year timing error and labeling the optimistic case as upside only; hold back any environmental-benefit claim until the pilot result exists to support it; and obtain a royalty-benchmarking dataset so the eventual rate is a benchmarked range rather than an unanchored number.

**Two internal checkpoints govern spending inside this phase.**

- *The rights-path checkpoint*, at roughly month four to six. Before the full pilot budget is committed, a freedom-to-operate opinion exists and is not adverse, and a credible, costed path to the executed license is in hand that fits inside the phase budget and the kill date. If instead the prior license forecloses the field with no obtainable carve-out, the program stops here at minimum spend.
- *The host-site checkpoint*. No sampling and no piloting begins until an access-and-liability agreement with an active operator is executed and keeps title and remediation standing with that operator. The check is not satisfied by the contract alone: it requires an environmental and mining counsel opinion that the structure survives third-party and successor-liability theories under the statute governing that specific site, not merely that the contract allocates liability to the operator between the two signing parties. A contractual allocation does not bind a regulator or a third-party claimant, and a site where the structure works only on paper between the parties while leaving LiT statutorily reachable is treated as an uncontainable-liability site. If no host site is reachable on containable-liability terms, the company holds or re-selects; it does not self-operate and does not sample an orphaned impoundment.

**This phase builds no permanent staff.** Its defining property is that unspent capital must be returnable if a gate fails, and money sunk into salaried headcount is not returnable. The phase therefore runs on the two existing principals plus contracted, scope-bounded specialists: patent counsel for the license and the freedom-to-operate opinion, and a pilot and metallurgical capability drawn through the university relationship and a research engagement that must itself be converted from an assumption into an executed, costed instrument during this phase. No business-development hire is made here, because there is nothing to sell until the gate clears; paying for a sales pipeline the company is not yet entitled to build would be premature. Keeping the phase staffed by contract rather than payroll is not only cheaper, it is what keeps the option to wind down real.

**The kill date.** The phase is bounded by both a budget and a calendar, and whichever binds first triggers the decision. The first tranche is the maximum spend, and the reserve inside it is ring-fenced for an orderly wind-down or a single re-scope-and-retry, so the money spent pursuing the gate cannot consume the money that funds a clean exit. The calendar bound is a pre-committed date, which we judge should fall twelve to eighteen months from the funding of the first tranche, with the rights-path checkpoint at roughly month four to six. The original plan booked a first license at around month twenty-five, which is an aggressive date; the gate-clearing that must precede any deal therefore has to complete well inside that window. The exact position of the date depends on how long a continuous pilot for this recovery chemistry actually takes, which is an input the company must firm up and scope before the kill date is fixed, so that the window is sized to the work rather than the work forced into the window. A calendar set without reference to the real run-time can kill a validation that was on track to succeed. The essential discipline is that the kill date is set earlier than the point at which the seed runs out, so that capital remains to execute the chosen fail action. A kill date that coincides with an empty bank account is not a kill date.

One protection makes the kill date real rather than aspirational. A confident, fully built program creates its own momentum, and the characteristic way a gated plan fails is that the kill date arrives and is moved because too much has been built to stop, so the gate that was designed as a brake operates as a ratchet. The defense is structural: the kill date and the ring-fenced reserve are enforced by a party with no incentive to continue, an investor or board holding return rights over unspent capital, rather than by the management team that built the program. The authority to stop sits outside the room that wants to go.

## The foundation gate

This is the entry condition to the entire commercial business, and it is the decision the whole plan converges on. Until it is passed, LiT has nothing it can sell to anyone, and no capital for scale is committed. Two readings must both be true, on acceptable terms, inside the budget and before the kill date, and neither one can compensate for a weakness in the other.

**The rights reading.** A fully executed license that is exclusive in the minerals field, sublicensable, defined to name the minerals application, and confirmed against the freedom-to-operate opinion to clear or be unblocked by the prior license. A license missing any one of these four properties does not pass. This reading is confirmed by external counsel and the freedom-to-operate opinion, not by management's own assessment.

**The uplift reading.** A recovery uplift, measured in percentage points against the named baseline, validated on a continuous pilot run that reaches and holds steady operation, at or above the break-even level the royalty economics require, and measured on the same incremental basis the royalty will actually be charged against. A batch test does not pass; the uplift must be demonstrated on a continuous run, because a batch result does not retire the risk that the process works at scale. Continuity over time is necessary but not sufficient: the pilot must also span the representative mineralogical and grade range of the target impoundment rather than a single favorable composite held in steady state, and the threshold must be met on the harder end of that range, because the operator's plant will draw the whole distribution over a production year and not the average. This reading is certified by an independent pilot with assays at an accredited laboratory, attesting to the feed variability actually covered and not only to the steady-state hours logged, and not graded by the company itself.

The two readings are deliberately kept separate and are never blended into a single score. A strong uplift does not buy a weak rights position, and an executed license does not make up for an uplift that falls short. Both are independent points of failure, and the gate treats them that way.

If the gate clears, the company enters Phase two. If the rights cannot be secured by the kill date, the company winds down and returns or redeploys unspent capital, or, only if it holds a genuinely defensible disclosure position, runs the services bridge while the rights question is decided; the options to proceed to a beachhead are off the table, not merely delayed. If the uplift validates below the required level, the company does not enter Phase two at the modeled rate; it re-scopes to a site or feedstock where the validated uplift clears, or it winds down. The headline resource figures remain stated as dependent on per-site assay regardless of the outcome.

## Phase two: the single-site beachhead

Entered only after the foundation gate clears. The mandate is to sign and bring into production exactly one license, on one deliberately chosen active-operator site, and to prove that the commercial model can be sold, delivered, and made to cover its own cost. The goal is to prove the model, not yet the portfolio.

The work runs in a fixed order.

**Site selection** for two properties at once: a high assayed grade, and a demand-led mineral basket the market actually wants. The site belongs to an active operator, never to an abandoned or orphaned property. Although only one site is actively pursued, the company qualifies and ranks a small backup set of two or three host-site candidates rather than betting the venture on a single counterparty's clock. Narrow-first concentration is the right scope decision; single-counterparty dependency with no fallback is the failure version of it. An explicit deal-by date is set inside the runway, well before exhaustion, that automatically triggers either re-selection to the next ranked candidate or the services bridge, so a stalled lead is discovered as a date rather than as an empty bank account.

**Deal structure** as the licensed process running inside the operator's own plant. Title to recovered material and remediation liability stay with the operator who already holds them; LiT never takes title to recovered material and never takes operator standing on the impoundment. This single structural choice is the most important risk decision in the phase, because it contains environmental liability and competitive exposure at the same time. Before any candidate site is signed, this structure must be confirmed securable at that specific site, with title and remediation staying with the operator and with an environmental and mining counsel opinion that the structure survives third-party and successor-liability theories under that site's governing statute, not merely that the contract allocates liability between the parties. A site that cannot be structured this way, or that leaves LiT statutorily reachable despite a clean contract, is rejected and another active-operator site is selected; the company never lets a single deal pull it onto the self-operate or orphaned-site path the positioning exists to avoid. This check fires for every candidate, every time, before signing.

**Pricing discipline.** The first rate is set and communicated as a pilot reference price, not as the rate that anchors the entire future portfolio. A single early deal struck before the company has a track record is a weak place from which to set the price of everything that follows. Calling the rate a reference does not make it one to the next counterparty, so two protections are built into the first contract rather than left to intention. The benchmarked rate band must be in hand before the deal is signed, not merely before the model is built, so the reference is positioned against a benchmark instead of invented. And the low first rate is contractually bounded as a pilot rate by time or volume, with a pre-agreed step-up or a most-favored-nation protection, so it cannot become a permanent floor that every later procurement negotiates down from. The deal is also tested against price, not only at spot: before it is signed it must clear coverage at a pre-committed conservative downside price as well as at prevailing price, and the company seeks a rate floor or minimum-payment leg wherever the operator will grant one. This does not remove the commodity-price exposure, which is structural and is never represented as removed; it ensures the company does not enter widening on a number that only the top of the cycle supports.

**Technical integration**, incurred once, with the cost of site characterization priced into the deal. Burn is held to a level a single royalty stream can plausibly carry, because the test of this phase is whether that stream covers its own cost.

**The two hires that the company needs most arrive here, brought forward.** The original organizational plan placed the dedicated technical-integration engineer, the role that produces the verified-recovery result on which renewals depend, only in year five, against a headline plan of sixteen licensees with concurrent pilots. The narrow-first path runs exactly one integration on one site, which retires most of that load by construction, and it is precisely that reduction in load that makes it affordable to bring the two execution-critical roles forward to the first deal: a business-development lead to run the single-operator sale and structure the deal, and the technical-integration engineer to run the in-plant integration and own the verified-recovery result. Moving the integration engineer from the sixteenth deal to the first is the single most important correction the plan makes to the original organization design. The single-site scope is what allows one business-development lead, rather than a team, to carry this phase.

**On-site work is crewed by the operator, not by LiT.** Because the licensed process runs inside the operator's own permitted plant, the operator's own workforce runs the recovery circuit day to day, with periodic technical support from LiT's integration engineer. LiT does not build a standing operating crew. This honors the asset-light, no-client-capital posture the fundability case depends on. The company should plan, conservatively, for on the order of 0.2 to 0.5 of an engineer's time per live site in steady operation, with near-continuous support during the commissioning window of each new site, stepping down as the operator's crew matures. One integration engineer can therefore support roughly two to five live sites at once. This planning figure is deliberately set toward the higher support cost until the first site produces a measured figure, because it feeds directly into the test of whether a deal covers its own cost, and understating it would flatter that test.

## The proof gate

One site is signed and producing, and the question is whether the single royalty stream covers the fixed cost of doing the deal, at the validated uplift and the re-based rate, measured on actual production rather than on a model. The gate distinguishes three outcomes deliberately, and the company pre-commits to the dividing line before the result is known so the line cannot be redrawn afterward.

- **The site over-delivers.** The stream covers its cost with real margin, and the site beats the conservative case on both the validated uplift and the economics. This, and only this, authorizes funding the widening phase on a staged raise, and authorizes considering federal anchoring of the recovery position. Broadening is earned by proof.
- **The site clears but does not over-deliver.** The stream covers its cost but does not beat the conservative case. The company holds at one site, does not widen, and reassesses. The plan does not widen on a marginal pass.
- **The site does not cover its own cost.** The company holds at one site and does not widen. It reassesses whether any asset-light royalty path is viable, or pivots to a strategic sale from the now-de-risked position.

One further condition runs throughout this phase independently of the cost test. If adoption proves materially slower than even the conservative case, with runway exposed, the company does not fund widening; it extends runway through the non-dilutive services bridge and the post-gate sale option. The response to a slow market is more runway, not more scope. And if a competitor or a government program threatens to foreclose the position before the first deal signs, the company accelerates the strategic-sale decision while its position still has scarcity value, rather than trying to out-spend a larger rival.

## Phase three: disciplined widening

Entered only when the first site over-delivers, and only against a staged raise tied to proof milestones, with an absolute line against ever using LiT's own capital for client deployments. The full platform, spanning multiple feedstocks, additional sites, and the broader recovery chemistry, is the destination, but it is reached by repeated validated increments, never opened all at once.

Each new feedstock or site class is its own increment and carries its own gate. An increment enters the commercial offer only after it is independently validated on its own continuous pilot, against capital already raised for that increment on its own proof milestone, and only once the in-plant liability structure is confirmed securable for that increment's site. The multi-feedstock breadth the company ultimately intends is unproven today, so no increment inherits another's validation, characterization, or capital; each earns its own. The widening loop has no fixed terminal count. It continues as long as each increment clears its gate and the staged raises remain available. The team grows only as far as proven, funded scope has reached; the year-five headcount endpoint of the original plan is reached, if at all, as the sum of validated increments, never as a curve drawn ahead of revenue.

Federal anchoring of the recovery position is considered only from inside this phase, after the first over-delivering proof. One caution travels with it: broadening the technology base through an allied research partnership can weaken the company's intellectual-property defensibility, and that cost must be weighed honestly rather than netted into a single favorable number.

## The two reserved branches, and what the plan excludes

**The services bridge** is available during the first two phases as a non-dilutive source of cash, and it is the only coherent way for the company to earn under a stall in the rights question. It is governed by one absolute precondition: an executed option-to-license with intellectual-property-assignment and confidentiality terms must be in hand, and the rights position must be far enough along that LiT has something defensible to disclose, before any technical disclosure is made. Run in the other order, a services engagement lets the customer learn the process without LiT holding the right to stop them using it, which sells the proof and keeps the risk. It is a financing posture, not the business model, and it never becomes the main line.

**The strategic sale** is reserved for a deliberate decision taken after the foundation gate, and ideally after the proof gate, when the company can sell a de-risked, proven position from strength and be paid for the value it has created. It is reachable from a marginal or failed proof gate, from adoption too slow to fund widening, or from a position being foreclosed by a competitor. It is never taken before the foundation gate, where it would sell an unvalidated process behind an unsecured right and let a sophisticated buyer keep the de-risking discount or route directly to the university.

**The plan explicitly excludes building, owning, and operating recovery plants, or entering a producer joint venture that puts LiT in that role.** That path inverts every containment decision the plan rests on, takes on the maximum environmental and title liability the company has worked to avoid, abandons the no-client-capital line the fundability case depends on, and exposes the company to the full commodity-price cycle with no royalty floor. It also rests on a premise that does not hold, that an operator would fund the capital cost of a plant built on an unvalidated process when it could fund a proven one instead. It is named here only to keep the declined trade-off explicit. It is not a phase, a branch, or a fallback.

## The capital draw, mapped to the gates

The capital structure and the phase structure are the same structure, drawn twice. The single most-cited weakness in the company's financing is that the $2.0M seed is a single tranche with no reserve, which collapses the moment any spend overruns or any deployment capital becomes LiT's. The cure is to make each tranche unlock at the same hard gate that unlocks the next phase. A tranche is not released by the calendar or by confidence; it is released by a documented gate pass.

**The first tranche** is the re-purposed seed and funds gate-clearing through the foundation gate. It carries the rights work, the validation pilot, lean principal operating cost for the time-boxed phase, the low-cost model and benchmarking housekeeping, and, critically, a named reserve for a failed first pilot or a forced re-scope, which the current single-tranche structure lacks. The cited financial base gives three hard line items against which only a $52K university study has been spent: an intellectual-property budget of roughly $235K, a pilot budget of roughly $700K, and the $2.0M seed. Both the rights line and the pilot line should be sized up from these figures, because the rights line must carry a contested multi-jurisdiction negotiation and a freedom-to-operate opinion, and the pilot line must carry a continuous rather than a batch pilot on representative feedstock.

The honest tension, which the company should face rather than paper over, is that $235K and $700K together already consume nearly half of a $2.0M seed before any operating cost or reserve. Sizing those line items up to their real contested scope and carving a genuine reserve pushes the responsible first raise to at or above the current $2.0M, or else forces the kill date in tight. The recommendation is to size the first tranche to the contested scope plus the reserve and raise to that number, rather than hold the $2.0M figure and let the reserve be the line item that quietly gets cut. We judge the adequacy of the seed as currently structured to be marginal, and the right response is to size the raise honestly, not to assume the figure is sufficient. One further discipline protects the reserve from being spent under schedule pressure: a breach of the ring-fenced reserve is itself a kill trigger, not a financeable overrun. The reserve cannot be quietly converted into pilot-completion money, because the moment it is drawn for anything but an orderly wind-down or a single re-scope, the clean-exit option it exists to fund has already been lost.

**The second tranche** is not raised, and not spent against, until the foundation gate passes. It funds the two Phase-two hires, the one-time technical integration at the first site, and the per-site characterization priced into the deal. It is held to a level a single validated royalty stream can plausibly carry, because proving exactly that is the purpose of the phase. Its precise size depends on the integration footprint at the first site, which the company will know more about once it characterizes that site. A cleared gate is not the same as a funded next phase, so the company works to soft-circle or condition this tranche before the foundation gate clears. The intent is that clearing the gate releases capital that already exists in commitment, rather than capital the company must then go and raise into whatever financing market happens to exist that quarter. The honest version of the plan says the seed is marginal; the honest contingency is that the next raise must be lined up before, not after, the gate that is supposed to unlock it.

**The per-increment tranches** in Phase three are each raised only against the specific increment's proof milestone, on a staged basis. No single raise opens the full platform.

Across every tranche and every phase, one line is non-negotiable: no LiT capital funds client deployment. The in-plant licensing structure is what makes this enforceable, because the operator funds and owns the plant the process runs in, so deployment capital sits with the party that holds the asset and the liability. Any deal that would require LiT to co-fund deployment capital is off the path, the same way the build-own-operate model is off the path. This line is the load-bearing assumption of the entire fundability case.

## How each milestone is measured

A gate that is tested against confidence rather than evidence is not a gate. Each decision point therefore measures a specific quantity, against a pre-set threshold, certified by a named party. Two of these tests carry the weight of the plan and deserve to be stated precisely.

**The break-even uplift at the foundation gate.** The measured quantity is the incremental recovery the LiT process achieves over the operator's current recovery on the same representative feedstock, in percentage points, on a continuous pilot. The threshold it must beat is the uplift at which one site's annual royalty covers Phase-two annual fixed cost. Stated as a formula the company parameterizes at the gate from its own figures:

> break-even uplift = annual fixed cost / ( royalty rate × site throughput × grade × payable fraction × commodity price )

The measured uplift must meet or exceed this computed break-even level on the same incremental basis the royalty is charged against. Of the inputs, the royalty rate is bounded by a benchmarked band running from low single digits up to roughly 5% of the recovered stream; the commodity price is established and carries full commodity-price exposure, which is why the break-even level moves with price and the test is re-run if price moves materially; and the site's throughput, grade, and payable fraction, along with the company's actual phase burn, are figures LiT supplies at the gate from the characterized site and its own books. We are deliberately specifying the test rather than inventing a single pass number, because the inputs that set the number are the company's to supply and the site's to determine. The point is that the gate cannot be cleared by a general claim that the process works; it is cleared only when a specific measured uplift beats a specific computed break-even on a like-for-like basis.

**The coverage ratio at the proof gate.** The measured quantity is the ratio of the one live site's annualized royalty revenue to the annual fixed cost of running that deal, including the LiT support time and the amortized one-time integration, computed on actual production. A ratio comfortably above one, beating the conservative case, authorizes widening. A ratio at or just above one means hold at one site. A ratio below one means hold and reassess or pivot to a sale. The over-deliver line that separates the first outcome from the second is committed before the phase begins, so it cannot be moved after the result is in.

The remaining checkpoints are binary on documents and structures existing: a non-adverse freedom-to-operate opinion and a costed license path at the rights-path checkpoint; an executed access-and-liability agreement that keeps title and remediation with the operator at the host-site checkpoint; the four-part license test at the foundation gate; and the in-plant liability structure confirmed securable before each site is signed. Each widening increment re-runs a scoped version of the break-even uplift test and the liability check on its own feedstock and its own site. Where a figure remains genuinely open, notably the company's own burn, the target site's tonnage and grade, the steady-state support time per site, and the continuous-pilot run-time, the plan carries it as a named input the company must supply rather than as an invented number.

The plan therefore concentrates its risk reduction on a small number of high-leverage structural choices rather than spreading it thin across many independent controls.

## The order of work, and the critical path

The plan is a sequence, but inside each phase several streams run at once, and a handful of them are strict prerequisites of others. Getting that order wrong is its own failure mode, because spending the pilot budget before the host-site liability is contained, pricing a deal before the site is characterized, or raising capital against a model that still carries a known error each spends scarce money or credibility on a foundation that is not yet there. Four hard prerequisites carry the sequencing. Each is enforced as a documented, externally witnessed artifact that the next spend literally cannot be released against, mirroring the tranche-to-gate capital lock, so that a prerequisite which lives in the plan cannot be quietly softened to hold a date. The full pilot budget releases only against a signed freedom-to-operate opinion and a signed access-and-liability agreement with the operator's title independently verified; the first rate is priced only against a signed characterization report; and any disclosure under the services bridge runs only against an executed option-to-license.

**The model is restated, and a preliminary rights read is taken, before the first tranche is raised against it.** The de-stacking, the timing correction, the labeling of the upside, the re-base, and the benchmarking are all cheap, fully within the company's control, and depend on nothing external, so they sit at the very front, before the funding clock even starts. The first raise is made on the strength of the model a counterparty sees; if that model still carries a known error when the raise opens, a sophisticated investor who finds it discounts everything else with it. The company spends its credibility before its capital if it does this in the wrong order. Alongside the model work, a cheap preliminary rights and freedom-to-operate read is obtained before the first tranche is raised, so that a clearly adverse read stops the raise rather than stopping a pilot already in flight. This sits ahead of the fuller freedom-to-operate opinion at the rights-path checkpoint, and it is the earliest and cheapest place the single most serious risk can be caught.

**Both internal checkpoints clear before the full pilot budget is committed.** The pilot is the largest single cost in the first phase, and it is not funded until the company knows two things the pilot itself cannot establish: that there is a credible path to actually owning the right to commercialize the result, and that there is a containable-liability site on which to generate it. Spending the pilot budget before either is in hand risks producing a validated uplift the company has no right to commercialize, or generating it by taking on the liability the plan is built to avoid.

**Per-site characterization precedes any rate being priced.** The throughput, grade, and payable fraction that set both the break-even test and any quoted rate are site-specific outputs of characterization, not headline resource figures. Characterization must sample the impoundment's spatial and draw-sequence variability sufficiently to produce a distribution, not a single representative number, because a one-time snapshot under-represents the grade the plant actually draws over a production life. The deal is then priced off a conservative percentile of that distribution rather than the headline assay, and the break-even and coverage tests are both computed on that conservative grade. No rate is priced, and the break-even test cannot even be computed, until the site is characterized to a distribution. This rule starts at the first host site and re-fires at every site and every increment thereafter.

**Rights are executed far enough to be defensible before any disclosure under the services bridge.** This is the one place where a sequencing error would hand away the entire asset. An executed option-to-license with assignment and confidentiality terms must be in hand before the process is disclosed to a services counterparty. Even the contingency that exists to survive a rights problem cannot run until a minimum rights position is secured.

The two first-phase workstreams run as independent parallel clocks deliberately, so that the calendar is the longer of the two rather than the sum, and they rejoin at the foundation gate, where both the rights reading and the uplift reading must clear and neither nets into the other. The longer of the two clocks sets the gate date, which must fall inside the twelve-to-eighteen-month window. The validation clock is the likelier driver of the calendar, because it carries the longest chain of dependent steps, host-site agreement, then characterization, then a continuous pilot run to steady operation, then the measured uplift, and the pilot run-time is the open quantity that most directly sets where in the window the gate lands. The rights clock is the likelier driver of risk even when it is not the longest, because it is a third-party legal event the company influences but does not control, and it can stall at the carve-out regardless of how fast LiT moves. The sequence is built so that the cheap, fast rights read happens at the early checkpoint, before the expensive validation spend, so that a fatal rights problem is found at minimum cost. The whole path is positioned so that the two things most likely to kill the business are both tested inside the window, with the cheap legal read and the liability check both clearing before the largest spend, and with the kill date set early enough that capital still remains to execute the fail action whenever it lands.

## The alternative view

This plan treats the two foundational questions, the right to license and the validated uplift, as conditions to clear in a funded first phase before any scope is committed. An alternative view holds that they should be treated as strict pass/fail preconditions and nothing more, and that until both are in hand the company should make no commitment to the plan as framed, on the reasoning that presenting a confident, fully built program before those two facts are settled risks reading as permission to proceed when it is not.

The execution architecture is built to satisfy both readings, and the agreement between them is the spine of the plan. The first phase is genuinely a stop-unless-cleared program, with a real kill date, a real budget, the return of unspent capital if either condition fails, and capital that cannot cross a hard gate any more than scope can. No scope is committed and no scale capital is spent until both conditions clear on acceptable terms. The two views differ on language and posture, not on the sequence: both hold that nothing commits until the gates clear, and this plan holds that line in its structure rather than only in its intentions.

## What remains open

A small number of inputs are genuinely not yet established, and the plan carries them as named inputs the company must supply rather than as assumptions dressed as facts. The company's own monthly burn, the target site's tonnage and grade, the steady-state support time per live site, and the continuous-pilot run-time for this recovery chemistry each feed a gate test and each must be firmed up: the first three as the company characterizes its first site and finalizes its budget, the last as it scopes the pilot. The first site's commissioning will replace the conservative support-time planning band with a measured figure that then sets the load for every later increment. None of these open inputs changes the architecture; each is the kind of number that is supplied when the work it measures is done, and the plan is built so that no decision is taken on an invented value in the meantime.
