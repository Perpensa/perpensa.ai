# CR-10 — Evaluation-to-signature timeline and deployment realities at operating mines

Commissioned research, Round 00 gate. Priority P3. Merges R-MILL-1, R-MILL-2.
Questions: (a) typical evaluation-to-signature timeline for adopting a new processing technology at a major mining operator (pilot on operator feedstock through procurement); (b) what "mobile/modular" mineral-processing deployment realistically involves in footprint, throughput, and on-site staffing.

Disposition per register: **Commission** what public case studies show; expect partial coverage. Done below. This calibrates the sales-cycle and integration assumptions behind Month-25 first license and the two-person team.

## Bottom line for the deliberation

The public record splits LiT's adoption question into two very different clocks. **Reaching a signed commercial agreement at a major operator is slow** (multiple years, gated by the industry's documented conservatism and the non-standardized nature of each mine), but **physical deployment after signature can be fast** (roughly three months) when the technology is pre-engineered into a containerized, modular unit. Jetti Resources is the cleanest analog: it first deployed at Capstone's Pinto Valley in 2019, and even today only **3 of its 23 pipeline projects are transitioning to commercial** with 5 in active pilot, despite blue-chip backing from BHP and Freeport. Its "Jetti in a Box" containerized product is what compresses post-agreement deployment to about three months. The deployment-realities question resolves favorably for LiT's modular pitch: containerized mineral-processing units have small footprints, run 1-200 tph (grinding-limited to ~15 tph per module), ship in roughly nine sea containers, and need only light civil foundations. The unflattering finding is the sales cycle, not the hardware. Month-25 first-license is **aggressive** against multi-year operator evaluation timelines, and the two-person team cannot simultaneously run a long enterprise sales cycle, operate a pilot, and stand up on-site deployments. This feeds Execution sequencing and the team-capacity Weakness.

## What the case studies and literature establish (Cited / Assumption)

### The slow clock: evaluation to signature
- Mining is **documented as conservative and slow to adopt** new technology, driven by high capital intensity, physical and commodity-price risk, geographic dispersion, and the **non-standardized nature of each mine** (a flowsheet proven at one site is not plug-and-play at the next). These are structural, not incidental, barriers. [Cited — ScienceDirect "Barriers and enablers of technology adoption in the mining industry"; Canadian Mining Journal "technology adoption problem"; NTWIST]
- **Jetti Resources** first deployed at **Pinto Valley (Capstone) in 2019** and remains there. As of recent reporting it has a **23-project pipeline with 5 active pilots and only 3 opportunities transitioning to commercial status**, despite strategic investment from **BHP and Freeport-McMoRan**. This is the proportion to internalize: even a celebrated, well-funded process-technology company converts pilots to commercial slowly and selectively. [Cited — Jetti Resources; MINING.COM; Fortune]
- **Assumption (moderate-high confidence):** from first operator contact to a signed commercial agreement at a major operator runs **multiple years**, dominated by the operator's risk-qualification, not by LiT's readiness. A single-pilot-to-first-license in ~25 months is at the optimistic edge and depends on finding an unusually motivated early adopter (or a smaller/distressed operator with more incentive to try new recovery economics).

### The fast clock: deployment after agreement
- Jetti's **"Jetti in a Box"** encloses the core dosing process in a single containerized unit and **deploys within ~3 months of commercial agreement**, by design reducing on-site complexity and civil work. The lesson for LiT: productize the deployment, and post-signature integration stops being the bottleneck. [Cited — Jetti Resources; im-mining.com; mining.com]
- This validates a modular delivery model for LiT but **does not shorten the sales cycle**; it only de-risks what happens after a yes.

### Deployment realities: footprint, throughput, staffing
- **Footprint:** modular/containerized units have small footprints (example unit ~2.3 m wide, ~5 m high) needing only **light civil foundations**; plants ship in **roughly nine sea containers** and are assembled on site with minimal civil work, enabling relocation. [Cited — Sepro Systems; SGS Bateman; Mineral Technologies FlexSeries]
- **Throughput:** individual modules run **1-200 tph**, scaled by adding modules in parallel, but **grinding/comminution modules are weight- and size-limited to ~15 tph** of rock per module, and full turnkey modular plants are typically pitched at **500-2,000 t/d**. So a modular LiT unit is plausible at pilot-to-small-commercial scale, but whole-mine throughput at a major operator means many modules or a fixed-plant integration, not one mobile skid. [Cited — Sepro Systems; at-minerals.com mobile equipment review]
- **On-site staffing:** the public modular-plant sources **do not quantify operating headcount**; containerization reduces installation labor but operating a continuous circuit on an operator's site still requires trained operators and LiT technical oversight. **NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL / Assumption (low confidence):** a two-person company cannot crew on-site operations directly and would depend on the operator's workforce plus periodic LiT support, which is a control and quality risk worth naming.

## How this calibrates LiT's plan

- **Sales-cycle assumption (Strategy / Execution):** treat the multi-year operator qualification cycle as the binding constraint on first revenue, not pilot completion. Month-25 first-license should carry an explicit fragility flag and a named early-adopter-targeting workstream (smaller, recovery-motivated, or distressed-asset operators ahead of the majors).
- **Delivery model (Strategy):** adopt the productized, containerized "in-a-box" pattern Jetti proved; it is the right way to make whole-mine integration credible and to compress post-signature deployment. But scope it honestly: modular throughput limits mean LiT's mobile pitch fits pilot and partial-stream commercial scale, with whole-mine deployment requiring multiple modules or fixed-plant work.
- **Team capacity (Weakness / Execution):** a two-person team running a years-long enterprise sales cycle, a pilot, and on-site deployments concurrently is over-subscribed. Sequence these or budget hires; do not assume one team carries all three at once.
