# CR-6 — Tailings residual grades, recoverable fractions, and fine-particle losses

Commissioned research, Round 00 gate. Priority P2. Merges R-CLAIM-1, R-CLAIM-2.
Question: typical residual critical-mineral grades and economically recoverable fractions in lithium and REE tailings; representative scale of fine/ultrafine target-mineral losses in conventional spodumene and REE flowsheets, to size LiT's recovery headroom.

Disposition per register: published-literature averages are **Commissioned** below. Site-specific grade and tonnage per impoundment is **NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL** and carried as a limit.

## Bottom line for the deliberation

The published literature supports the direction of LiT's pitch (real residual value and real fine-particle losses exist in conventional flowsheets) but does not, by itself, validate any specific recovery-uplift number for any specific feedstock. Tailings grades are highly heterogeneous by site, ore, era, and process. Treat the value-headroom claim as **Assumption (moderate confidence)** at the category level and **Unsupported** at any site-specific level until per-impoundment characterization is retrieved. The recovery-uplift figure itself remains an unvalidated assumption per the sponsor stipulations, not a present capability.

## What the literature establishes (Cited)

### Lithium / spodumene flowsheet losses to tailings
- In conventional spodumene beneficiation, lithium is overwhelmingly hosted in spodumene (one ore: **~93% of total Li hosted in spodumene**), with the remainder in muscovite/mica and chlorite that are **largely unrecoverable under conventional flotation**. That residual mica/chlorite-bound lithium reports to tailings as a structural loss, not an operator error. [Cited — Springer/ScienceDirect spodumene beneficiation studies, 2025]
- Particle-size is the core loss mechanism: maximum spodumene flotation recovery occurs for **coarse particles ~38-45 μm**, while **fines below ~30 μm cause froth instability, raise reagent consumption, and are commonly desliemd/removed before flotation** rather than recovered. Fine and ultrafine spodumene therefore reports to tailings. [Cited — coarse-particle beneficiation overview, PMC12334675, 2025; spodumene flotation reviews]
- One dense-media-separation example: **~8% of lithium lost to DMS tailings** in the 43% of mass rejected at the DMS stage, before flotation even begins. [Cited — MDPI Minerals 11(6):649]
- Read-across: the documented losses concentrate in the fine/ultrafine and mica-hosted fractions. This is exactly the headroom a fines-capable or chemically-different second stage would target, which is consistent with LiT's two-stage thesis in direction. It does not quantify LiT's specific uplift.

### Rare earth element residual value in tailings
- REEs occur in tailings as discrete minerals, adsorbed onto clays, and complexed with organic matter; **abundance is heterogeneous** across source ore, processing method, and weathering. There is no single representative grade. [Cited — ScienceDirect S221334372401248X; MDPI Separations 12(2):52]
- Demonstrated recovery economics from waste are real but small-scale today: three West Virginia pilot facilities each produce **4-5 tons of rare earth oxides per year** from mine drainage/waste; a planned Berkeley Pit (Montana) facility targets **40 tons REO per year** from acid mine water. [Cited — Yale e360, 2024]
- Aggregate-supply framing: Colorado School of Mines research cited that **recovering ~90% of existing mining byproducts could supply almost the entirety of US critical-mineral needs**. This is a category-scale opportunity statement, not a per-site grade. [Cited — Yale e360, 2024]
- Reprocessing economics advantage (qualitative, Cited): reprocessing avoids mining and primary crushing cost since the material is already liberated and surface-stored; carbon footprint is roughly half that of conventional mining and milling. [Cited — Yale e360; ScienceDirect review]

### Scale of the accumulated resource (Cited, category-level)
- Worldwide: **~8,500 active and inactive tailings facilities storing ~217 billion cubic meters** of material. [Cited — Yale e360, 2024]
- US: more than **140,000 abandoned hardrock mine sites on federal lands**, ~22,500 posing serious hazards. [Cited — WRI / EPA, see r00-tailings-liability.md]
- Named reprocessors confirm a live market: Phoenix Tailings (building a pilot to process **3 million tons of iron-mine tailings**), Envirogold Global (planning a first **500-ton facility** in Arizona), DOE NETL coal-fly-ash pilot near Gillette, WY. [Cited — Yale e360, 2024]

## How this calibrates LiT's claims

- "Decades of accumulated value" in tailings — **supported in direction, category-level (Assumption, moderate).** The global 217 billion m3 inventory and the 140,000 US sites are real. Whether any target impoundment carries economic residual grade for LiT's minerals is site-specific and unproven here.
- "16-19% recovery" (the dossier's recovery-uplift claim) — **Unsupported by this research as a validated number.** The literature confirms that fine/ultrafine and mica-hosted fractions are lost in conventional flowsheets, so non-trivial headroom exists in principle, but no published average pins LiT's uplift for any feedstock. Remains an unvalidated assumption; validation is the stated use of seed funds, not a present capability.
- Fine-particle loss is a genuine, well-documented gap in conventional spodumene flotation, which strengthens the technical premise that a differently-mechanized second stage could capture value others leave behind. This belongs in Strengths/Opportunities in direction, tagged Assumption, with the uplift number explicitly unvalidated.
