# CR-7 — Tailings reprocessing permitting, environmental liability, and product title

Commissioned research, Round 00 gate. Priority P2. From R-WAT-2.
Question: who holds environmental liability, what permits reprocessing triggers, and who owns recovered product when reprocessing existing or abandoned tailings impoundments in the US.

Disposition per register: **Commission** at the framework level (CERCLA, the General Mining Law of 1872, the 2024 Good Samaritan reform). Done below.

## Bottom line for the deliberation

The abandoned-tailings pitch carries a structural liability and title exposure that LiT's documents underweight. Under CERCLA, an entity that disturbs a contaminated site can be pulled in as a potentially responsible party for the whole site, even if it did not create the contamination and is there to clean it up. On federal land, the United States retains title to unpatented mining claims, so tailings sitting on those claims are not unowned, and recovered-product ownership is not automatic. A 2024 federal reform (the Good Samaritan Act) creates a narrow, capped permit pathway that begins to address this, but it is a 15-permit, seven-year pilot with eligibility rules that may exclude a profit-seeking reprocessor. This should land in Threats and the Execution risk register, and it argues for a partner-with-the-operator licensing model over LiT itself operating at orphaned sites.

## What the framework establishes (Cited)

### CERCLA liability is the central obstacle
- Under CERCLA, **a party that disturbs a contaminated site can become a responsible party for all contamination at that location**, even if it did not cause the pollution and is attempting to improve conditions. Potential remediation cost can exceed recovery revenue, which has a documented chilling effect on private legacy-site projects. [Cited — EPA/ITRC and Good Samaritan program materials, 2024-2026]
- Liability follows ownership, including bare legal title. The Tenth Circuit held the **United States liable as a CERCLA "owner"** for releases on unpatented mining claims because it held legal title, rejecting the argument that bare title is insufficient: "the term owner covers fee title holders for purposes of CERCLA liability, irrespective of any additional indicia of ownership." [Cited — Jackson Kelly, US liable under CERCLA for releases on unpatented mining claims, 10th Cir.]

### Title and ownership of the tailings themselves
- Under the **General Mining Law of 1872**, unpatented claims let private operators mine on federal land while the **US retains legal title to the land**. Tailings deposited on unpatented lands sit on land the government never relinquished. [Cited — Jackson Kelly; Colorado Lawyer]
- Consequence for LiT: at an orphaned/abandoned site on federal land, **who owns the recovered product is not automatic**. It can turn on land title, claim status, any surviving operator/successor interest, and the permit terms. Some sites have unclear title or disputes over whether reprocessing aged material is even appropriate. [Cited — EPA Abandoned Mine Lands; ITRC reuse-of-mining-waste case studies]

### Permitting and the 2024 Good Samaritan reform
- The **Good Samaritan Remediation of Abandoned Hardrock Mines Act** was signed into law **December 17, 2024**. It directs EPA to run a **pilot program issuing up to 15 permits** that let qualifying "Good Samaritans" remediate mine waste **without enforcement or liability** under specified environmental laws. **The program terminates after seven years.** [Cited — Congress.gov S.2781; EPA Good Samaritan program]
- A "Good Samaritan" is defined as a party that **(1) is not a past or current owner/operator of the site, (2) had no role in creating the waste, and (3) is not otherwise potentially liable** for remediation of the mine waste. [Cited — EPA; Womble Bond Dickinson]
- Permits require demonstrated **financial resources or third-party financial assurance** to complete the project; March 2026 federal guidance addresses financial assurance. [Cited — Federal Register 2026-04386, Mar 5 2026]
- The reform explicitly contemplates **reclaiming valuable minerals during remediation** to help meet critical-mineral demand, and EPA's companion EMRTAI initiative assesses critical-mineral recovery from mine waste during cleanup. So the policy door for recover-during-remediation is opening, but narrowly. [Cited — WRI; EPA EMRTAI]
- Scale of the underlying opportunity: **>140,000 abandoned hardrock mine sites on federal lands; ~22,500 pose serious hazards.** [Cited — WRI/EPA]

## How this calibrates LiT's strategy

- **A profit-seeking reprocessor may not cleanly qualify as a "Good Samaritan."** The statutory definition centers on remediation by a non-liable, non-owner party; a business whose primary purpose is mineral revenue, and which by reprocessing disturbs the waste, sits in tension with both the eligibility test and the CERCLA-disturbance trap. Treat "LiT operates at abandoned sites under Good Samaritan cover" as **Assumption (low-to-moderate confidence), legally untested for a commercial reprocessor.**
- **Strategic implication (direction, for Round 03+):** the liability framework favors LiT **licensing its process to the operator/owner that already holds the liability and the title**, rather than LiT taking possession of orphaned tailings itself. This aligns with the whole-mine licensing model and avoids LiT becoming a CERCLA PRP. Operating-at-orphaned-sites is the higher-risk path and should carry an explicit liability gate.
- This belongs in **Threats** (CERCLA PRP exposure; uncertain product title at abandoned sites; narrow/capped permit pathway) and in the **Execution risk register** (liability gate before any LiT-operated site; counsel review of Good Samaritan eligibility; product-title confirmation as a deal precondition).
