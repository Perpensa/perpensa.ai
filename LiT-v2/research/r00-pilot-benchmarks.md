# CR-9 — Bench-to-pilot cost and timeline for a multi-feedstock two-stage flowsheet

Commissioned research, Round 00 gate. Priority P3. From R-CRU-2.
Question: realistic cost and timeline to take a two-stage mineral-processing flowsheet from bench to pilot across multiple feedstocks, to test the $700,000 pilot budget and the Month-25 first-license assumption.

Disposition per register: **Commission** published benchmarks for pilot-scale mineral-processing programs. Done below. This is a use-of-funds and milestone pressure-test, not a spine claim.

## Bottom line for the deliberation

Published benchmarks put LiT's $700,000 pilot budget at the **low end of a single-flowsheet pilot, and most likely short for a multi-feedstock two-stage flowsheet.** Industry sources price bench-scale testwork at roughly $300,000-$400,000 and pilot-scale testwork at $250,000-$1,000,000 per program, and a recent real-world proof-of-concept pilot plant sized at 300-500 kg/hr cost about $2.0M for the hardware alone. LiT's plan compresses both stages and several feedstocks into a number that one ordinary single-feed pilot can consume on its own. The timeline is the bigger exposure: each feedstock typically needs its own bench-then-pilot campaign of several quarters, and any process with recirculating streams (LiT's two-stage flowsheet has them by construction) must run **continuous piloting to prove the circuit reaches and holds equilibrium**, which single-batch tests cannot demonstrate. The Month-25 first-license milestone implicitly assumes one clean pilot cycle on one feedstock; the multi-feedstock premise multiplies that. This belongs in Execution as a budget and schedule fragility note, and supports the team-capacity Weakness.

## What the benchmarks establish (Cited / Assumption)

### Cost
- **Bench-scale testwork: ~$300,000-$400,000; pilot-scale testwork: ~$250,000-$1,000,000** per program, per industry processing-cost guidance. [Cited — Fewstern processing-plant guide synthesis; consistent with AusIMM testwork-scheduling literature]
- **A 300-500 kg/hr proof-of-concept pilot plant cost ~$2.0M** (hardware/acquisition) in a recent 2025-2026 critical-minerals example (Critical Metals Corp, Tanbreez). This is a useful order-of-magnitude anchor for a physical pilot rig versus a testwork campaign run at a contract lab. [Cited — StockTitan/Critical Metals Corp news]
- Pilot-plant quotes are **budget estimates, not fixed prices**: metallurgical testwork carries too many unknowns to commit to lump sums, and every pilot is scoped individually. A multi-feedstock program is therefore best treated as several stacked estimates, not one. [Cited — ALS Global; 911 Metallurgist; SRK pilot-testwork guidance]
- **Assumption (high confidence):** $700,000 covers at most one feedstock through a modest pilot, leaving little for a second or third feed, for continuous (versus batch) operation, or for the chemical-stage pilot that LiT's two-stage flowsheet also requires. The budget reads as single-campaign money against a multi-campaign plan.

### Timeline and the continuous-piloting requirement
- Pilot-scale testing runs the plant at **20-50% of design capacity** on representative ore to validate the flowsheet, recovery, concentrate grade, and utilities before scale-up. Recent projects report pilot commissioning **several quarters** after metallurgical testwork completes (one example: testwork through to Q2 2026 pilot commissioning). [Cited — Fewstern/911 Metallurgist; Critical Metals Corp]
- Most projects "will eventually need to undergo **continuous piloting** to ascertain the effect of recirculating streams and prove the process can reach and sustain continuous equilibrium." A two-stage flowsheet inherently has inter-stage recycle, so batch bench results do not retire the scale-up risk; a continuous campaign is the real gate. [Cited — 911 Metallurgist, pilot plants for process engineering]
- **Inadequate metallurgical testwork is a leading documented cause of mining project schedule and cost overruns.** Under-scoping the pilot to fit a budget is the specific failure this literature warns against. [Cited — AusIMM testwork guidelines]
- **Assumption (moderate confidence):** a credible multi-feedstock program is bench-then-pilot per feed, each several quarters, partly parallelizable but not collapsible to one cycle. Month-25 first-license is achievable only if LiT pilots **one** lead feedstock first and sells off that single proof, treating "multi-feedstock" as a later-phase claim rather than a Phase-1 deliverable.

## How this calibrates LiT's plan

- **Use of funds (Execution):** size the pilot line to one lead feedstock and one continuous campaign, and state plainly that additional feedstocks and the chemical-stage pilot are separately funded later phases. Do not present $700,000 as covering the full multi-feedstock, two-stage proof.
- **Milestone schedule (Execution / Strategy):** keep Month-25 first-license only if it is explicitly tied to a single-feedstock physical-stage pilot. If the first license is meant to rest on multi-feedstock validation, the date is optimistic and should carry a fragility flag.
- **Team capacity (Weakness):** running even one continuous pilot campaign while also selling and managing R&D strains a two-person team; this reinforces the team-capacity Weakness rather than introducing a new one.

## NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL (carried to evidence lock)

- LiT's own pilot scope and vendor quotes (contract-lab versus owned rig, batch versus continuous, number of feedstocks in Phase 1). Not publicly available; the $700,000 line and Month-25 date are **Assumption** until LiT produces the scoped program.
