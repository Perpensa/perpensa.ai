# CR-8 — Export-control and tech-transfer exposure of India-based critical-minerals R&D

Commissioned research, Round 00 gate. Priority P2. From R-WAT-3.
Question: export-control and tech-transfer considerations for developing US-origin critical-minerals process IP through an Indian research institution (IIT (ISM) Dhanbad), given the ITAR/EAR clauses in the VT agreement and the 75% India R&D-spend assumption.

Disposition per register: **Commission** the regulatory framework (EAR/ITAR applicability to mineral-processing know-how, deemed-export rules). Application to LiT's specific facts is **NEEDS EXTERNAL RETRIEVAL: export-control counsel review.** Framework done below.

## Bottom line for the deliberation

Export control is a real but second-order compliance vector for LiT, not a deal-breaker, and the documents do not analyze it. Three findings shape it. First, mineral-processing chemistry and flotation know-how is almost certainly **EAR99** (subject to the EAR but not listed on the Commerce Control List and not ITAR munitions), so for most destinations no license is required and the regime is light. Second, **India is a favored destination**: it holds Strategic Trade Authorization Tier 1 (STA-1) status and is a Major Defense Partner, so even controlled dual-use items face a streamlined path. Third, and most important for LiT, the real exposure is **self-inflicted by the IP strategy, not by the destination**: the moment LiT treats the IIT (ISM) Dhanbad work as proprietary (which it must, to claim defensible IP), it forfeits the Fundamental Research Exclusion and the work becomes "subject to the EAR," activating deemed-export and recordkeeping obligations and the ITAR/EAR flow-down clause in the VT agreement. The compliance burden is manageable, but it is an owned obligation, and it sits in tension with the company's own IP-defensibility story. This belongs in Threats (lightly) and in the Execution risk register as a named, low-cost compliance workstream.

## What the framework establishes (Cited)

### The regime: EAR vs ITAR, and where mineral processing sits
- **ITAR** (22 CFR 120-130, State/DDTC) governs munitions on the US Munitions List. **EAR** (15 CFR 730-799, Commerce/BIS) governs commercial and dual-use items. Mineral-processing chemistry, flotation, and hydrometallurgical know-how are not munitions, so **ITAR does not apply** to LiT's core process IP. [Cited — BIS/Trade.gov; secureframe EAR vs ITAR guide]
- Under the EAR, an item not specifically listed on the Commerce Control List defaults to **EAR99**. EAR99 items are subject to the EAR but **generally require no license** except to embargoed destinations, denied parties, or for prohibited end-uses. Lithium-from-spodumene and tailings-recovery process know-how, absent a specific CCL entry, is best treated as **EAR99 (Assumption, high confidence pending a formal classification)**. [Cited — BIS EAR; Pitt/Microsoft EAR overviews]

### Deemed exports: the mechanism that reaches foreign researchers
- A **"deemed export"** is the release of controlled technology or source code to a foreign national, treated as an export to that person's country of citizenship/permanent residence. Release can occur by demonstration, oral briefing, site visit, or transmission of non-public data. The license test is the same as for a physical export to that country. [Cited — BIS deemed-export FAQs; UCSB/Stanford research-compliance guidance]
- Deemed-export rules bite for **controlled (CCL-listed) technology**. For **EAR99** technology, no deemed-export license is generally required regardless of the foreign national's nationality, which is the likely posture for LiT's process know-how. [Cited — BIS; LBNL export-control training]

### The Fundamental Research Exclusion and why it does NOT save LiT
- Technology that arises during or results from **fundamental research** and is intended to be published is **not subject to the EAR** (15 CFR 734.8). Fundamental research is research whose results "ordinarily are published and shared broadly," and **for which the researchers have accepted no restrictions for proprietary or national-security reasons.** [Cited — eCFR/Cornell 15 CFR 734.8; University of Minnesota and BIS guidance]
- Critical limitation, directly on point for LiT: **once a party decides to keep the technology proprietary or restrict publication, the FRE is lost and the technology becomes subject to the EAR.** A company sponsoring research and asserting ownership of the results, with publication-approval or confidentiality terms, **cannot rely on the FRE.** [Cited — 15 CFR 734.8; BIS; OSU/UCCS research-security guidance]
- Consequence: the VT-style university work might qualify as fundamental research while it is genuinely open, but LiT's commercial premise is **proprietary, defensible IP**. To the extent LiT directs and owns the IIT (ISM) Dhanbad output under confidentiality and assignment terms, that work is **outside the FRE and inside the EAR.** This is the crux of the vector.

### The VT agreement's flow-down obligation
- VT Sponsored Research Agreement **Section 17 (Export and Sanctions Compliance)** binds both parties to ITAR and EAR, defines export-controlled "Items," acknowledges VT faculty/students **may be foreign persons (22 CFR 120.16; 15 CFR 772)**, and **requires written notice to VT's Office of Export and Secure Research Compliance (oesrc@vt.edu) before any exchange of export-controlled Items**, with an export-classification description. VT may decline or limit receipt of such Items, and transfers "may require a license." Section 17 also flows down **sanctions/denied-party screening** (SDN, Entity List, Denied Persons, Unverified, Debarred lists). [Cited — LiT_VT_Sponsored_Research_Agreement_2024.md Sec. 17]
- So LiT already carries a contractual, named compliance touchpoint at VT. Any controlled Item it provides to or receives from VT triggers notice, classification, and possible VT refusal. This is an operational dependency, not just a legal abstraction.

### India is a favorable, not adverse, destination
- The US elevated **India to Strategic Trade Authorization Tier 1 (STA-1)** in 2018, granting license-exception access to a broad range of dual-use technologies controlled by Commerce, and India is a designated **Major Defense Partner**. The **US-India Strategic Trade Dialogue (2023)** continues to ease bilateral export-control friction. [Cited — Hughes Hubbard STA-1 alert; Carnegie Endowment US-India export-controls analysis; State Dept]
- Net: even if some LiT item were CCL-controlled, India's status materially lowers the licensing burden versus a non-favored destination. The destination is **not** the risk; the IP-classification and the proprietary-work posture are. [Assessment grounded in the above Cited sources]

## How this calibrates LiT's strategy

- **Threats (light entry):** performing proprietary, company-owned process R&D abroad forfeits the Fundamental Research Exclusion and pulls the work under the EAR, creating deemed-export, recordkeeping, technology-control, and VT-notice obligations. Mischaracterizing the work as "fundamental research" to avoid this would simultaneously undercut the IP-defensibility claim. The exposure is **compliance cost and process discipline, not likely license denial**, given EAR99 classification and India's favored status.
- **Tension to name for leadership:** the **75% India R&D-spend** plan and the **proprietary-IP** thesis pull against each other under export law. The more genuinely proprietary and restricted the work (good for IP), the more squarely it sits under the EAR (compliance load). The more open and publishable (cheap, FRE-covered), the weaker the IP moat. This is a coherence issue between CR-1 (IP/FTO) and CR-8, and should be flagged where the two meet.
- **Execution risk register (owned mitigation, low cost):**
  1. Obtain a **formal EAR classification (likely EAR99)** of the core process technology from export-control counsel; do not assume.
  2. Stand up a lightweight **Technology Control Plan** governing what is shared with IIT (ISM) Dhanbad personnel and under what confidentiality/assignment terms, with deemed-export screening of any non-US persons touching controlled data.
  3. Honor the **VT Section 17 notice-and-classification step** before exchanging any Item with VT; treat VT's right to decline as a schedule risk.
  4. Run **denied-party/sanctions screening** (SDN, Entity List, etc.) on all India and other foreign counterparties, per VT Sec. 17 and standard EAR practice.
- These are inexpensive, standard startup compliance items. The strategic point is that they must be **owned and budgeted**, because the documents currently assume them away.
